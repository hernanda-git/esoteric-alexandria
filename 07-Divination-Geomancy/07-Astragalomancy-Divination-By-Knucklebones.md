# Astragalomancy — Divination by Knucklebones

## The Object

**Astragalomancy** is divination by **knucklebones**: the **astragaloi** in Greek, the **tali** in the Roman world, and the generic **tesserae** when broader dice are used. The operative bone is the **astragalus** of sheep, goats, or deer — a roughly rectangular piece with four irregular faces and one rounded end, so it never rests stably on the fifth side.

## The Values and Combinations

The classical Greek assignment is well attested in Pollux (*Onomasticon* 9.94–99) and in archaeological finds from sanctuaries such as Olympia and Delphi. The four faces carry values **1, 3, 4, 6**.

Throwing five astragaloi yields fourteen recognized combinations. Five of these are named:

- **Venus throw**: 1-3-4-6-x with all four visible sides different; all higher and all lower sides different — the rarest and most favorable.
- **Sow** or **dog**: low total, generally unfavorable.
- **Throw of the three**: 1-3-4 across three bones.
- **Throw of the four**: 1-3-4-6 on four bones.
- **Hercules / throw of the fifty**: high total, forceful outcome.

The numbered results were not simply “yes/no.” They triggered **prescribed responses**: temple consultations, ritual offerings, warnings for migration or agriculture, marriage decisions, or auspiciating the start of a war.

## Ritual and Context

The practice was not casual in classical culture. It was bound to:

1. **Sanctuary hands** — at Delphi, Dodona, and Amphiaraus the priesthood managed knucklebones as part of the formal consultative mechanism.
2. **Military choice** — Roman generals cast the tali in the Campus Martius before marching season.
3. **Social play with divinatory residue** — knucklebones were also children’s toys and tavern gambling pieces; the divinatory reading reuses a familiar object and transfers its social valence.
4. **Festival calendar** — in the Greek *tropaia* and processions, throws could decide routing, priestly schedules, or city quotas for Panhellenic attendance.

## Technical Divination by Configuration

Some attested techniques go beyond totals:

- **Pairwise reading**: read each side separately before summing, looking for a paired Mars-Jupiter or Venus-Mercury character.
- **Position with respect to the thrower**: left-side up versus right-side up could invert the valence.
- **Direction of throw**: throws toward the east or toward a specific altar carried different meanings.
- **Ritual purity of bone set**: bones from specific sanctuaries were treated as more authoritative than generic bones.

## Relationship to Other Sortilege

Astragalomancy is a **cleromantic** method: it relies on randomized lots. Its parent class is the same as **sortilege** in the medieval Mediterranean and **ikakes** in Near Eastern traditions. The key difference is object authority. Not any random tokens qualify: the bone must come from the canonical animal, must have four marked faces, and must be *taken seriously enough that the community accepts its fall as authoritative*.

That sociological condition is what separates divination from gambling. The same bone set used on a tavern table is leisure; the same bone set thrown at Delphi under liturgical supervision is **cleromancy**.

## Modern Analogue

If modern interpretation seeks a structural analogue, the closest is **Bayesian randomization**: the practitioner allows a physical randomizer to sample from a pre-committed outcome space, then interprets the symbol against a fixed interpretive schema. The sacred addition is the **social seal**: whoever can enforce interpretive authority defines the meaning. In a priest-run sanctuary, that is the priesthood; in an amateur reconstructionist setting, it is the practitioner’s own schema.
