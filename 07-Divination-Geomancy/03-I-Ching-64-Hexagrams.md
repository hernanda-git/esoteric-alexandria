# The I Ching — 64 Hexagrams

## The Book

The **I Ching** (易經, *Yijing*, "Book of Changes") is the oldest of the Chinese classical texts, the foundation of the Confucian canon, and the most widely consulted divination text in the modern world. The text has three layers:

1. **The Zhouyi** (周易) — the original 64 hexagrams with their judgments, attributed to King Wen of Zhou (~1100 BCE) and his son the Duke of Zhou
2. **The Ten Wings** (十翼) — the commentary tradition attributed to Confucius and his school (~500 BCE)
3. **The 64 hexagrams with line texts** — the layered text, the result of the editorial process

The 64 hexagrams represent the 64 possible states of the cosmos, derived from 8 **trigrams** (the **bagua**, 八卦) combined in all 2³ × 2³ = 64 ways.

## The 8 Trigrams

The 8 trigrams are the **building blocks**. Each trigram is 3 lines, each line either solid (yang ☰) or broken (yin ☷). The 8:

| # | Trigram | Name | Image | Character | Family |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | ☰ | **Qian** (乾) | Heaven | The Creative, the strong, the father | Father |
| 2 | ☷ | **Kun** (坤) | Earth | The Receptive, the yielding, the mother | Mother |
| 3 | ☵ | **Kan** (坎) | Water | The Abysmal, the dangerous, the deep | Middle son |
| 4 | ☲ | **Li** (離) | Fire | The Clinging, the bright, the dependent | Middle daughter |
| 5 | ☳ | **Zhen** (震) | Thunder | The Arousing, the shocking, the awakening | Eldest son |
| 6 | ☴ | **Xun** (巽) | Wind | The Gentle, the penetrating, the flexible | Eldest daughter |
| 7 | ☶ | **Gen** (艮) | Mountain | The Keeping Still, the still, the resting | Youngest son |
| 8 | ☱ | **Dui** (兌) | Lake | The Joyous, the joyful, the productive | Youngest daughter |

The 8 trigrams are the **atomic units** of the I Ching system. The 64 hexagrams are the **molecular combinations**.

## The 64 Hexagrams

Each hexagram is 2 trigrams stacked. The 64 hexagrams have traditional **names** (e.g., Hexagram 1 is *Qian* the Creative, Hexagram 2 is *Kun* the Receptive, Hexagram 3 is *Zhun* Difficulty at the Beginning, Hexagram 11 is *Tai* Peace, Hexagram 64 is *Wei Ji* Before Completion) and **judgments** (the core text, 3-5 lines of classical Chinese).

A few of the most important:

- **Hex. 1, Qian** — The Creative. Pure yang. The dragon. The impulse to begin. "The superior person is constantly self-improving."
- **Hex. 2, Kun** — The Receptive. Pure yin. The mare. The capacity to receive. "The superior person supports all beings with their great virtue."
- **Hex. 11, Tai** — Peace. Heaven and Earth in communication. "The small departs, the great approaches. Success."
- **Hex. 12, Pi** — Standstill. Heaven and Earth out of communication. "The small approaches, the great departs. Not success."
- **Hex. 29, Kan** — The Abysmal. Water repeated. "The superior person is always increasing their virtue."
- **Hex. 30, Li** — The Clinging. Fire repeated. "The superior person is clear and bright, sustaining all beings with their brilliance."
- **Hex. 49, Ge** — Revolution. The time of change. "The superior person reforms the calendar."
- **Hex. 50, Ding** — The Cauldron. The container. "The superior person stabilizes the people with their great virtue."
- **Hex. 64, Wei Ji** — Before Completion. The last moment. "The superior person is careful about the end."

The 64 hexagrams form a **circular pattern**. Hexagram 1 (Qian, pure yang) flows into Hexagram 2 (Kun, pure yin) flows into Hexagram 3 (Zhun, the first movement) ... through to Hexagram 64 (Wei Ji, before completion), which then **returns** to Hexagram 1. The cycle is **eternal**.

## The Consultation

A consultation of the I Ching has 7 steps:

1. **Hold the question in mind** — the question should be specific, important, and answerable
2. **Generate the hexagram** — traditionally by **yarrow stalks** (50 stalks, manipulated in a complex procedure, ~20 minutes); in modern practice by **3 coins** tossed 6 times (~3 minutes)
3. **Record the lines** — each line is either old yang (9), old yin (6), young yang (7), or young yin (8)
4. **Compute the primary hexagram** — the 6 lines in order from bottom to top
5. **Compute the changing lines** — old yang and old yin are the lines that change
6. **Compute the secondary hexagram** — when the changing lines flip, you get a new hexagram
7. **Read the judgment and the line texts** — the judgment is the **theme**, the line texts are the **specific** guidance for each changing line

The reading is a **multi-layered interpretation** of the primary hexagram (the current situation), the changing lines (the movement), and the secondary hexagram (the outcome).

## The Two Reading Modes

The I Ching can be read in two modes:

### 1. The Confucian Mode (the moral reading)

- **Source**: Confucius and the Ten Wings
- **Reading**: the hexagram describes the **ethical situation** of the querent; the line texts give the **specific advice** for moving through the situation with integrity
- **Use**: guidance on how to act correctly in a moral situation
- **Example**: Hex. 29, Kan (The Abysmal), with changing line 5: "The abyss is not full. The superior person fills their heart with the central virtue." The reading: you are in a difficult situation; maintain your integrity; you will emerge intact if you do not compromise.

### 2. The Taoist Mode (the natural reading)

- **Source**: the original Zhouyi, the natural reading of the natural signs
- **Reading**: the hexagram describes the **energy** of the moment; the line texts describe how the energy **flows**
- **Use**: guidance on how to act in **accordance with the moment**, not in opposition to it
- **Example**: Hex. 29, Kan, with changing line 5: the energy is water, the deep, the dangerous; the line is a warning; the querent should not act precipitously but should wait for the water to settle.

The two modes are not exclusive. The Wilhelm/Baynes translation (1950) presents both modes in a single book, and the reader is free to read in either.

## The Modern Note

The I Ching is **the most influential** of the Chinese texts in the modern West. The Wilhelm/Baynes translation is in print in every Western country. The I Ching has been used by:

- **Carl Jung** — Jung's "synchronicity" essay (1952) used the I Ching as a primary example of meaningful coincidence
- **The decision-making community** — the I Ching is consulted for business, personal, and political decisions
- **The psychotherapy community** — Jungian analysts use the I Ching with patients as a projective technique
- **The technology community** — the I Ching is one of the foundations of the **Wilhelm/Baynes** tradition that influenced **Heinz von Foerster**, **Heinz Pagels**, and other modern complexity theorists
- **The Taoist religion** — the I Ching is the foundation text of the Taoist religious practice

The library's category 07 treats the I Ching as the **Western world's most used** divination system, after the Tarot. The two together are the modern Western divination default.

## The Practice

A modern I Ching practice requires:

1. **Read the Wilhelm/Baynes** (or another standard translation) — the book itself is the introduction
2. **Generate the hexagram** — coin or yarrow
3. **Read the judgment** — the theme of the moment
4. **Read the changing lines** — the specific movement
5. **Read the secondary hexagram** — the outcome
6. **Sit with the reading** — do not act immediately; the reading will work on the unconscious
7. **Verify** — track which readings came true, which did not

The library's category 07 is the **textual foundation**. The practice requires only the book, the coins, and the patience. No chain is required (the I Ching is one of the few traditions that **is** available in book form alone, though a teacher is always helpful).
