# Jinn — The Islamic Spirit-Lore

## The Source

The **jinn** (الجنّ, singular *jinni* or *jinn*) are the **non-human, non-angel intelligent beings** of the Islamic tradition. The Quranic account in **Sura 72 (al-Jinn)** and the **Hadith literature** is the canonical source. The pre-Islamic Arabian tradition is older and contributes the **naming systems** and the **tribal structures** of the jinn.

The jinn are one of three categories of rational being in Islamic cosmology:

1. **Mala'ika** (ملائكة) — the angels, created from light, no free will, perpetually in service of God
2. **Bashar** (بشر) — the humans, created from clay, free will, the vicegerents of God on earth
3. **Jinn** (جنّ) — created from **smokeless fire** (Quran 55:15), free will, parallel to humans in many ways

The jinn are the **closest** to humans in nature (free will, moral responsibility, marriage, offspring, death) and the **closest** in danger. The Quranic jinn have prophets, scriptures, and a Day of Judgment just as humans do. The jinn can be **Muslim**, **Christian**, **Jewish**, or **kafir** (unbelieving).

## The Five Tribes of Jinn

The classical Arabic tradition gives **five tribes** of jinn, each with different characteristics:

| Tribe | Element | Color | Power | Nature |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Jann** | Earth | Earth-brown | Concealment, the night | The original jinn, the most common |
| **Jinn** (also *jin*) | Air | Wind-gray | Movement, the breath | The second tribe, the most varied |
| **Shayatin** (شَيَاطِين) | Fire | Red | Temptation, evil | The "demons" — Satan's army |
| **Ifrit** (عفريت) | Fire (most powerful) | Black | Strength, cunning | The powerful jinn, the "kings" |
| **Marid** (مارد) | Water / Smoke | Green / White | The most powerful, the rebellious | The "old jinn", the most difficult to bind |

The pre-Islamic tradition (the **Arabian Nights**, the *Kitab al-Tabak*) names the most powerful jinn by name. The most famous:

- **Iblis** (إبليس) — the original Shaytan (Satan), the "despairer" — a jinn who was elevated to the angelic realm and then refused to bow to Adam
- **Al-'Uzzab** — a jinn that causes nightmares (the *'aqd* — the knot that tightens in the chest)
- **Midad** — a jinn that calls to the traveler in the desert
- **Hannash** — the chief of the jinn of the mountains
- **Dabaran** — the chief of the jinn of the desert

The 72 jinn parallel the 72 Goetic spirits (see [[02-Goetic-Spirits-72-Shemhamphorash]]), and the 9 most powerful jinn parallel the 9 choirs of the angelic hierarchy (see [[01-Angelic-Hierarchy]]).

## The Creation

The Quran states that the jinn were created from **"smokeless fire"** (Quran 55:15) — the fire of the spirit, the fire of the breath, the fire of the will. Some commentators (al-Tabari, al-Qurtubi) say "smokeless" means a fire without the impurity of matter; the jinn are a **pure spirit-form** of fire, with enough body to act in the world.

The jinn were created **before** the humans. In some traditions, the jinn were the **first inhabitants** of the earth, and they were "corrupted" — they shed blood, broke the divine law, and were replaced by the angels. In other traditions, the jinn and the humans are **co-inhabitants**, both with the same purpose of worshipping God.

The famous pre-Islamic story: the jinn heard the Prophet Solomon's prayer at the Temple, and many of them **accepted Islam** voluntarily. The Quran (Sura 27, al-Naml) describes the jinn working in Solomon's construction of the Temple. The Quran (Sura 34, Saba) says Solomon's throne was carried by an **ifrit jinn** in the blink of an eye.

## The Jinn in Modern Folklore

The modern Arab, Turkish, Persian, and South Asian folklore is **rich** in jinn lore. The most common themes:

- **The jinn in the bottle** — a powerful jinn imprisoned in a bottle, released by the finder (the *Arabian Nights* motif)
- **The jinn of the ruin** (*'aqar*) — a haunted building, a desert ruin, an abandoned house where a jinn lives
- **The jinn of the tree** — a tree (often a thorn tree) where a jinn resides
- **The jinn of the sea** — the jinn of the ocean, the most powerful, the most dangerous
- **The jinn of the night** — the jinn that come out at night, the cause of nightmares, the cause of sudden illness
- **The jinn lover** — a jinn that falls in love with a human, takes human form, marries them
- **The jinn who teaches** — a jinn that gives esoteric knowledge (this is the most common in the *'ilm al-huruf* tradition; al-Buni reports being taught by a jinn named **Shamhurish**)

The library's category 06 is not a folklore collection. The jinn lore is documented for:

1. **The cross-tradition concordance** — jinn ↔ demons ↔ spirits in all traditions
2. **The theological structure** — the jinn occupy a specific place in the Islamic cosmology
3. **The 'ilm al-huruf connection** — the jinn are the **source** of much of the *'ilm al-huruf* knowledge in the al-Buni tradition

## The Jinn and the Practitioner

In the *'ilm al-huruf* tradition (see [[02-Hurufiyya-Lettrism/01-Ilm-al-Huruf-Overview]]), the jinn are not just entities to be feared — they are **collaborators** in the work. Al-Buni reports:

- Receiving **teachings from jinn** in dreams and visions
- **Binding jinn** to serve in specific operations
- **Invoking jinn** for specific purposes (the same as the Goetic tradition)
- The **shayateen** (demons) are the most useful and the most dangerous — they know the hidden names and the hidden properties of things

The practice of binding jinn is called ***'azima*** (عزمة, "the binding"). The *'azima* is the Islamic equivalent of the Western **conjuration**. The practitioner:

1. **Identifies the jinn** (by name, by tribe, by element)
2. **Constructs a binding** (a talisman, a sigil, a verbal formula)
3. **Invokes the jinn** at the right time (the planetary hour, the day of the jinn's ruler)
4. **Commands the jinn** to do the work
5. **Releases the jinn** properly (with a dismissal, a thanks, a protection)

The *'azima* is **identical in structure** to the Western conjuration. The traditions are the same practice with different vocabularies.

## The Jinn in Modern Exorcism

The **exorcism of jinn** (*ruqya shar'iyya*) is a living practice in the modern Muslim world. The practitioner (*raqi*) recites the Quran, blows on water, and gives the water to the afflicted. The most commonly recited verses:

- Sura 1 (al-Fatiha) — the opening
- Sura 2:255 (Ayat al-Kursi) — the throne verse
- Sura 112 (al-Ikhlas) — the unity
- Sura 113 (al-Falaq) — the daybreak (against evil)
- Sura 114 (al-Nas) — the people (against the whisperer)

The practice is **widespread** in the modern Muslim world, especially in Egypt, the Gulf states, and Indonesia. The official Islamic scholarly position varies: some scholars (the Salafis) reject the practice entirely, others accept it as legitimate, others accept it but with restrictions on the practitioner.

The library's category 06 documents the practice academically. The practice is for **qualified scholars and healers**, not for casual reading.

## The Modern Note

The jinn are **alive** in modern Islamic culture in a way that the Western demons are not. Modern Muslim-majority countries have **ruqya** healers, **jinn**-related TV shows, **jinn**-related novels, **jinn**-related movies. The "**Jinn Hunter**" genre is a real genre of modern Arabic pop culture.

The library's category 06 treats the jinn as:

1. **Theological** — the jinn are a category of being in the Islamic cosmology
2. **Cultural** — the jinn are alive in modern Muslim culture
3. **Magical** — the jinn are collaborators in the *'ilm al-huruf* tradition
4. **Psychological** — the jinn are the **shadow** of the human, the unintegrated aspects of the personality

The four levels are not exclusive. A modern Muslim scholar of the jinn might work at all four levels.

## The Practice

A modern practice of jinn work requires:

1. **The foundation** — a strong grounding in the Quran, the Hadith, the *sira* (biography of the Prophet)
2. **The lineage** — a recognized teacher in a *silsila* (see [[01-Origins-Traditions/07-Master-Disciple-Chains]])
3. **The protection** — the Quranic verses, the *dhikr* (remembrance), the *salah* (ritual prayer)
4. **The purpose** — the work must be for a *halal* (permissible) purpose, not for curiosity or harm
5. **The exit** — every jinn operation must be properly closed
