# Jafr — Divination by Letters

## Definition

*Jafr* (Arabic: جفر, "divination by letters") is a Shia-derived occult practice that uses letter-number calculations on Arabic texts — usually the Quran, the *sahifa* of Imam Ali, or the hadith — to predict future events or uncover hidden meanings.

The word *jafr* may derive from a tradition attributed to the Prophet: "There is a *jafr* that you will learn, in which there is knowledge of what will be until the Day of Resurrection" (Suyuti, *al-Itqan*).

## The Two Levels

| Level | Arabic | Scope | Difficulty |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lesser Jafr | *al-jafr al-asghar* | Predictions about specific events (dates, people) | Advanced practitioner |
| Greater Jafr | *al-jafr al-akbar* | The complete predictive system, includes the *jafr jami'* (comprehensive cipher) | Reserved for the Imams / Master practitioners |

The Ismailis in particular developed the *jafr jami'* into a complete cosmology — a system for translating any text or event into a numerical address and back.

## The Mechanics

1. **Source text selection** — the practitioner picks a sacred text appropriate to the question
2. **Letter-to-number mapping** — using the abjad system
3. **Operation** — addition, subtraction, multiplication, sometimes geometric manipulation
4. **Interpretation** — the result is mapped to letters or to a calendrical date

Example (simplified, classical style): to know if a journey on a given date will be safe, the practitioner writes the basmala, takes the abjad of the question phrase, and computes a square root. The resulting number maps to a day in the lunar calendar; the planetary ruler of that day is the answer's "color".

## The Controversy

*Jafr* has been controversial within Islam for 1,200 years. The classical ulema of the Sunni mainstream rejected it as *bid'a* (innovation) and as close to *sihr* (sorcery). The Shia ulema have been more divided, with some defending it as a legitimate esoteric practice, others rejecting it as a misattribution to the Imams.

The Ismailis, the Druze, and the Alawites have maintained the most rigorous *jafr* traditions. The Druze practice of *jafr* is documented in D. B. MacDonald's "The Development of the Idea of Ghost in Arabia" (1909) and more recently in works on Druze religion.

## Modern Parallels

*Jafr* is structurally similar to:

- **Cryptanalysis** — given a ciphertext (Quranic text), find a key (abjad mapping) and an algorithm (the operation)
- **Numerical taxonomy** — given an object, map it to a multi-dimensional key
- **Information retrieval** — given a query, compute an address and fetch the document
- **Quantum measurement** — given a system, the answer "collapses" to a specific value when observed

The most productive modern frame is **cryptanalysis**. The classical *jafr* practitioners were medieval codebreakers, working with sacred texts as their "key text".

## A Note on Practice

This library documents *jafr* academically. It does not advocate practice. The methods are recorded for:

1. **Historical interest** — how did medieval Muslim intellectuals think about prediction?
2. **Cognitive science** — what does it mean to "compute on sacred text"?
3. **Bridge to modern categories** — see `15-Modern-Parallels/`

If you are a Muslim practitioner interested in *jafr* as a living tradition, find a qualified teacher in a recognized *silsila*. The tradition is oral by design, and you cannot get it from a book alone.
