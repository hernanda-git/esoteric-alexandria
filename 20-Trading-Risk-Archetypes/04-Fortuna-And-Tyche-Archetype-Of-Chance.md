# Fortuna and Tyche — The Archetype of Chance in Trading

## The Honesty Note

This is **not** a claim that markets are governed by a goddess. It is a **careful comparison** between the **classical personification of chance** (the Roman **Fortuna**, the Greek **Tyche**) and the **modern trader's lived experience of randomness**. The comparison is **legitimate** as a *psychological* and *structural* lens; it is **not** a cosmological claim. The previous draft overclaimed a "goddess of the tape." This draft holds the comparison more carefully: chance is **real**, the personification is **a map**, not the territory.

## What the Comparison Captures

1. **Variance is not skill.** The Stoics personified *Tyche* precisely because outcomes confound desert — the worthy suffer, the unworthy prosper. A trader who attributes a winning streak to skill has made the **same error** the ancients warned against: mistaking a **favorable turn of the wheel** for **earned station**. Taleb's *Fooled by Randomness* is the modern, mathematical version of Boethius' complaint that Fortune "raises the unworthy and casts down the deserving."

2. **The wheel turns.** Fortuna's defining attribute is the **rotating wheel** (*rota Fortunae*): ascent is never secure. The trader's **drawdown** is the wheel's downward arc. The classical lesson — *nil mortalibus arduum est* (nothing is too high for mortals to fear) — maps onto the risk rule that **no edge survives regime change unchanged**.

3. **Humility as technique.** Boethius' Lady Philosophy argues the wise man is **free** precisely because he does not depend on Fortune's gifts. The trading translation: the disciplined trader **neutralizes** Tyche by sizing positions so that a bad turn is **survivable** (see [[02-Position-Sizing-Real-Mathematics]]). Humility before chance is not piety; it is **position sizing**.

## What the Comparison Does Not Capture

1. **No intentional agent.** Fortuna/Tyche are **personifications**, not agents. Markets are **emergent** systems of many agents; "luck" is a name we give to **low-probability tails** we failed to model. Treating chance as a *being* to be propitiated is the **error** — the trader who "appeases the market gods" has confused a **distribution** with a **deity**.

2. **The math is the honest tool.** Where the ancients had only *Tyche*, the trader has **variance**, **expectancy**, **Sharpe**, **max drawdown**. These are **measurable**; the goddess is not. The modern parallel **replaces** the archetype with the statistic wherever possible.

3. **Agency remains.** Fortuna governs *outcome*, not *action*. The trader controls the **process** (entry rules, risk limits, journaling) and **not** the result. This is the Stoic dichotomy of control (*epiktētos*) — the genuinely useful residue of the archetype.

## The Practice

A trader who wants the **discipline** the archetype points at, without the metaphysics:

1. **Log the luck.** After each trade, mark whether the outcome was **deserved by process** or **borrowed from variance**. The honest log separates *Fortuna* from *skill*.
2. **Size for the wheel.** Size so the worst *plausible* turn does not end the game ([[02-Position-Sizing-Real-Mathematics]]).
3. **Expect the turn.** Pre-commit: "this edge will degrade." The wheel's rotation is the only certainty.

## The Critical Note

The archetype is a **mirror**, not a cause. It is useful because it **checks the ego** that a green P&L inflates; it is dangerous if it becomes an **explanation** that excuses poor process. Use *Tyche* to stay humble; use the **math** to stay alive.
