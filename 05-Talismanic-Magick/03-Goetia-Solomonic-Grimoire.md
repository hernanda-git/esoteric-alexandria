# The Goetia — The Lesser Key of Solomon

## The Book

**The Lesser Key of Solomon** (Latin: *Lemegeton Clavicula Salomonis*) is the most influential grimoire of the Western tradition. The current form is a **17th-century manuscript compilation** combining several older sources. The text contains the procedures for conjuring 72 demons (the "Goetia" section) plus 4 other sections covering theurgia, pauline art, almadel, and the keys of the 50 gates of Binah.

The **Goetia** (the first section) is what is usually meant when modern readers refer to "the Lesser Key". It gives 72 spirits, each with:

- A **name** and **title**
- A **rank** (King, Duke, Prince, Marquis, Earl/Count, Knight, President)
- A **legion count** (the number of spirits under their command)
- A **seal / sigil** — a geometric figure the magician draws
- A **description** of the spirit
- A **special power** (teaching arts, finding treasures, causing love, etc.)
- A **constellation** — the spirit appears when a particular planet/constellation is "rising"

The 72 spirits are the **counterparts** of the **72 angels of the Shem ha-Mephorash** (the Kabbalistic 72-fold name of God) — see [[06-Angels-Jinn-Demons/02-Goetic-Spirits-72-Shemhamphorash]]. The system pairs each angel with a demon; invoking the angel is the light, invoking the demon is the shadow. The Western occult tradition since the 16th century has been fascinated with the Goetia and has produced hundreds of editions, commentaries, and practical accounts.

## The Source

The **Testament of Solomon** is the earliest source (1st-5th c. CE), a Greek magical text where King Solomon interrogates and binds demons. The medieval grimoire tradition (the **Sworn Book of Honorius**, the **Book of Abramelin**, the **Grand Grimoire**) all draw on the Testament.

The Solomonic theme is **central** to Western magic. The legend: Solomon received his wisdom from God, built the Temple, and ruled over all the spirits of the air, earth, and water. He bound them to his service by a **seal** (the pentagram) and a **ring** (the pentacle). The grimoires give the procedures for invoking this Solomon-like power and commanding the spirits as he did.

## The Conjuration Procedure

The Goetia gives a detailed procedure for conjuring a spirit. The 7 steps:

1. **Preparation** — 3-9 days of fasting, abstinence, prayer, ritual purification
2. **The Circle** — the magician draws a circle on the floor with the **sword** or **knife**, using the consecrated ink
3. **The Triangle** — outside the circle, the magician draws a **triangle** where the spirit will appear
4. **The Pentacles** — the magician has the **Art** of the appropriate spirit inscribed on a piece of paper, placed at the triangle
5. **The Invocation** — the magician reads the **conjuration** aloud (multiple times, in increasing intensity)
6. **The Appearance** — the spirit appears in the triangle (in the **smoke** of the suffumigation, traditionally, or in the **air** in more dramatic accounts)
7. **The Command** — the magician commands the spirit, asks for their name, their seal, their office, their purpose

The procedure is **dangerous** by design. The Goetia is explicit that an unprepared conjurer will be **possessed**, **deceived**, or **driven mad** by the spirit. The seal of the spirit is what binds the spirit; the magician must **not** step outside the circle, must not break the chain, must not let the spirit touch them.

## The Modern Note

The Goetia is the **most reproduced** grimoire in the modern era. The Mathers-Crowley edition (1904) is the most famous English edition; Joseph Peterson's 2001 critical edition is the most useful for serious students. The **Order of the Golden Dawn** (1888-1903) used the Goetia extensively; **Aleister Crowley** called the Goetia "the most potent engine of practical magic ever devised".

The Goetia has entered popular culture through:

- **The book** itself — modern editions by Penguin, Weiser, Llewellyn, etc.
- **The Lemegeton** — the full 5-part compilation
- **H.P. Lovecraft** — the **Necronomicon** in the Cthulhu Mythos is partly inspired by the Goetia
- **The 72 Angels** — the Goetic spirits are sometimes interpreted as **shadow aspects** of the 72 Kabbalistic angels (the "evil" twin, the "daimon" of the Greek tradition)
- **The practice** — modern ceremonial magicians, chaos magicians, and demonolaters all engage with the Goetia in different ways

## The Practice

A modern Goetic practice requires:

1. **The training** — basic ceremonial skills, ritual purity, knowledge of the planetary hours
2. **The source texts** — the Lemegeton, the Book of Abramelin, the Sworn Book
3. **A teacher or tradition** — the Goetia is **not** for solo practice. The tradition is clear: an unprepared conjurer is in danger.
4. **A purpose** — the Goetia is for **practical operation**, not for entertainment. The spirits are invoked for specific purposes (teaching, treasure-finding, love, protection, etc.)
5. **An exit ritual** — the conjuration must be properly closed. The spirit must be **dismissed**, not banished. The seal must be **closed**, not broken.

The library's category 05 documents the Goetia academically. The **practice** requires a tradition, a teacher, and a community. The library is the **map**, not the territory.

## The Interpretation

The Goetia can be read at three levels:

1. **Literal** — the spirits are real entities, the conjuration works on them, the seal binds them
2. **Psychological** — the 72 spirits are **archetypes** of the unconscious, the conjuration is a meditative practice, the seal is a **mandala** that the practitioner uses to focus
3. **Mystical** — the spirits are **veils** of the divine, the conjuration is a form of prayer, the seal is a **symbol** of the practitioner's authority over their own inner world

Each level is legitimate. The literal level is the most dangerous; the psychological level is the most accessible; the mystical level is the most rewarding.

The library takes no position on which is **correct**. Each level has its own tradition, its own community, its own evidence.
