# Active Imagination — The Dialogue with the Unconscious

## The Claim

**Active imagination** is Jung's term for a **deliberate, waking dialog with the unconscious** — the method by which the images of the inner world are met, not merely observed, and brought into conscious relation. Where dream analysis is **passive** (the dream arrives; you interpret it), active imagination is **active** (you enter the image and let it answer back).

Jung arrived at the method during his own 1913-1916 crisis, recorded in *Liber Novus* (The Red Book). Faced with a flood of inner figures, he chose not to repress them and not to be overwhelmed by them, but to **treat them as real interlocutors**. The practice became the **engine** of the individuation process (see [[04-Individuation-Process]]): the shadow, the anima/animus, and the Self are not abstract ideas but **figures met in this dialog**.

## The Method

Jung describes the practice in "The Transcendent Function" as a **four-stage movement**:

1. **Elicit** an image. Begin from a mood, a dream fragment, or a spontaneous fantasy. Fix it in a stable form — by writing, drawing, painting, or movement (Jung painted; many practitioners use a journal).
2. **Enter** the image. With relaxed attention, let the scene develop **on its own**. You do not author it; you **witness and participate**. If a figure appears, you may speak to it.
3. **Dialog**. Ask the figure what it wants, why it is there. The critical move is that the **unconscious is allowed to reply** — the answer is not pre-scripted by the ego. This is the difference from ordinary daydreaming.
4. **Ethical consolidate**. Bring the content into **conscious life**: a decision, a change of behavior, an insight held. The work is worthless if it stays on the page; it must become **incarnate** (Jung: "the symbol wants to be lived").

## The Two Kinds of Thinking

Jung's essay "Two Kinds of Thinking" distinguishes **directed thinking** (verbal, logical, goal-bound — the thinking of the daytime ego) from **dreaming / fantasy thinking** (image-bound, associative, purposeless in the surface sense). Active imagination is the **bridge**: it takes the *imaginal* stream and submits it to a *moral* encounter. The "transcendent function" is the name for the **third thing** born when the two kinds of thinking are held in tension — the symbol that resolves the conflict between them. This is the psychological counterpart of the *coniunctio oppositorum* in [[04-Individuation-Process]].

## The Dangers

Jung was explicit that the method is **not** for the untrained or the unstable. The risks:

- **Inflation** — the ego identifies with the figure (especially a "wise" or "divine" one) and loses grounding. The cure is the **ethical consolidation** (stage 4): the symbol must be lived, not worshipped.
- **Possession** — unintegrated material floods the conscious mind. A *dream journal* and, for serious work, a **trained analyst** are the guardrails.
- **Confabulation** — the ego quietly writes the answers. Discipline is to **wait** for the reply that surprises you.

## The Modern Practice

A contemporary, bounded practice:

1. Keep a **dream journal** and select one charged image.
2. Set a timer (15-20 min). In writing, let the figure speak; respond as yourself.
3. Note where the reply **surprised** you — that is the unconscious, not the ego.
4. Translate one insight into a **concrete action** that week.
5. For depth or distress, work with a **Jungian analyst**, not alone.

The library's category 18 is the textual foundation; the practice requires discipline and, at the edge, a guide.
