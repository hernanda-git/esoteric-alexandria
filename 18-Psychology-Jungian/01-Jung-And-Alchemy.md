# Carl Jung and the Alchemical Unconscious

## The Man and the Project

**Carl Gustav Jung** (1875-1961) was the **founder** of **Analytical Psychology** (his own term, to distinguish from Freud's Psychoanalysis). Jung's intellectual project was **enormous**: he wanted to understand the **unconscious** in a way that included **the spiritual, the symbolic, the transpersonal** — the dimensions that Freud had **excluded** from his own theory of the unconscious.

The project led Jung to the **study of the alchemical tradition** in the 1920s, an interest that **dominated the second half** of his life (1935-1961). Jung was the **first modern psychologist** to take alchemy seriously as a **psychological** document. His study resulted in:

- **Psychology of the Transference** (1946, CW 16) — the first major work on alchemy as psychology
- **Aion** (1951, CW 9ii) — the alchemical symbolism of the Self, with the Christ as a symbol of the Self
- **Mysterium Coniunctionis** (1955/56, CW 14) — the magnum opus, the alchemical symbolism of the coniunctio oppositorum (the conjunction of opposites)
- **The Practice of Psychotherapy** (1954, CW 16) — the integration of alchemy with the clinical practice

Jung's study of alchemy was the **most thorough** in the modern era. He read hundreds of alchemical texts, made extensive notes, corresponded with the leading alchemists of the 20th century (mostly with **Marie-Louise von Franz**, his long-term collaborator), and produced the most influential modern psychological reading of the tradition.

## The Central Concepts

Jung's analytical psychology is built on **several central concepts** that map directly to the esoteric tradition:

### 1. The Unconscious

The **unconscious** (Jung's *das Unbewußte*) is the **vast reservoir** of psychic material that is **not** in the conscious awareness. The unconscious has **two layers**:

- The **personal unconscious** — the **suppressed, forgotten, or unacknowledged** material from the individual's life (similar to Freud's unconscious, but Jung's is **larger** and **less sexualized**)
- The **collective unconscious** — the **universal, inherited, species-wide** material that all humans share. The collective unconscious contains the **archetypes**.

### 2. The Shadow

The **shadow** is the **dark side** of the personality — the aspects of the self that the conscious mind has **rejected, suppressed, or denied**. The shadow is the **sum total** of the personal unconscious, plus the **dark archetypal material** (the dragon, the demon, the trickster).

The shadow is the **first thing** the practitioner must encounter. The shadow is the **alchemical nigredo** (the blackening, the putrefaction). The encounter with the shadow is the **first stage of individuation**.

### 3. The Anima and the Animus

The **anima** is the **feminine** archetype in the **male** psyche; the **animus** is the **masculine** archetype in the **female** psyche. The anima/animus is the **soul-image** — the inner figure of the opposite sex that the psyche uses to relate to the world and to the unconscious.

The anima/animus is the **alchemical mercury** (the volatile, the spirit, the soul). The work with the anima/animus is the **alchemical albedo** (the whitening, the purification).

### 4. The Self

The **Self** is the **totality** of the psyche — the **whole person**, including the conscious, the personal unconscious, and the collective unconscious. The Self is the **archetype of the totality**; the Self is what the psyche **is aiming at** in the process of individuation.

The Self is the **alchemical rubedo** (the reddening, the integration). The Self is the **philosopher's stone** — the goal of the *Opus Magnum*. The Self is the **Christ** (in the Christian symbolism) and the **Tao** (in the Taoist symbolism) and the **Atman** (in the Hindu symbolism).

### 5. Individuation

**Individuation** is the **process of becoming a self** — the **integration** of the conscious and the unconscious, the **wholeness** of the personality. Individuation is **not** individualism; individuation is the **opposite** of individualism (individuation is the **realization** of the **collective** in the **individual**).

Individuation is the **alchemical Opus Magnum** in modern form. The stages of individuation correspond to the stages of the *Opus*:

| Individuation stage | Alchemical stage | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Encounter with the shadow | **Nigredo** | The confrontation with the dark, the rejection, the suppressed |
| Integration of the anima/animus | **Albedo** | The work with the soul-image, the relation to the unconscious |
| Discovery of the Self | **Citrinitas** | The illumination, the insight, the dawn |
| Realization of the Self | **Rubedo** | The integration, the wholeness, the coniunctio oppositorum |

The correspondence is **exact**. Jung's individuation process is the **modern psychological statement** of the alchemical work.

## The Alchemical Correspondence

Jung's reading of alchemy is the **most influential** of the modern era. The alchemical symbols are the **psychological symbols**:

| Alchemical symbol | Psychological meaning |
|---|---|
| **Lead** (the prima materia) | The shadow, the unintegrated, the base |
| **Gold** (the goal) | The Self, the integrated, the totality |
| **Mercury** (the volatile) | The anima/animus, the soul-image, the connecting principle |
| **Sulfur** (the combustible) | The will, the fire, the conscious direction |
| **Salt** (the fixed) | The body, the matter, the physical reality |
| **The King and the Queen** | The conscious and the unconscious |
| **The Dragon** | The shadow, the unredeemed, the material |
| **The Rebis** (the two-thing) | The coniunctio oppositorum, the integration of masculine and feminine |
| **The Black Sun** (the sol niger) | The nigredo, the deepest darkness before the whitening |
| **The Red King/Queen** | The rubedo, the conjoined opposites |
| **The Green Lion** | The shadow, the dissolution, the dangerous energy |
| **The White Swan / Red Rose** | The albedo and the rubedo, the two final products |

The alchemical work is the **psychological work** of integrating the conscious and the unconscious, the personal and the collective, the masculine and the feminine. The alchemist in the lab is the **same person** as the alchemist in the consulting room.

## The Modern Reception

Jung's alchemical psychology is **alive** in three communities:

1. **The Jungian analytical community** — the **C.G. Jung Institute** (Zurich, 1948), the **C.G. Jung Institute of New York** (1962), the **Inter-Regional Society of Jungian Analysts** (USA), and dozens of other training institutes train analysts in the alchemical-psychological reading
2. **The depth-psychology community** — the **Archetypal Psychology** tradition (James Hillman, Thomas Moore), the **Process Work** (Arnold Mindell), the **psychosynthesis** (Roberto Assagioli) all draw on Jung's work
3. **The general reading public** — Jung's *Man and His Symbols* (1964) is the most-read introduction to Jungian psychology; the **Memories, Dreams, Reflections** (1963) is the most-read biography; the *Red Book* (Liber Novus, 2009) is the most-discussed posthumous work

The library's category 18 treats Jung as the **bridge** between the esoteric tradition and the modern psychological tradition. The alchemical work is the **psychological work**, and the psychological work is the **modern alchemical work**.

## The Practice

A modern practice of the Jungian tradition requires:

1. **The texts** — read Jung's *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*, *Man and His Symbols*, the *Collected Works* (the relevant volumes)
2. **The dream work** — keep a **dream journal**, record the dreams, work with the symbols
3. **The active imagination** — practice the **active imagination** technique (dialog with the unconscious figures)
4. **The shadow work** — identify the **shadow material** in your life, work with it consciously
5. **The analyst** — for serious work, find a **Jungian analyst** in a recognized institute

The library's category 18 is the **textual foundation**. The practice requires an analyst and a community.
