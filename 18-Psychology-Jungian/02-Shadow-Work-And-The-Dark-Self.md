# Shadow Work and the Dark Self

## The Concept

The **shadow** is the **dark side** of the personality — the aspects of the self that the conscious mind has **rejected, suppressed, or denied**. The shadow is the **sum total** of the **personal unconscious** (the forgotten, the suppressed, the denied from the individual's own life) plus the **dark archetypal material** (the dragon, the demon, the trickster, the predator).

The shadow is **not** necessarily evil. The shadow is the **part of the self** that the conscious mind has **not yet integrated**. The shadow can be **negative** (the rage, the envy, the greed) or **positive** (the creativity, the vitality, the power) — both are equally **repressed** by the ego, and both are equally **part** of the shadow.

The shadow is **the first** of the archetypes. The shadow is **the first thing** the practitioner must encounter. The shadow is the **alchemical nigredo** (the blackening, the putrefaction).

## The Two Layers

The shadow has **two layers**:

### 1. The Personal Shadow

The **personal shadow** is the **material from the individual's own life** that the conscious mind has **rejected, suppressed, or denied**. The personal shadow is **biographical** — it is the rage that the child was **not allowed to express**, the envy that the young adult was **not allowed to feel**, the sexuality that the adolescent was **not allowed to acknowledge**.

The personal shadow is **specific** to the individual. The personal shadow is the **biographical** shadow — the content of the personal unconscious.

### 2. The Archetypal Shadow

The **archetypal shadow** is the **universal, species-wide** material that the conscious mind has **rejected, suppressed, or denied**. The archetypal shadow is **biographical in the species** — it is the **dark god**, the **predator**, the **destroyer**, the **warrior**, the **trickster**.

The archetypal shadow is **collective**. The archetypal shadow is the **content of the collective unconscious** that the conscious mind has **not yet integrated**.

The two layers are **continuous**. The personal shadow **rests on** the archetypal shadow. The personal shadow is the **specific form** of the archetypal shadow in the individual life.

## The Mechanisms of the Shadow

The shadow is **formed** by **three mechanisms**:

### 1. Repression

The most common. The conscious mind **rejects** a feeling, a desire, a thought. The rejected material is **pushed into** the unconscious. The rejected material becomes the shadow.

The repression is often **early in life** (the child learns that certain feelings are not acceptable — the rage, the envy, the sexuality). The repression is **automatic** (the child does not choose to repress; the child simply learns).

### 2. Projection

The most visible. The shadow material is **projected onto** another person. The individual **sees** in another person the qualities that the individual **will not see** in themselves.

The projection is the **mechanism** of the **phobia**, the **prejudice**, the **scapegoating**. The projection is the **mechanism** of the **political division** (one group projects its shadow onto another group).

The projection is **bi-directional**. The **hero-worship** is a **projection** of the **golden shadow** (the positive qualities that the individual will not claim); the **scapegoating** is a **projection** of the **dark shadow** (the negative qualities that the individual will not own).

### 3. Denial

The most subtle. The conscious mind **does not see** the material in the first place. The denial is **not** a repression (the material is not pushed away); the denial is a **failure to register** the material.

The denial is the **mechanism** of the **addiction** (the addict does not see the addiction; the addict's denial is **invisible** to the addict). The denial is the **mechanism** of the **group-think** (the group does not see the group's blindness).

## The Encounter

The encounter with the shadow is **painful**. The shadow is the **part of the self** that the conscious mind has **denied**. The encounter is the **re-owning** of the denied part.

The encounter is **necessary**. The shadow is the **first** of the archetypes. The shadow is the **first thing** that must be **integrated** before the further work can begin.

The encounter is **typical in the early adult years** (the 30s and 40s), when the individual has accumulated enough life experience to have a **substantial shadow**. The encounter can be **triggered** by:

- A major life event (a divorce, a job loss, a serious illness, a death)
- A psychological crisis (a depression, an anxiety, a panic)
- A relationship conflict (the projection is **exploded**)
- A dream (the unconscious sends the message in the dream)
- A body symptom (the shadow is **embodied** in the symptom)

## The Golden Shadow

The **golden shadow** is the **positive shadow** — the qualities of the self that the conscious mind has **denied because they are too bright**. The golden shadow is the **creativity**, the **vitality**, the **power**, the **love**, the **joy** that the individual has **not claimed**.

The golden shadow is the **most painful** of the shadows. The dark shadow is painful because the individual has to **own the dark**; the golden shadow is painful because the individual has to **own the bright**.

The golden shadow is the **source** of the **hero's journey** (the hero must claim the **birthright** that has been denied). The golden shadow is the **source** of the **midlife opening** (the midlife adult must claim the **vitality** that has been suppressed).

## The Abraxas

The **Abraxas** is the **god above all gods** — the **god of the shadow** that **unites** the **light and the dark**. The Abraxas is the **Gnostic** term (the Gnostic teacher Basilides, 2nd c. CE, used the term); Jung adopted the term in *Seven Sermons to the Dead* (1916, the *Septem Sermones ad Mortuos*).

The Abraxas is **beyond** the opposition of **light and dark**, of **good and evil**. The Abraxas is the **wholeness** that **includes** both.

The Abraxas is the **deepest** of the symbols. The Abraxas is the **goal** of the individuation process — the **realization** that the opposites are **united** in the **wholeness**.

## The Practice

A modern practice of shadow work requires:

1. **The observation** — notice the **reactions** to others. The strong reaction (positive or negative) is **a signal** of the shadow. The reaction is **a projection**.
2. **The journaling** — keep a **shadow journal**. Record the **strong reactions** to others. Record the **recurring dreams**. Record the **physical symptoms**. Look for the **patterns**.
3. **The active imagination** — practice the **active imagination** technique. Dialog with the shadow figure. **Listen** to the shadow. **Receive** the shadow's message.
4. **The re-owning** — the most important step. The shadow is **not** an enemy; the shadow is **a part of the self**. **Re-own** the shadow. **Integrate** the shadow. **Thank** the shadow for the message.
5. **The community** — the practice is best learned in a **community** of practitioners (a **men's group**, a **women's group**, a **therapy group**, a **Jungian analysis**).

The library's category 18 is the **textual foundation**. The practice requires a community and (for serious work) a **trained analyst**.

## The Modern Note

Shadow work is **alive** in three communities:

1. **The Jungian analytical community** — the **shadow** is one of the **core concepts** of the Jungian training
2. **The modern popular spirituality** — the **shadow work** of **Debbie Ford**, **Connie Zweig**, and the "**Chopra**" tradition has made the concept **popular**
3. **The modern psychotherapy** — the **IFS** (Internal Family Systems) of **Richard Schwartz**, the **Shadow Work** of **Cliff Barry**, the **Voice Dialogue** of **Hal Stone** and **Sidra Stone** are all **descendants** of the Jungian shadow

The library's category 18 treats the shadow as the **central concept** of the modern psychological reading of the esoteric tradition. The shadow is the **first** of the archetypes; the encounter with the shadow is the **first** of the stages of individuation.
