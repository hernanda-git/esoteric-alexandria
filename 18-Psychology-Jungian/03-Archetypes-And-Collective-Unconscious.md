# Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious

## The Concept

The **archetype** (Greek *archetypon*, "the original pattern") is Jung's term for the **universal, inherited, species-wide patterns of the human psyche**. The archetypes are the **deep structure** of the collective unconscious; they are the **patterns** that the personal unconscious draws upon, the **forms** that the psyche uses to shape the contents of consciousness.

The archetypes are **not** the **contents** of the unconscious; the archetypes are the **forms** that the contents take. The archetype is the **channel**, the **mold**, the **template**; the content is the **specific** dream, the **specific** myth, the **specific** symbol that is shaped by the archetype.

The archetype is **comparable** to:

- The **platonic Form** or **Idea** (the universal, the abstract, the principle)
- The **Kantian category** (the form of the understanding that shapes the sense-data)
- The **linguistic deep structure** (Chomsky) (the universal grammar that shapes the specific language)
- The **DNA** (the inherited pattern that shapes the organism)

The archetype is **biologically** based. The archetype is the **psychic equivalent** of the instinct. The archetype is the **psychic organ** that the human species has **evolved** to deal with the recurrent problems of human life.

## The Major Archetypes

Jung identified **several major archetypes**. The most important:

### 1. The Self

The **Self** is the **totality archetype** — the archetype of the **whole person**, the **integrated psyche**. The Self is the **goal** of individuation. The Self is the **center** of the mandala.

The Self is **represented** by:
- The **circle** (the mandala)
- The **quaternity** (the four)
- The **child** (the divine child, the Christ child, the Buddha child)
- The **stone** (the philosopher's stone, the foundation, the corner-stone)
- The **sun** (the solar deity, the Christ, the Buddha, the Atman)

### 2. The Shadow

The **Shadow** is the **dark archetype** — the archetype of the **rejected, the suppressed, the dark**. The shadow is the **first** archetype encountered in the process of individuation. The shadow is the **sum total** of the personal unconscious, plus the **dark archetypal material** (the dragon, the demon, the trickster).

The shadow is **represented** by:
- The **dragon** (the unredeemed, the dangerous)
- The **serpent** (the wisdom that is dangerous)
- The **dark** (the night, the unknown, the unconscious)
- The **swamp** (the dark, the stagnant, the suppressed)

### 3. The Anima (in men) and the Animus (in women)

The **Anima** (in the male psyche) and the **Animus** (in the female psyche) are the **soul-image archetypes** — the inner figure of the opposite sex. The anima/animus is the **mediator** between the conscious and the unconscious; the anima/animus is the **bridge** to the inner world.

The anima is **represented** by:
- The **goddess** (Isis, Persephone, Demeter, the Virgin Mary, Sophia)
- The **muse** (the inspiring, the beautiful, the soulful)
- The **siren** (the dangerous, the seductive, the death-bringing)

The animus is **represented** by:
- The **hero** (the active, the directive, the masculine)
- The **sage** (the wise, the logical, the directive)
- The **demonic** (the cruel, the destructive, the negative animus)

### 4. The Persona

The **Persona** is the **mask** — the **social face** that the individual presents to the world. The persona is the **adaptation** to the social world; the persona is the **role** that the individual plays in the social drama.

The persona is **necessary** (the individual cannot exist in society without a persona), but the persona is **not** the whole self. The individual must **distinguish** the persona from the **true self**; the individual must **not** confuse the two.

### 5. The Trickster

The **Trickster** is the **boundary-crossing archetype** — the archetype of the **boundary, the rule, the limit**. The trickster is the **Coyote** of the Native American tradition, the **Loki** of the Norse tradition, the **Hermes** of the Greek tradition, the **Eshu / Elegba** of the Yoruba tradition.

The trickster is the **mediator** between the order and the chaos; the trickster **tests** the rules, the trickster **breaks** the rules, the trickster **re-establishes** the rules.

### 6. The Wise Old Man (the Sage)

The **Wise Old Man** is the **wisdom archetype** — the archetype of the **guidance, the meaning, the deep knowledge**. The wise old man is the **Hermes**, the **Thoth**, the **Odin**, the **Gandalf**, the **Yoda**.

The wise old man is **represented** in dreams as the **father, the grandfather, the professor, the doctor, the priest, the wizard**.

### 7. The Divine Child

The **Divine Child** is the **potential archetype** — the archetype of the **future, the possibility, the becoming**. The divine child is the **Christ child**, the **Buddha child**, the **Krishna child**, the **son of the mother** (the puer aeternus, the eternal youth).

The divine child is **represented** in dreams as the **child, the baby, the small son, the young prince**.

## The Numinous

The archetypes are **numinous** — they are experienced as **powerful, mysterious, charged with meaning**. The experience of the numinous is the experience of the **sacred**, the experience of the **archetypal**.

The numinous is **not** the rational; the numinous is **beyond** the rational. The numinous is the **felt presence** of the archetype.

The numinous is **comparable** to:

- The **Hebrew *kavod*** (the divine glory, the weighty presence)
- The **Islamic *sakinah*** (the divine peace, the divine presence)
- The **Hindu *Brahman*** (the absolute, the sacred)
- The **Buddhist *bodhi*** (the awakened, the enlightened)

The numinous is the **psychological name** for the **sacred**.

## The Mandala

The **mandala** (Sanskrit, "circle") is the **archetype of the Self**. The mandala is the **circular figure** that the psyche uses to **represent** the Self. The mandala is the **most common** of the **spontaneous** images in the psyche.

The mandala has:
- A **center** (the point of the Self)
- A **circle** (the boundary of the Self)
- A **quaternity** (the four cardinal points, the four functions of consciousness)
- Often a **gate** (the entrance to the inner world)

The mandala is the **therapeutic image**. The mandala **organizes** the psyche; the mandala **centers** the disordered self. Jung recommended the **daily mandala** to his patients (the practice of drawing a mandala each day as a way of integrating the day's unconscious material).

## The Modern Note

The archetype concept is **alive** in three communities:

1. **The Jungian analytical community** — the **archetype** is one of the **core concepts** of the Jungian training
2. **The depth-psychology community** — the **archetypal psychology** of **James Hillman** (the founder of the **Archetypal Psychology** tradition) has **modified** the concept (Hillman prefers "image" over "archetype", but the field is the same)
3. **The popular spirituality community** — the "archetype" is a common concept in the popular spirituality literature

The library's category 18 treats the archetype as the **central concept** of the modern psychological reading of the esoteric tradition. The archetypes are the **deep structure** of the human psyche; the esoteric symbols are the **cultural expressions** of the archetypes.

## The Practice

A modern practice of the archetype concept requires:

1. **The texts** — read Jung's CW 9i, the von Franz and Edinger commentaries, the Hillman re-reading
2. **The dream work** — keep a **dream journal**, identify the **archetypal figures**, work with the symbols
3. **The active imagination** — practice the **active imagination** technique (dialog with the archetypal figures)
4. **The mandala work** — draw a **daily mandala** (the Jungian practice)
5. **The analyst** — for serious work, find a **Jungian analyst** in a recognized institute

The library's category 18 is the **textual foundation**. The practice requires an analyst and a community.
