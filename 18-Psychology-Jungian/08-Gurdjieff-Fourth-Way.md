# Gurdjieff and the Fourth Way — The Work on Oneself

## The Teaching That Called Itself a "Work"

The **Fourth Way** — also called **The Work** — is the esoteric system of **George Ivanovich Gurdjieff** (c. 1866–1949), one of the most enigmatic figures in Western esotericism. Gurdjieff presented his teaching not as a doctrine, a philosophy, or a religion, but as a **practical method of self-transformation** — a technology for producing conscious human beings from the raw material of mechanical humanity.

The system's core premise is severe:

> **The ordinary human being is a machine.** We do not act. We are *acted through*. Every thought, feeling, and movement is the mechanical result of external stimuli filtered through a disordered set of independent "centers" that act automatically and without our awareness. We have no unified "I." We are a parade of **fleeting, contradictory selves** — one deciding, another acting, a third watching, none aware of the others. We are not born with a permanent self. We must **create it** — through conscious labor and intentional suffering.

This is not a metaphor. Gurdjieff insisted on a **literal, physiological, and energetic** reading: the human machine has multiple energy centers (intellectual, emotional, instinctive, moving, sexual) that operate at incompatible speeds and with different "fuels." The goal of the Work is not belief but **being** — a transformed quality of presence that manifests as unified consciousness, voluntary attention, and objective reason.

## The Life: The Greek-Armenian Teacher from the Caucasus

Gurdjieff's early life is a matter of his own (likely mythologized) account in *Meetings with Remarkable Men*. He was born in Alexandropol (now Gyumri, Armenia) to a Greek father and Armenian mother. His father was a *gusan* — a traditional bard who recited the Epic of Gilgamesh and other ancient oral narratives. This oral transmission format deeply shaped Gurdjieff's own teaching style.

As a young man, Gurdjieff claims he travelled through Central Asia, the Middle East, India, Tibet, and Egypt — searching for **"ancient knowledge"** preserved in esoteric schools that he called the **"Sarmoung Brotherhood"** and the **"Inner Circle of Humanity."** He located such a school (or schools) in the mountains of the Hindu Kush, or possibly in the monasteries of Sarmoung near the Oxus River. The historical accuracy of these claims is debated. What is certain is that between 1885 and 1912, Gurdjieff assembled the core of his system from a synthesis of:

- **Sufi teachings** (especially the Naqshbandi tradition of the Khwajagan, the "Masters of Wisdom")
- **Esoteric Christianity** (the Philokalia, Hesychast prayer, the Eastern Orthodox ascetic tradition)
- **Tibetan Tantrism** (Gurdjieff's "Movements" — the sacred dances — show close parallels to Tibetan cham dances and mandala visualizations)
- **Central Asian shamanism** (the use of music, drumming, and group trance)
- **The Western esoteric tradition** (to which he explicitly aligned himself, claiming his system was a "recovery" of knowledge known to the ancient Egyptians, Pythagoreans, and early Christians)

In 1912, Gurdjieff began teaching in Moscow and St. Petersburg. He gathered a circle of intellectuals who would become the first wave of his pupils, most notably the Russian philosopher‑journalist **Pyotr Demianovich Ouspensky** (1878–1947), whose book *In Search of the Miraculous* is the most lucid and systematic exposition of Gurdjieff's ideas in any language.

The Russian Revolution scattered the group. Gurdjieff led a remarkable cross‑Caucasus exodus on foot, established the **Institute for the Harmonious Development of Man** at the Château du Prieuré in Fontainebleau‑Avon (France) in 1922, and then toured the United States twice. In 1924, he was nearly killed in a car accident. What followed was a decade of writing — the massive trilogy **"All and Everything"** — and the gradual re‑formation of his teaching groups. He died in Paris in 1949.

## The Seven Foundational Ideas

### 1. The Human Machine and the "False Personality"

Gurdjieff's anthropology begins with a brutal diagnosis: the human being is not a unity but a **multiplicity**. The "I" that the average person speaks of — "I think," "I feel," "I decide" — is not a stable entity. It is a **momentary identification** that shifts from one sub‑personality to another as external circumstances change:

> "Man has no individual I. But there are hundreds and thousands of separate little I's, very often entirely unknown to one another, never coming into contact, or, on the contrary, hostile to each other, mutually exclusive and incompatible." — *In Search of the Miraculous*

These thousands of "I's" are organized around a central formation that Gurdjieff calls the **False Personality** — the collection of opinions, roles, identifications, and protective masks that the ordinary person mistakes for their real self. The Work's first task is to **develop an observer** — a "witness‑consciousness" — that can see the false personality in action without being identified with it.

### 2. The Four Centers — The Mechanical Structure

The human machine has **four** (or more) **brains/centers**, each operating with its own form of intelligence:

| Center | Function | Fuel | Defect |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| **Intellectual** | Thinking, calculation, abstraction | Finer energies (impressions) | Non‑stop talking; "mental diarrhoea" |
| **Emotional** | Feeling, attraction/aversion, aesthetic sense | Coarser (emotion) | Attachment, sentimentality, negative emotions |
| **Instinctive** | Breathing, digestion, heartbeat, sensations | Food (grossest) | Waking sleep — runs automatically |
| **Moving** | Movement, learning by doing, habit‑formation | Same as instinctive | Acting before knowing; endless friction |

In the ordinary person, these centers operate **at cross‑purposes**. The intellectual center works at 30,000 "vibrations per second," the emotional center at a different speed, the moving center at another. They interfere. The intellectual center tries to do the work of the emotional center; the emotional center tries to do the work of the moving center; the results are chaotic, wasteful, and self‑defeating.

The Work aims to **harmonize the centers** — to have each center do its own proper work at its own proper speed, synchronized under the direction of a developed **master‑consciousness** (the "I").

### 3. Self‑Remembering — The Fundamental Practice

If ordinary humans are machines, driven by mechanical associations, the single most important act of resistance is **self‑remembering**:

> "Self‑remembering is the moment in which a man is present to himself as he is — not thinking about himself, not feeling himself, not imagining himself, but *being himself*."

The practice is simpler and harder than it sounds: in the middle of any activity, you divide your attention. One part remains on what you are doing. The other part faces **inward** — aware of the posture, the breathing, the quality of sensation, the feeling of "I" in this moment. Gurdjieff used a concrete instruction: **"Remember yourself"** — not as an incantation, but as a call to instant presence that breaks the mechanical chain.

Self‑remembering is not mindfulness (which is a continuous state of attention from one moment to the next). It is a **punctual interruption** — a voltage spike of consciousness that the machine cannot sustain for long but that, repeated often enough, begins to build a permanent "wire" of self‑awareness in the nervous system.

### 4. The Law of Three and the Law of Seven

Gurdjieff claimed to have discovered two universal laws that govern all processes, from the creation of galaxies to the digestion of bread to the transformation of consciousness:

**The Law of Three** (the **Triamazikamno** in Gurdjieff's invented language): Every phenomenon — every event, process, being, or thought — arises from the interaction of three forces:
- **Affirming force** (active, positive, "+")
- **Denying force** (passive, resisting, "−")
- **Reconciling force** (neutral, the principle that binds the two, "o")

This law — derived ultimately from the Hindu *gunas* (sattva, rajas, tamas) and the Hermetic trinity of Sulphur‑Mercury‑Salt — operates at every level. A chemical reaction requires an acid, a base, and a catalyst. A thought requires a subject, an object, and the act of perception. A work requires a doing, a being‑done‑to, and the witnessing.

**The Law of Seven** (the **Heptaparaparshinokh**): No process moves in a straight line. All processes — the octave, the week, the stages of alchemical transformation — proceed through **seven incremental steps** with specific **intervals** (the "gaps" between mi‑fa and si‑do in the musical scale) where the process can either continue with an external "shock" or fall back into the starting state.

The Enneagram (see below) is the combined diagram of the Law of Three and the Law of Seven — the mathematical figure that shows how they interlock.

### 5. The Enneagram — The Universal Glyph

![The Enneagram figure — a nine‑pointed star inscribed in a circle, with the diagram of the Law of Seven (1‑4‑2‑8‑5‑7) and the Law of Three (3‑6‑9) super‑imposed.]

The **Enneagram** (Greek *ennea* = nine) is Gurdjieff's most famous — and most misunderstood — contribution. It is a geometric figure: a circle with nine points (1 through 9), connected by two lines:
- The **Law of Seven** describes the inner movement: 1 → 4 → 2 → 8 → 5 → 7 → (back to 1)
- The **Law of Three** describes the stable triangular figure: points 3, 6, and 9 connected

Gurdjieff used the Enneagram as:
1. **A cosmic diagram** — showing how the three forces and seven stages interlock in every process
2. **A teaching tool** — Gurdjieff called it the "universal symbol" and the foundation of "sacred science"
3. **A movement score** — the Enneagram guides the choreography of Gurdjieff's **sacred dances** (the Movements)

Gurdjieff's Enneagram is **not** the same as the popular "Enneagram of personality types" (9 types of ego‑fixations popularized by Oscar Ichazo, Claudio Naranjo, and the Enneagram Institute). Gurdjieff's Enneagram is a **process symbol** — a map of transformation, not a personality typing tool. The personality type system was retro‑fitted onto the diagram by later interpreters and is entirely absent from Gurdjieff's own teaching.

### 6. Conscious Labor and Intentional Suffering

The Work has two concrete methods:

**Conscious labor** — action done with divided attention (self‑remembering while working). Gurdjieff's favourite form was manual labour: chopping wood, digging gardens, scrubbing floors — but done *consciously* as a practice, not lazily or mechanically. The point is not the task but the state of the doer.

**Intentional suffering** — the voluntary, conscious acceptance of discomfort, frustration, and the unpleasant parts of one's own nature without complaint, identification, or reaction. This is not asceticism (punishing the body for spiritual merit). It is the willingness to feel the gap between where you are and where the Work asks you to be — without trying to close it by fantasy or escape.

These two methods together Gurdjieff called the **"chief means"** — the only way to accumulate the finer energies (life‑force, soul‑stuff) that eventually crystallize as the **"astral body"** (or "kesdjan body") and later the **"spiritual body"** (the soul that survives physical death).

### 7. Being, Essence, and the Possible Evolution of Man

For Gurdjieff, the category of "human" is not a fixed state but a **range of possibilities**:

| Level | Gurdjieff's Term | Characteristic |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Man #1** | Centered in moving/instinctive center | Physical man; the body‑dominated; athlete, peasant, soldier |
| **Man #2** | Centered in emotional center | Feeling‑man; artist, lover, devotee |
| **Man #3** | Centered in intellectual center | Thinking‑man; scientist, philosopher, scholar |
| **Man #4** | Balanced centers; the "man of the fourth way" | The person who has begun the Work; the first step toward real being |
| **Man #5** | Crystallized astral body | Unified consciousness; "has a permanent I" |
| **Man #6** | Crystallized spiritual body | Objective consciousness; knows reality directly |
| **Man #7** | Complete man | "Full human"; has a permanent I, a developed will, and objective reason |

The ordinary person (Man #1, #2, or #3) has **personality** (the acquired, automatic repertoire of responses) but not **being** (the stable, conscious, self‑possessed core). Most people die with nothing but personality — which dissolves at death. The Work exists to help a person grow **being** while alive — to become capable of conscious survival after death.

## The Fourth Way: Why It Is Different

Gurdjieff distinguished the Fourth Way from the three traditional approaches:

| Traditional Path | Dominant Center | Characteristic | Danger |
|-----------------|----------------|----------------|--------|
| **The Way of the Fakir** | Physical/moving | Mastery of the body through extreme asceticism (yogis, stylites) | Takes decades; one dominates only the body; the other centres remain raw |
| **The Way of the Monk** | Emotional | Mastery of the emotions through devotion, prayer, and surrender | Faith is a gift; the mountaintop is not available on demand; the dry periods are destructive for the un‑prepared |
| **The Way of the Yogi** | Intellectual | Mastery of the mind through concentration, study, and discrimination | Intellect can be developed while emotions remain completely infantile; many "spiritually advanced" people are emotional children |
| **The Fourth Way** | All three, balanced | Work on body, emotions, and intellect **simultaneously**, within ordinary life | Demands constant vigilance; no external authority to lean on |

The Fourth Way is different in three crucial respects:

1. **No retreat** — the Fourth Way is practised **in daily life**. You do not need to become a monk, a yogi, or a fakir. The material of the Work is your ordinary existence: your job, your relationships, your habits, your reactions.

2. **No dogma** — the Fourth Way does not require belief. It is a technology of transformation that works whether or not you accept its cosmological vocabulary. You test it by experiments: try self‑remembering for thirty seconds; try refusing a mechanical negative emotion for five minutes; try listening without already formulating your reply.

3. **The school is the method** — the Fourth Way cannot be practised alone, at least not in its early stages. You need a **conscious circle** of fellow workers and (ideally) a teacher who has already completed the transformation and can see what you cannot see about yourself. Gurdjieff's school at the Prieuré was the archetype: a controlled environment of manual labour, Movement practice, study, and group interaction designed to "bang" each participant against the mirror of the others.

## Parallels in the Library

### With Jung (18‑Psychology‑Jungian)

| Gurdjieff | Jung |
|-----------|------|
| Personality vs. Essence | Persona vs. Self |
| False personality | The persona‑complex; the shadow-self one mistakes for the real self |
| Self‑remembering | Active imagination — deliberately holding an inner tension |
| The centers (intellectual, emotional, moving) | The four functions (thinking, feeling, sensation, intuition) |
| Mechanicality — the human as machine | The complexes — autonomous psychic fragments that run us |
| The development of Being | Individuation — becoming one's own Self |
| The Enneagram as process diagram | The mandala as process diagram |

Jung and Gurdjieff never met (though Jung was aware of Gurdjieff's work). The structural parallels are remarkable, especially given that both derived their systems from decades of direct observation of unconscious processes in groups and individuals.

### With Sufism (09)

Gurdjieff's relationship to Sufism is his most discussed (and debated) lineage claim. The parallels are concrete:

- The **Naqshbandi** practice of *yad dasht* (remembrance in the marketplace) maps onto self‑remembering
- The *khwajagan* (Masters of Wisdom) tradition of secret esoteric transmission parallels Gurdjieff's account of his teachers
- The **Maqamat** (stations of the journey) map onto the levels of being
- Gurdjieff explicitly used Sufi stories (especially the *Mulla Nasrudin* corpus) as teaching tools
- The **Mevlevi Sema** (whirling) has a direct analogue in Gurdjieff's sacred Movements

The difference: classical Sufism operates within an Islamic theological framework (tawḥīd, prophethood, sharīʿa). Gurdjieff deliberately de‑Islamised the techniques for a Western audience and framed them in a psychological‑cosmological language.

### With Alchemy (04)

The opus magnum of alchemy is the transformation of base metal into gold. For Gurdjieff, it is the transformation of mechanical man (lead) into conscious being (gold). The stages align:

| Alchemical Stage | Gurdjieff's Equivalent |
|-----------------|------------------------|
| Calcination | Breaking the false personality |
| Dissolution | Seeing through one's own identifications; the moment of "I am a machine" |
| Separation | Distinguishing essence from personality, the observer from the observed |
| Conjunction | Bringing the centers into harmony; the "third force" within |
| Fermentation | The shock that changes the quality of being |
| Distillation | Refinement of the finer energies through conscious labor |
| Coagulation | Crystallization of the permanent "I"; the Philosopher's Stone |

## The Question of Legitimacy

The Fourth Way has always been controversial:

- **Historical verifiability**: Gurdjieff's claims about his travels and his teachers are unverifiable and almost certainly embellished. The "Sarmoung Brotherhood" has no independent historical record.
- **Cult‑like structure**: The Prieuré had elements that would today be called cultic — hierarchical discipline, confession‑style group work, financial demands on pupils.
- **The teacher‑pupil bond**: Gurdjieff's methods were unorthodox even by esoteric standards. He deliberately created uncomfortable situations, manipulated group dynamics, and broke personal boundaries in the name of awakening the pupil. The ethical line between "intentional suffering" and abuse is not always clear in the historical record.
- **The later fragmentation**: After Gurdjieff's death, the Work fragmented into competing groups — the Ouspensky‑founded "Society for the Study of Normal Psychology," the Gurdjieff Foundation (under Jeanne de Salzmann), the Bennett‑influenced groups, the Fourth Way schools fed into the human potential movement (Esalen, Gestalt therapy), and the endlessly‑commercialized Enneagram system. Which, if any, preserves Gurdjieff's original aim is an open question.

Despite these controversies, the core **diagnostic utility** of the Fourth Way — the recognition that ordinary consciousness is mechanical, that unity of being must be built, and that self‑observation is a technical skill to be learned — remains an extraordinarily powerful tool for anyone engaged in self‑transformation, whether or not they accept the full metaphysical apparatus.

## Reading List

**First‑time reader:**
- P. D. Ouspensky, *In Search of the Miraculous: Fragments of an Unknown Teaching* (1949) — start here. This is the most coherent, systematic presentation of Gurdjieff's ideas.
- G. I. Gurdjieff, *Meetings with Remarkable Men* (1963) — the autobiographical narrative; an easier entry than the trilogy.
- Kathleen Riordan Speeth, *The Gurdjieff Work* (1976) — the best short modern introduction.

**Advanced:**
- G. I. Gurdjieff, *Beelzebub's Tales to His Grandson* (1950) — the primary text. Demanding, idiosyncratic, deliberately opaque. Read in small doses with a group.
- G. I. Gurdjieff, *Views from the Real World* (1973) — early talks transcribed by pupils; more accessible than Beelzebub.
- Maurice Nicoll, *Psychological Commentaries* (5 vols., 1952‑1956) — the most thorough psychological exegesis from a Jungian‑inflected perspective.

**Secondary & Critical:**
- J. G. Bennett, *Gurdjieff: Making a New World* (1973) — comprehensive biography by a direct pupil.
- Jacob Needleman (ed.), *Gurdjieff: Essays and Reflections* (1996) — philosophical and comparative essays.
- William Patrick Patterson, *Struggle of the Magicians* (1996) — the Gurdjieff‑Ouspensky relationship.
- Gary Lachman, *In Search of P. D. Ouspensky* (2004) — the definitive Ouspensky biography, with extensive context on the Fourth Way in the 20th‑century spiritual ferment.
