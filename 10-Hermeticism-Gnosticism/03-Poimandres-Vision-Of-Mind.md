# The Poimandres — The Vision of the Mind

## The First Treatise

The **Poimandres** (Ποίμανδρος, "Shepherd of Men" or "Man-Shepherd") is the **first treatise** of the Corpus Hermeticum. It is the **master text** of the Hermetic tradition — the vision that sets the tone for all the other 16 treatises. The Coptic and Latin traditions preserve the title variously; the modern standard is **"Poimandres"** in the form given by the 1460 Greek manuscript.

The text is a **first-person vision** — Hermes reports being visited by **Poimandres** (the **Mind in person**, the *Nous* hypostatized) and being shown the **cosmogony** (the origin of the cosmos) and the **return of the human** to the divine.

The text is the **most-quoted** of the Hermetic treatises. The Renaissance, the Golden Dawn, the modern Thelemic tradition have all used it as the **central contemplative text** of the Hermetic path.

## The Vision

The vision is structured in **5 movements** (the modern section divisions, which match the natural breaks in the Coptic and the 15th-c. Greek):

### Movement 1 — The Vision of the Mind (Sections 1-3)

Hermes is in a state of "thought, conjecture, and reflection" when **Poimandres** appears — an enormous, beautiful, all-seeing vision of a giant being. The Mind shows Hermes **the boundless vision** of the cosmos. Hermes is overcome; his body trembles; he sees the **endless vision** of the divine.

### Movement 2 — The Cosmogony (Sections 4-7)

The vision shows Hermes the **origin of the cosmos**. The sequence:

1. **The Light** — the primal light, the "Paternity-Father"
2. **The Darkness** — the material world, the deep, the abyss
3. **The Fire** — the divine nature, the "power of the Word"
4. **The Spirit** — the world-soul, the connecting principle
5. **The Logos** — the Word, the creative utterance
6. **The seven governors** — the **7 planetary rulers** (Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Sun, Venus, Mercury, Moon), the Archons of the cosmic structure
7. **The earth and the waters** — the material world, the home of the human

The cosmogony is **reminiscent of Genesis** but **strikingly different**. The Hermetic creator is **not** a person commanding "let there be light"; the Hermetic creator is the **process of the Mind** bringing the cosmos into being through contemplation. The cosmos is **thought becoming form**.

### Movement 3 — The Origin of the Human (Sections 8-10)

The Mind (Poimandres) tells Hermes the **origin of the human**:

> "The Mind, the Father of all things, the Life and the Light, gave birth to Man, equal to itself, beloved by it, beautiful in image. For in truth the Father, who is Life and Light, gave birth to Man, the same as himself, and he gave him over to his own hand, the creative hand, that he might know the things that were made."

The human is **born from the Life and the Light** of the divine Mind. The human is the **image of the creator**. The human is given **dominion** over the cosmos ("he gave him over to his own hand").

The human looks at the cosmos, sees the seven governors, and **ascends into the cosmic structure**. The cosmos falls in love with the human; the seven governors love the human; the human is **at home** in the cosmos.

But then — **the fall**. The human looks at the **lowest part of the cosmos** (the material world) and **descends into matter**. The human **breaks** the unity of the cosmos; the human becomes **mortal**; the human forgets the divine origin.

### Movement 4 — The Two Lives (Sections 11-14)

The Mind shows Hermes the **two lives**:

1. **The life of the body** — the mortal, the perishable, the many
2. **The life of the Mind** — the immortal, the imperishable, the one

The Mind shows Hermes the **vision of the dissolution** of the cosmos. The seven governors return to the divine; the cosmos is **unmade**. The mortal rises **from the body** to the divine. The **imperishable** (the spark, the noetic) returns to the Mind.

The Mind then **disappears**. Hermes is left with the **memory of the vision**.

### Movement 5 — The Rebirth (Sections 15-19)

Hermes, **enlightened** by the vision, asks Poimandres how to **return**. Poimandres teaches the **rebirth** (*palingenesia*, the "second birth"):

1. **Repentance** — turn from the body, turn from the mortal
2. **Gnosis** — know the divine origin
3. **Re-birth** — the divine descends into the human; the human ascends into the divine
4. **The hymn of praise** — the practitioner sings the **hymn of the regeneration**
5. **The vision** — the practitioner is shown the cosmos from the perspective of the Mind
6. **The un-becoming** — the practitioner **dissolves** into the divine; the practitioner's body is left behind; the practitioner's Mind returns to the Mind

The re-birth is **not** an event in time; it is a **state of consciousness**. The practitioner who has been re-born **lives** in the re-birth; the practitioner who has been re-born **knows** the divine directly; the practitioner who has been re-born is **already** saved.

## The Hymn of the Rebirth

The Poimandres closes with a **hymn** of praise (Sections 18-19), recited by the re-born:

> "I know myself, I am the Mind, the Word, the Father of the All, the One, the All. From me all things exist, and in me all things have their end."

This hymn is the **central prayer** of the Hermetic tradition. The Golden Dawn, the Thelemic tradition, the modern Hermetic orders all recite this hymn in their rituals.

## The Modern Practice

A modern practice of the Poimandres requires:

1. **Read the text** — multiple times, in different translations (Copenhaver, Salaman, Scott)
2. **Meditate on the vision** — sit with the cosmogony, the fall, the return
3. **Practice the hymn** — recite the hymn of the re-birth as a daily practice
4. **Study the symbols** — the 7 governors, the 5 movements, the 2 lives
5. **Find a tradition** — the Hermetic tradition has many lineages

The Poimandres is the **most-read** of the Hermetic texts. It is the **easiest entry** to the Corpus. The 5 movements are the **skeleton** of the Hermetic contemplative practice.

## The Connection to Other Traditions

The Poimandres has **remarkable parallels** to other traditions:

| Poimandres element | Parallel |
|---|---|
| The Mind (Nous) | The Nous of Plotinus, the Logos of Philo, the Christ of the Gospel of John |
| The cosmogony (Light, Darkness, Fire, Spirit) | The Genesis creation, the Zoroastrian Ahura Mazda / Angra Mainyu, the Kabbalistic Ein Sof / Tzimtzum |
| The fall of the human | The Adam and Eve of Genesis, the Gnostic Sophia's fall, the Hindu *pravritti* (outward turn) |
| The 7 governors | The 7 Archons of the Gnostics, the 7 planetary rulers of the Hermetic and Islamic traditions, the 7 chakras of the Tantric tradition (in a different mapping) |
| The re-birth (palingenesia) | The Christian baptism, the Buddhist second birth, the Sufi fana' (annihilation), the Hindu moksha (liberation) |
| The hymn of the re-birth | The bhakti hymns of the Hindu and Sufi traditions, the Christian liturgical hymns, the Buddhist gathas |

The Poimandres is the **bridge text** of the Western esoteric tradition. It connects the Greek philosophical tradition, the Jewish Kabbalistic tradition, the Christian mystical tradition, the Gnostic tradition, and the modern Hermetic tradition.

## The Modern Note

The Poimandres is the **single most influential** of the pre-Christian esoteric texts in the modern era. The Renaissance humanists (Ficino, Pico, Bruno) used it as the **foundation text** of the Western esoteric tradition. The Golden Dawn used it as the **central contemplative text**. The Thelemic tradition (Crowley) used it as the **basis of the new Aeon**.

The library's category 10 treats the Poimandres as the **most-cited** and **most-practiced** of the Hermetic texts. Every modern Hermetic order studies the Poimandres as the **first text** of the curriculum.
