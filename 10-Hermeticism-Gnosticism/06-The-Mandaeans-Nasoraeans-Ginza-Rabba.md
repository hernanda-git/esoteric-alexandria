# The Mandaeans — The Nasoraeans and the Ginza Rabba

## The People

The **Mandaeans** (from *manda*, "**knowledge**", "**gnosis**" — **Manda d-Hayyi**, "**Knowledge of Life**") are a **Gnostic** **baptist** **community** of **lower** **Iraq** and **Khuzestan** (south-west **Iran**). They are the **only** **continuous** **Gnostic** **tradition** **surviving** from **late** **antiquity** into the **present**; they are a **living** **religion**, not a **textual** **corpus** **excavated** from the **sand**. Their **numbers** today are **small** (estimated **60,000-100,000**, many **displaced** since **2003**) but their **continuity** makes them the **critical** **witness** to **what** **"Gnostic"** **meant** as a **practice**, not merely a **heresy** **label**.

The Mandaeans call themselves **Nasoraeans** (*Naṣuraiia*), a term **distinct** from **"Nazoraeans"** / **Nazarenes** (the **early** **Jewish-Christian** **sect**) and **not** a **reference** to **Jesus** of **Nazareth**. The **Nasoraean** is the **initiate** who **holds** the **secret** **knowledge** and the **priestly** **discipline**; the **term** signals **continuity** with a **pre-Christian** **baptist** **current** (the **world** of **John the Baptist**, whom the Mandaeans **revere** as a **great** **teacher**, though **not** as the **Messiah**).

## The Scripture — The Ginza Rabba

The **Ginza Rabba** ( genus "**The Great Treasure**") is the **principal** **scripture** of the Mandaeans. It is **divided** into the **Right Ginza** (*Ginza Smala*, the "**light**" **side**, the **revelatory** **cosmology** and **myth**) and the **Left Ginza** (*Ginza Smaala*, the "**dark**" **side**, the **hymns** for the **dead** and the **afterlife**). The **text** is in **Mandaic**, a **dialect** of **Eastern** **Aramaic** (the **closest** **living** **relative** of the **language** of **Jesus** and **John**). The **compilation** is **dated** to **c. 3rd-7th c. CE**, with **earlier** **strata** and **later** **additions**.

The **Ginza** presents the **standard** **Gnostic** **myth** in **Mandaean** **form**: the **World of Light** (the **realm** of the **Great Life**, *Hayyi Rabbi*), the **fall** of **portions** of **light** into the **World of Darkness** (the **realm** of **Ur**, the **dark** **cosmos**), the **creation** of the **material** **world** by a **demiurgic** **figure** (the **Seven** and the **Twelve**, the **planetary** **archons**), and the **call** of the **soul** to **return** through the **watchtowers** of the **archons** to the **Light**. The **myth** is **structurally** **isomorphic** with the **Valentinian** and **Sethian** **systems** of the **Nag Hammadi** **library** (see [[05-Nag-Hammadi-Texts-Real-Gnostic-Library]]); the **two** are **not** the **same** **tradition** but **share** the **deep** **structure**.

## The Practice — Baptism and the Lightworld

The **central** **practice** of the Mandaeans is **baptism** (*masbuta*), performed in **living** **water** (**the** **river**, **the** **mandi**), **repeated** **throughout** **life** and at **death** (*masiqta*, the "**ascent**", the **baptism** of the **dead**). The **baptism** is **not** a **one-time** **rite** of **initiation** (as in **Christianity**); the **baptism** is a **continuous** **purification** and **refreshment** of the **light** **spark** in the **soul**, a **return** to the **Lightworld** **anticipated** in **ritual**.

The **priestly** **equipment** is **symbolic**: the **drabsha** (the **banner**, a **cross-shaped** **pole** with a **white** **cloth**, **not** a **Christian** **cross** but a **Gnostic** **emblem** of the **Lightworld**), the **mishqa** (the **cup**), the **skandola** (the **signet** **ring**). The **ritual** **language** is **Mandaic**; the **priests** (the **Nasoraeans**) **transmit** the **texts** in an **unbroken** **chain** from **antiquity**. The **practice** is a **real** **ethnographic** **fact**, **documented** by **Drower** (1937) and **Buckley** (2002), **not** a **reconstruction** from **fragments**.

## The Cosmology — Light, Darkness, and the Soul

The **Mandaean** **cosmology** is a **classic** **Gnostic** **dualism**, **softened** by the **doctrine** of the **Great Life** as the **source** **above** the **dualism**. The **soul** (*nishimta*) is a **portion** of **light** **trapped** in the **body**; the **body** is the **garment** of the **darkness**; the **goal** is the **ascent** (*'urasha*) through the **matarata** (the **watchtowers** / **purgatories**) **guarded** by the **archons**, **aided** by the **uthras** (the **light** **beings** / **angels**) and the **baptism**.

The **cosmology** is **structurally** **parallel** to the **Neoplatonic** **descent** and **ascent** of the **soul** (see [[11-NeoPlatonism-Theurgy/02-Plotinus-Enneads-One-Mind-Soul]]) and to the **Hermetic** **doctrine** of the **soul's** **return** (see [[03-Poimandres-Vision-Of-Mind]]). The **Mandaean** **map** is **more** **mythic** and **less** **philosophical**, but the **trajectory** — **fall** into **matter**, **recognition**, **ascent** — is the **same** **curve**. The **tradition** is the **proof** that **Gnosticism** was a **living** **way**, not a **library** of **codex** **fragments**.

## The Modern Reception

The **Mandaeans** were **first** **described** to the **West** by the **missionaries** and **orientalists** of the **19th c.** (Nicholas Siouffi, 1880). The **definitive** **scholarship** is **E. S. Drower** (1937, 1959, 1960) and **Kurt Rudolph** (1960-61, English 1983). The **recent** **work** of **Jorunn Buckley** (2002) and **Häberl / McGrath** (2015) has **revived** the **field** and **produced** **critical** **texts** of the **Book of John** (*Sidra d-Yahia*).

The **Mandaeans** are a **critical** **resource** for the **esoteric** **library** because they **close** the **gap** between the **Hermetic** / **Gnostic** **texts** of **antiquity** and a **practiced** **tradition** **today**. They are **not** a **"survival"** **curiosity**; they are the **continuous** **witness** that the **Gnostic** **path** — **knowledge**, **baptism**, **ascent** — was a **real** **way** of **life**. The **student** should **read** **Drower** (1937) for the **people**, **Lidzbarski** (1925) for the **text**, and **Buckley** (2002) for the **modern** **community**.
