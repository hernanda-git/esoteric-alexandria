# The Corpus Hermeticum — The Foundational Texts

## The Corpus

The **Corpus Hermeticum** is a collection of **17 Greek treatises** attributed to **Hermes Trismegistus** ("thrice-greatest"). The texts were composed in **Egypt** in the **1st-3rd centuries CE**, in a cultural environment where Greek philosophy (especially Plato and the Stoics), Egyptian priestly religion, and Jewish wisdom traditions (especially Philo of Alexandria) were in dialogue.

The Corpus was **lost** to the Latin West for 1400 years. In 1460, the Italian humanist **Leonardo da Pistoia** brought a Greek manuscript from Macedonia to Florence. **Marsilio Ficino** translated the Corpus into Latin in 1463, just before his more famous translation of Plato. The publication caused a **renaissance** in the Western esoteric tradition.

The Greek title is **Ποίμανδρος** (Poimandres) for the first treatise, and the rest are titled by the content (e.g., "On the Mind", "On the Common Mind", "The Cup of Hermes", etc.). The full collection in the standard edition (Nock & Festugière, 1945-1954):

1. **Poimandres** — the vision of the Mind
2. **The General Treatise** (or "On the Mind to Hermes")
3. **The Sacred Sermon** (or "On the Mind")
4. **The Cup, or the Monad**
5. **That the Greatest Evil Among Men is Ignorance of God**
6. **That God is Hidden and Yet Manifest**
7. **That the Greatest of Evils is Ignorance of God** (duplicate of #5? or a different text?)
8. **That Nothing in the Sensible World Perishes**
9. **On Thought and Sense**
10. **The Key**
11. **The Mind to Hermes**
12. **On the Common Mind to Tat**
13. **The Secret Sermon on the Mountain**
14. **The Letter of Hermes to Asclepius**
15. **The Definitions of Asclepius to the King**
16. **That the One and the Same is at Rest and Moves**
17. **On the Fleshly Body**

Plus the **Asclepius** (a separate treatise surviving only in Latin, attributed to Hermes through his disciple Asclepius), and the **Latin Asclepius** is more famous than the Greek *logos telios*.

## The Myth of Hermes Trismegistus

The **Hermes Trismegistus** figure is a syncretism of:

- **Thoth** (Egyptian god of wisdom, writing, magic, the ibis-headed)
- **Hermes** (Greek god of wisdom, writing, magic, the messenger)
- **Moses** (in some traditions, Hermes is identified with Moses)
- **Enoch** (in the Jewish-Christian tradition, Enoch is identified with Hermes)
- **A legendary Egyptian priest-king** who lived in the deepest antiquity

The name "**thrice-greatest**" (trismegistos) is the Greek translation of the Egyptian epithet of Thoth ("the very great"). The figure is presented in the tradition as a **pristine revealer** — a culture-hero who gave humanity the arts, the sciences, the laws, the philosophy, the religion.

The Corpus opens with a **prayer** of Hermes to "the Father of the Universe" — the prayer is remarkable for being **not** mythological (no gods, no gods' loves, no gods' wars) but **philosophical-prayerful** (a direct address to the highest principle).

## The Core Teachings

The Corpus teaches:

### 1. The One

The first principle is **The One** (ἕν, *hen*) — the singular, the indivisible, the source. The One is not a god in the mythological sense; it is the **principle of unity** itself, beyond all attributes, beyond all names. The One **is**.

> "God is the One, and the One is God, and the One is in all things, and the One is all things." (CH XI)

### 2. The Mind (Nous)

The One **emanates** the **Mind** (νοῦς, *nous*). The Mind is the first thing that exists, the **first thought** of the One. The Mind is the **instrument** of creation — the One creates through the Mind.

### 3. The Word (Logos)

The Mind **emanates** the **Word** (λόγος, *logos*). The Word is the **active expression** of the Mind, the creative utterance, the **instrument of making**.

### 4. The World-Soul (Anima Mundi)

The Word **fills** the world with the **World-Soul** (ψυχὴ τοῦ κόσμου, *psyche tou kosmou*). The World-Soul is the **living presence** of the divine in the material world. The cosmos is **alive** in a literal sense, not metaphorically.

### 5. The Human

The human is the **microcosm** — the **small world** that contains all the principles. The human has the **Mind** (from the One), the **Word** (from the Mind), the **Soul** (from the World-Soul), and the **Body** (from matter). The human is the **knot** of the cosmos.

### 6. The Return

The human's task is to **return** to the divine. The return is by **gnosis** (γνῶσις, *gnosis* — knowledge, acquaintance). Gnosis is not intellectual knowledge; it is the **direct acquaintance** with the divine, the **taste of the One**. The gnostic is the one who **knows** in this sense.

### 7. The Three Lives

The human is invited to choose among **three lives** (CH I, the *Poimandres* vision):

- The **first life** is the life of the body — the ordinary, the material, the animal
- The **second life** is the life of the mind — the philosophical, the contemplative
- The **third life** is the life of the **noetic** (the Mind) — the gnostic, the perfect, the **deified**

The third life is the goal. It is achieved by **re-birth** (*palingenesia* — the second birth). The re-birth is a **transformative experience** in which the practitioner **becomes** the Mind itself, **becomes** the divine.

## The Modern Reception

The reception of the Corpus in the Renaissance is **one of the great stories** in the history of Western thought:

- **Marsilio Ficino** (1433-1499) translated the Corpus (1463) and used it as the philosophical foundation of his synthesis of Platonism, Christianity, and the Hermetic tradition
- **Giordano Bruno** (1548-1600) used the Corpus as the foundation of his **infinity of worlds** cosmology
- **Isaac Casaubon** (1559-1614) demonstrated in 1614 that the Corpus was **not** the work of a contemporary of Moses (as the tradition had claimed) but a Greek text of the 1st-3rd c. CE. The dating ended the prisca theologia claim but did **not** end the influence of the texts
- **The Golden Dawn** (1888-1903) used the Corpus extensively
- **The Thelemic tradition** (Crowley) re-read the Corpus as the foundation of the new Aeon
- **The modern Hermetic Order** (the **Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn**, **Theurgia**, **O.T.O.**) all draw on the Corpus

The Corpus is **alive** in the modern Western esoteric tradition in a way that no other pre-Christian text is. It is the **founding text** of the Western esoteric tradition as a continuous movement.

## The Practice

A modern practice of the Corpus Hermeticum requires:

1. **Read the Corpus** — Copenhaver's 1992 translation is the modern scholarly standard; the Salaman translation is more readable
2. **Practice the meditation** — the *Poimandres* vision (CH I) is the most explicit meditative text; it can be used as the basis for a contemplative practice
3. **Practice the discipline** — the Hermetic discipline is **gnosis** — the direct acquaintance with the divine. The discipline includes:
   - **Silence** (the hermetic tradition values silence as the ground of speech)
   - **Solitude** (the practitioner makes regular retreats)
   - **Contemplation** (the practitioner sits with the principles)
   - **Generosity** (the practitioner shares what they have received)
4. **Find a tradition** — the Hermetic tradition has many lineages (the modern Theurgic Society, the modern Golden Dawn offshoots, the modern Thelemic tradition)

The library's category 10 treats the Corpus as the **master text** of the Western esoteric tradition. Every other text in the tradition is downstream of the Corpus.
