# From Alchemy to Chemistry — The Lineage

## The Claim

> **Modern chemistry is the legitimate descendant of alchemy. The alchemists were the first chemists, and the language of the lab is still the language of the alchemists.**

This is the **standard view** in the history of science. The discontinuity between alchemy and chemistry is **less sharp** than the 19th-century founders of modern chemistry wanted to claim. Lavoisier, Dalton, and Mendeleev all worked in the alchemical lineage.

## The Alchemists Who Made Modern Chemistry

| Alchemist | Date | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Jabir ibn Hayyan** (Geber) | 8-9th c. | Mercury-sulfur theory, balance (stoichiometry), systematic distillation |
| **al-Razi** (Rhazes) | 9-10th c. | Classification of substances, *Kitab al-Asrar* (Book of Secrets) is the first systematic materia medica |
| **Pseudo-Geber** (*Summa Perfectionis*) | 13th c. | The sulfur-mercury theory systematized for the Latin West |
| **Paracelsus** | 16th c. | Iatrochemistry (medicine as chemistry), the **tria prima** (salt, sulfur, mercury), the doctrine of signatures |
| **Libavius** | 1597 | *Alchymia* — the first textbook of chemistry as a distinct discipline |
| **Van Helmont** | 17th c. | The concept of "gas" (he coined the word), willow-tree experiment (the first quantitative biology experiment) |
| **Boyle** | 1661 | *The Sceptical Chymist* — the modern concept of an element, the rejection of Aristotelian elements |
| **Newton** | 17-18th c. | ~10 million words of alchemical manuscripts (more than his physics and math combined); the **action at a distance** in his physics is influenced by the alchemical notion of sympathy |
| **Stahl** | 18th c. | Phlogiston theory (later disproved) — the last great alchemical theory to dominate the field |
| **Lavoisier** | 1789 | *Traité Élémentaire de Chimie* — the oxygen theory of combustion, the conservation of mass, the modern naming |
| **Dalton** | 1808 | Atomic theory — the first modern theory of chemical combination |
| **Mendeleev** | 1869 | The periodic table — the systematic organization of the elements |

## The Continuity

Three threads of continuity can be traced from Jabir to the modern lab:

### 1. Operations

Modern lab operations are **the same operations** as the alchemical lab:

| Alchemical | Modern |
|---|---|
| Distillatio | Distillation |
| Sublimatio | Sublimation |
| Calcinatio | Calcination |
| Solutio | Dissolution |
| Coagulatio | Crystallization, precipitation |
| Putrefactio | Decomposition, fermentation |
| Fermentatio | Catalysis |

The names survive in modern chemistry. The lab has changed; the operations have not.

### 2. Apparatus

The **alembbic** (from Arabic *al-anbiq*, after the Greek *ambix*) is still the standard distillation apparatus. The **athanor** (the alchemical furnace) is the ancestor of the **muffle furnace** and the **Bunsen burner**. The **kerotakis** (a sublimation apparatus) is the ancestor of the **sublimation chamber**.

### 3. Substances

Many modern chemical substances were first isolated by alchemists:

- **Alcohol** (the word itself is Arabic, *al-kohl*, originally meaning "the fine powder" used as eye-makeup, then the concentrated spirit)
- **Aqua regia** (the only acid that dissolves gold) — known to Jabir
- **Caustic soda** (sodium hydroxide) — Jabir
- **Sulfuric acid** (oil of vitriol) — al-Razi, 10th c.
- **Nitric acid** (aqua fortis) — Pseudo-Geber, 13th c.
- **Aqua fortis / aqua regia** — Pseudo-Geber

The alchemists were **the first systematic discoverers of the mineral acids**.

## The Real Discontinuity

The discontinuity is **not** in operations or apparatus or substances. The discontinuity is in **theory**.

The alchemists had the mercury-sulfur theory (or the sulfur-mercury-salt theory after Paracelsus). The modern chemists replaced it with the **elementary** theory (an element is a substance that cannot be decomposed by chemical means) and the **atomic** theory (matter is composed of indivisible atoms combining in fixed ratios).

The shift happened in the period 1660-1810. Boyle's *Sceptical Chymist* (1661) is the early marker; Lavoisier's *Traité* (1789) is the late marker. The shift was **slow, contested, and partial**. Newton was both an alchemist and a modern physicist, and the two strands of his thought are not as separated as the textbooks suggest.

## The Modern Note

The continuity is now part of the **standard history of science**. William Newman's *Atoms and Alchemy* (2000) is the definitive modern account. The Princeton **Chymistry of Isaac Newton** project (https://webapp.library.princeton.edu/chem/) has digitized Newton's alchemical manuscripts.

The implication for the library:

- The alchemists were not wrong about the **importance** of chemistry
- The alchemists were wrong about **specific theories** (mercury-sulfur as the composition of metals, phlogiston as the principle of combustion)
- The alchemists were right about **the operations** and the **phenomena** they observed
- The inner-psychological reading of alchemy is **not scientific**, but it is **not anti-scientific** either — it is a different mode of inquiry

The library treats modern chemistry as the **outer layer** of the alchemical tradition, and the psychological / spiritual alchemy as the **inner layer**. Both are valid; they are not the same inquiry.

## The Modern Practice

A modern "alchemist" can engage in two legitimate practices:

1. **The chemical practice** — study chemistry seriously. Understand the periodic table, the molecular orbital theory, the thermodynamics of reactions. The alchemists' operations are the same operations, with much better equipment and much better theory.
2. **The symbolic practice** — engage with the alchemical symbols (lead, dragon, nigredo, philosopher's stone) as **psychological symbols**. Read Jung's *Mysterium Coniunctionis* and Marie-Louise von Franz's commentaries.

These are **not** the same practice. But they are both legitimate, and a serious student of the tradition can do both.
