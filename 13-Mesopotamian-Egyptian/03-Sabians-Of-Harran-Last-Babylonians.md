# The Sabians of Harran — The Last Babylonians

## The City

**Harran** (تل حران, ancient Carrhae) is a city in **upper Mesopotamia** (modern southeastern Turkey, near the Syrian border). The city is one of the **oldest continuously inhabited** sites in the world, founded by the Sumerians (c. 2000 BCE) and continuously occupied through the Hellenistic, Roman, Persian, Islamic, Ottoman, and modern periods.

The city is famous in the history of religion as the **last stronghold** of the **pre-Islamic Mesopotamian religion** — the **Sabians**. The Sabians survived in Harran from pre-Islamic antiquity until the **11th century CE**, when they were destroyed by the Seljuk Turks.

The Sabians are one of the **most mysterious** of the religious communities of late antiquity. They are mentioned in the Quran (Sura 2:62, 5:69, 22:17) as one of the **"people of the book"** (along with the Jews, the Christians, and the Zoroastrians) — a protected religious category in the early Islamic empire. The Sabians **claimed** this protection by **masquerading** as a Gnostic-Christian community that venerated John the Baptist.

## The Two Sabians

Modern scholarship distinguishes **two groups** of Sabians:

1. **The Mesopotamian Sabians** — the **Harranians**, the actual pre-Islamic Babylonian star-worshippers. They are the **real** Sabians. They survived in Harran until the 11th century. They practiced **astrolatry** (the worship of the planets and stars) and **theurgy** (the ritual invocation of the planetary intelligences).

2. **The Mandaeans** — the **Mandaean Sabians**, the surviving Gnostic community of southern Iraq. The Mandaeans are **not** the Harranian Sabians, but they were **identified** with the Sabians by medieval Muslim scholars. The Mandaeans survive to the present day (~70,000 members) and are the **only continuous** Gnostic tradition in the world.

The **Picatrix** author (attributed to al-Majriti, 10th c.) was a Sabian of Harran. The Sabian influence on the Islamic esoteric tradition (the *'ilm al-huruf*, the planetary talismans, the Picatrix) is **profound**.

## The Theology

The Harranian Sabian theology can be reconstructed from the **Picatrix**, the **Fihrist** of Ibn al-Nadim (10th c. CE), and the **Damascius** account of the Harranian philosopher Marinus (5th-6th c. CE):

1. **The highest god** is the **One**, the "first principle" (the Neoplatonic formulation)
2. The **seven planetary gods** are the **secondary deities**, each ruling a sphere
3. The **angels** (mala'ika) are the **intermediaries** between the planetary gods and humans
4. **Theurgy** (the ritual invocation of the gods) is the **central practice**
5. **Hermes Trismegistus** is the **patron saint** of the tradition, identified with the **prophet Idris** (in the Islamic tradition, Idris is the prophet who taught humanity writing, the sciences, the arts)
6. **Agathodaimon** is the **second patron**, the "good spirit" (the **Agathodaimon** figure is the Egyptian-Greek equivalent of the "good genius" of the tradition)
7. **Theurgy** is the **highest practice** — the ritual that brings the soul into contact with the divine

The Harranian theology is **Neoplatonic in structure** (the emanation from the One, the hierarchy of beings) but **Mesopotamian in content** (the planetary gods, the angelic intermediaries, the ritual invocation). The combination is the **distinctive feature** of the Sabian tradition.

## The Picatrix as Sabian Text

The **Picatrix** is **explicitly attributed** to the Harranian Sabian **Maslama al-Majriti** (though modern scholarship dates the work to the **Toledan** compilation movement, 10th-11th c. CE). The Picatrix is the **most detailed** surviving document of the Harranian theurgic practice.

The Picatrix is a **practical manual** of the Harranian theurgy. The 232 talismans in Book II are the **standard repertoire** of the Sabian tradition. The 28 lunar mansions in Book III are the **astrological foundation**. The operations in Book IV are the **ritual protocols**.

The Picatrix is the **most important** Sabian document, and the most important document of the pre-Islamic Babylonian tradition in the Islamic world.

## The Sabian Influence on the Islamic Esoteric Tradition

The Sabian influence on the **'ilm al-huruf** tradition is **major**. Al-Buni, the author of the **Shams al-Ma'arif**, explicitly cites the **Sabian** tradition as the source of much of his material. The Sabian influence is visible in:

- The **planetary attributions** of the Arabic letters (the *huruf al-falakiyya*)
- The **magic squares** (the *wafq*) — the Sabian tradition has a **complete** system of planetary magic squares
- The **divine name** system (the 99 names of God, the *al-asma al-husna*) — the Sabian tradition gives the **planetary attributions** of the divine names
- The **theurgic ritual** — the *'azima* of the 'ilm al-huruf tradition is a **Harranian** practice
- The **love potions** and the **talismans** — the Picatrix repertoire

The Sabian tradition is the **link** between the pre-Islamic Mesopotamian tradition (the Enuma Anu Enlil) and the Islamic esoteric tradition (the *'ilm al-huruf*, the planetary talismans, the grimoire tradition). Without the Sabians, the Islamic esoteric tradition would be **substantially different**.

## The Destruction of the Sabians

The Harranian Sabians were **destroyed** in the **11th century CE** by the Seljuk Turks. The events:

1. The Seljuk vizier **Nizam al-Mulk** (1018-1092) was hostile to the Sabians
2. The Seljuk general **Qutb al-Din Mawdud** destroyed the Sabian temple in Harran in **1082**
3. The **last** known Sabian priest was **Mundhir al-Tall al-Qura'i**, who was executed in the 11th c.
4. The Sabian community was **dispersed**, with some members fleeing to Egypt and Syria

The destruction of the Sabians in 1082 is one of the **great losses** of the esoteric tradition. The Sabian community preserved the **pre-Islamic Mesopotamian** tradition for over 2,000 years; after 1082, only the **written texts** (preserved in the Arabic, the Latin, and the Hebrew translations) survived.

## The Modern Note

The Sabian tradition is **not extinct**. The Mandaeans (the Mandaean Sabians) survive in southern Iraq and Iran, and in the diaspora (~70,000 members). The Mandaean tradition preserves the **Sabian practice** in a continuous line from antiquity to the present.

The Harranian Sabian tradition is documented in the **Picatrix**, the **Fihrist**, the **Damascius** account, and the **medieval Arabic astrological texts**. The Harranian tradition is the **link** to the pre-Islamic Mesopotamian tradition, and the **source** of much of the Islamic and Western esoteric tradition.

The library's category 13 treats the Sabians as the **continuity** between the Babylonian, the Greek, the Islamic, and the Western esoteric traditions. The Sabians are the **bridge** people of the Western esoteric history.
