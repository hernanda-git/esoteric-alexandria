# Enuma Anu Enlil — Babylonian Celestial Divination

## The Text

The **Enuma Anu Enlil** (𒀭𒉣𒈗𒀭𒂗𒆤, "When the gods Anu and Enlil") is the **master text** of Babylonian celestial divination. The text is preserved in **70 cuneiform tablets** (each tablet has 4 sides, giving 280 sides) containing **~7,000 omens**.

The text was composed over a **long period** — from the Old Babylonian period (c. 1900 BCE, the reign of Hammurabi) to the Neo-Babylonian period (c. 540 BCE, the fall of Babylon to Cyrus). The oldest omens are from the **Old Babylonian** period; the text was edited and expanded in the **Kassite** and **Neo-Assyrian** periods; the final form was standardized in the **Neo-Babylonian** period.

The text is the **most extensive** pre-modern collection of astrological omens. It is the **foundation** of the entire Mesopotamian astrological tradition, and through the Persian, Greek, and Arabic transmission, the **ancestor** of the Western astrological tradition.

## The Structure

The 70 tablets are organized into **4 sections** by the type of omen:

| Section | Tablets | Content | Sample Omen |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I. The Moon** | Tablets 1-13 | Lunar omens — eclipses, first appearance, halos | "If the moon is eclipsed in the month of Nisan, the king will die; if the eclipse is partial, the king's army will be defeated." |
| **II. The Sun** | Tablets 14-26 | Solar omens — eclipses, parhelia, sun's color | "If the sun is eclipsed in the morning watch, the king of Akkad will be besieged." |
| **III. The Weather** | Tablets 27-39 | Meteorological omens — thunder, lightning, rain, earthquakes | "If it thunders in the month of Kislimu, the crops will fail." |
| **IV. The Planets and Stars** | Tablets 40-70 | Planetary omens — Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Venus, Mercury, Sirius | "If Venus appears in the morning during the month of Abu, there will be a war; if Venus is dim, the king will be safe." |

The structure is **comprehensive**. The omen covers every visible celestial phenomenon, every meteorological event, and every major natural occurrence (earthquakes, comets, fires).

## The Logic of the Omen

A Babylonian omen has the form:

> **Protasis**: "If [celestial phenomenon X]"
> **Apodosis**: "then [terrestrial event Y]"

The protasis is the **observation**; the apodosis is the **prediction**. The link between the two is **not** scientific (in our modern sense); it is **sympathetic** — the celestial event and the terrestrial event are **woven into the same fabric** of the cosmos.

The Babylonian understanding of the omen is **non-causal**. The celestial event does **not cause** the terrestrial event; the celestial event **signals** the terrestrial event. The signal is a **manifestation of the will of the gods**, the **Anunnaki**, the **great gods** who rule the cosmos.

The Babylonian omen tradition is the **ancestor** of the entire Western astrological tradition. The form "If [celestial] then [terrestrial]" is the form of the astrological horoscope. The Babylonian collection of omens became the **Hellenistic** astrological tradition (Berossos, 3rd c. BCE) and from there the **Western** and **Arabic** astrological traditions.

## The Observation Program

The Enuma Anu Enlil was **not just a text**. It was the **manual** for a **state-funded observation program**. The Neo-Assyrian empire (911-609 BCE) maintained a **roster of astronomers** (*ṭupšar Enuma Anu Enlil*, "scribes of Enuma Anu Enlil") who observed the sky and recorded the omens. The observations were sent to the king as **reports** (the **Astronomical Diaries**, preserved on clay tablets in the British Museum).

The observation program was **continuous** for over 600 years (the **Babylonian Astronomical Diaries** cover 652-61 BCE, with some gaps). This is the **longest continuous scientific program** in the history of the world. The data from the Diaries is still used by modern astronomers (the **Greek astronomical records** from the 4th c. BCE onwards are based on the Babylonian data).

## The Interpretation

The interpretation of an omen was a **professional activity**. The *ṭupšar Enuma Anu Enlil* (the "scribe") had the following tasks:

1. **Observe** the sky at the right time
2. **Record** the observation in the proper form
3. **Match** the observation to the relevant omen
4. **Interpret** the omen in the current context (the king, the country, the time of year)
5. **Report** the interpretation to the king
6. **Recommend** the appropriate **namburbi** (the apotropaic ritual to avert the predicted evil)

The **namburbi** ritual is the **preventive** counterpart of the omen. The omen predicts; the namburbi averts. The namburbi is a complex ritual involving the substitution of a sacrificial animal (the *šurpu* and *maqlû* are the major ritual texts), the recitation of specific prayers, the construction of amulets.

## The Modern Note

The Enuma Anu Enlil is **the most important** pre-modern astronomical/astrological text. It is the **foundation** of:

- The **Babylonian astronomical tradition** (which is the **modern** scientific tradition — the 24-hour day, the 360° circle, the zodiac, the 12-month calendar all come from Babylon)
- The **Hellenistic astrological tradition** (Berossos, 3rd c. BCE; the *Astronomica* of Manilius, 1st c. CE; Ptolemy's *Tetrabiblos*, 2nd c. CE)
- The **Arabic astrological tradition** (Sahl ibn Bishr, 9th c. CE; Albumasar, 9th c. CE; the Picatrix, 10th c. CE)
- The **Western astrological tradition** (Ficino, Pico, Agrippa, the modern tradition)

The Enuma Anu Enlil is the **single most important** pre-modern celestial text, by any measure. It is the **source** of the Western astrological vocabulary, the Western astronomical conventions, and the Western mantic tradition.

## The Library's Position

The library's category 13 treats the Enuma Anu Enlil as the **deep ancestor** of the entire celestial-omen tradition. The text is **not** the same as the Western astrology, but it is the **source** of the Western astrology. The reader who wants to understand the **deep structure** of the astrological tradition must read the Enuma Anu Enlil.

The text is **not** in print as a complete English translation (the 70 tablets are partially translated in Reiner & Pingree's multi-volume *Babylonian Planetary Omens*). The library's category 13 documents the structure, the logic, the influence, and the modern relevance.
