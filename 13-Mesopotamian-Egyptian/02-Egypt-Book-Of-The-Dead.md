# The Egyptian Book of the Dead

## The Text

The **Book of the Dead** (Egyptian *rw nw prt m hrw*, "the chapters of coming forth by day") is the **most copied** of the ancient Egyptian funerary texts. The text is preserved on **hundreds of papyri** from the New Kingdom (c. 1550 BCE) to the Roman period (1st c. CE). The most famous is the **Papyrus of Ani** (c. 1250 BCE, now in the British Museum).

The Book of the Dead is **not a single text** but a **collection of 192 spells** (called "chapters") of varying lengths, designed to be **selected and copied** into a funerary papyrus for a specific individual. The selection depends on the wealth of the deceased, the local tradition, and the priest's choice.

The spells are **spells** in the literal sense — they are designed to be **recited** (or "activated") by the deceased in the afterlife. The **spells activate forces** in the next world; without the correct spell, the deceased is vulnerable. The Book of the Dead is a **portable afterlife manual**.

## The Lineage — From Pyramid Texts to Coffin Texts to Book of the Dead

The Egyptian funerary tradition has a **continuous development** over 2,500 years:

| Period | Text | Format | Who |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Old Kingdom** (c. 2400-2200 BCE) | **Pyramid Texts** | Carved on the walls of the pyramids | Only the king |
| **Middle Kingdom** (c. 2100-1800 BCE) | **Coffin Texts** | Painted on the inside of the coffins | The nobility |
| **New Kingdom** (c. 1550-1100 BCE) | **Book of the Dead** | Written on papyrus, placed in the tomb | The wealthy and the literate |
| **Late Period** (c. 750-300 BCE) | **The Amduat** ("That Which is in the Underworld") and other **underworld books** | Carved on tomb walls (especially the Valley of the Kings) | The royal tombs |
| **Ptolemaic-Roman** (c. 300 BCE - 200 CE) | **The Book of Breathings** and other demotic texts | Papyri | The educated class |

The tradition is **continuous** — the Coffin Texts are expansions of the Pyramid Texts; the Book of the Dead is a selection from the Coffin Texts; the Late Period underworld books are expansions of the Book of the Dead.

## The Spells — A Selection

The 192 spells are not all equally important. The most famous:

- **Spell 1** — the opening, the introduction of the deceased to Osiris
- **Spell 17** — the famous "Negative Confession" — the 42 declarations of innocence before the 42 assessor gods
- **Spell 22** — the opening of the mouth, the activation of the senses in the afterlife
- **Spell 30** — the heart scarab, the magical heart amulet
- **Spell 125** — the judgment of the dead before Osiris, the weighing of the heart against the feather of Ma'at
- **Spell 144** — the gates of the underworld, the "Seven Arits" (the seven gates, each guarded by a doorkeeper)
- **Spell 148** — the preservation of the body, the mummification
- **Spell 151** — the magical protection of the limbs
- **Spell 182** — the Book of the Dead, the "spell of becoming a falcon" (the deceased becomes Horus, can fly to the sky)

## The Negative Confession (Spell 125)

The **Negative Confession** is the most famous of the spells. The deceased stands before the 42 assessor gods (one for each of the 42 nomes, or administrative districts, of Egypt) and declares their innocence of 42 specific sins. The declarations are in the form:

> "Hail to you, O [name of assessor god], I have not done [sin]."

The 42 sins include:
- I have not stolen
- I have not killed
- I have not told lies
- I have not been angry without cause
- I have not been deaf to the words of truth
- I have not been a troublemaker
- I have not been jealous
- I have not been a gossip
- I have not been unfaithful
- I have not cursed the king
- I have not polluted the water
- I have not defrauded the poor
- I have not done any of the other 30 sins

The list is **comprehensive** — it covers the ethical life of the Egyptian. The declaration is **not** a boast; it is a **statement of fact**. The Egyptian who can truthfully make the declaration is **fit to enter the afterlife**. The Egyptian who cannot is **devoured by Ammit** (the "devourer", a hybrid creature with a crocodile head, a lion forepart, and a hippopotamus hindpart) and **ceases to exist**.

## The Weighing of the Heart

The most famous image from the Book of the Dead is the **weighing of the heart**. The scene:

1. The deceased is led by **Anubis** (the jackal-headed god of mummification) to the Hall of Ma'at
2. The deceased's **heart** (the seat of the conscience) is placed on a scale
3. The **feather of Ma'at** (the goddess of truth, order, justice) is placed on the other side
4. **Thoth** (the ibis-headed god of writing) records the result
5. **Ammit** waits beside the scale to devour the heart if it is heavier than the feather
6. If the heart is **light** (i.e., light with sin), the deceased is led by **Horus** to **Osiris** (the king of the dead) and judged fit to enter the afterlife
7. If the heart is **heavy**, the deceased is devoured and **ceases to exist**

The weighing of the heart is the **moral center** of the Egyptian tradition. The image is **extraordinarily** influential — it has been reproduced in thousands of books, articles, and modern media.

## The Book of the Dead and the Hermetic Tradition

The Book of the Dead is the **deep ancestor** of the Hermetic tradition (see [[10-Hermeticism-Gnosticism/01-Corpus-Hermeticum]]). The **Hermes Trismegistus** figure is partly derived from **Thoth** (the Egyptian god of writing and wisdom). The Poimandres vision (CH I) has many parallels with the Book of the Dead:

- The vision of the **cosmic order** (the Book of the Dead's "Road of the Sun")
- The **seven** regions of the underworld (parallels to the Hermetic seven governors)
- The **ascent** of the deceased (parallels to the Hermetic return of the soul)
- The **judgment** before Osiris (parallels to the Hermetic judgment of the soul)

The Renaissance Hermetists (Ficino, Pico, Bruno) saw the Book of the Dead as **the Egyptian equivalent** of the Corpus Hermeticum. The 19th-century Egyptology (Champollion, Budge, Lepsius) discovered the Book of the Dead and the Hermetic texts in their **original Egyptian** context, which complicated the Renaissance narrative but confirmed the deep relationship.

## The Modern Note

The Book of the Dead is **the most influential** of the ancient Egyptian texts. The Wallis Budge translation (1895) is the most-reproduced English version. The text has:

- **The popular culture** — the mummy, the weighing of the heart, the scarab, the canopic jars — all of these are from the Book of the Dead iconography
- **The academic study** — modern Egyptology (Jan Assmann, Erik Hornung, John Taylor) has produced sophisticated studies of the text
- **The esoteric reception** — the **Theosophical Society**, the **Golden Dawn**, the **modern Thelemic tradition** have all drawn on the Book of the Dead for the Egyptian portion of their tradition
- **The modern spirituality** — the "ancient Egypt" spiritual movement, the "Isis and Osiris" popular tradition, the New Age Egyptian revival

The library's category 13 treats the Book of the Dead as the **Egyptian counterpart** of the Enuma Anu Enlil. The two texts, in the same category, show the **Babylonian-Egyptian duality** that the Hermetic and the Western esoteric traditions inherit.

## The Practice

A modern practice of the Book of the Dead requires:

1. **Read the text** — Budge's translation is the most accessible; the modern critical editions (Faulkner, Allen) are more accurate
2. **Study the iconography** — the Book of the Dead is **vividly illustrated**; the images are a key part of the tradition
3. **Use the spells** — the spells can be used as **meditation seeds** (the modern practice)
4. **Find a tradition** — the modern Egyptian revival has many lineages (the modern **Temple of Isis**, the **Kemetic Orthodoxy**, the **Church of the Eternal Source**)

The library's category 13 is the **textual foundation**. The practice requires the tradition.
