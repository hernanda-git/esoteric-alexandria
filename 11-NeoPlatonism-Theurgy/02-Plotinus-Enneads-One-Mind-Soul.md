# Plotinus and the Three Hypostases

## The Man

**Plotinus** (Greek: Πλωτῖνος, Plōtinos; c. 204-270 CE) was born in **Lycopolis, Egypt**, possibly of Greek or Egyptian parents. He studied in **Alexandria** under **Ammonius Saccas** (c. 175-242 CE), the same teacher as **Origen of Alexandria** (the Christian) — a fact that has given rise to endless speculation about whether the two were aware of each other.

At age 40 (c. 244 CE) Plotinus joined the **expedition of Emperor Gordian III** against Persia, in order to learn the **Persian and Indian** philosophies. The expedition failed (Gordian was murdered in 244); Plotinus escaped to **Antioch** and eventually settled in **Rome** (c. 246 CE), where he taught until his death in 270.

Plotinus wrote in Greek, but his Greek was famously **defective** — his student **Porphyry** says he had grammatical errors and was unable to express himself clearly. Porphyry edited the treatises into the present **Enneads** and gave each a short Greek title.

The **Enneads** are 54 treatises, arranged in **6 groups of 9** (*ennea* = "nine") in the chronological order of Porphyry's own edition. The first 24 were written before Porphyry's arrival (c. 263); the rest, after.

## The Three Hypostases

Plotinus' central doctrine is the **three hypostases** (*hypostaseis* — "substances" or "subsistences") of being:

1. **The One** (*to Hen*) — the absolute, the first principle, beyond all being, beyond all thought, beyond all categories. The One is **not** a being, **not** a thought, **not** a cause in the ordinary sense. The One is **pure unity** that **precedes** all multiplicity
2. **Nous** (the Intellect) — the realm of **being** and **thought**, the eternal Forms (the **Platonic Ideas**), the *Living Being* of the *Timaeus*, the world of the gods of the intellect. Nous is the **image** of the One; Nous is what the One becomes when it "looks at itself" (the **intellectual emanation**)
3. **Soul** (*Psyche*) — the **rational** and **irrational** soul, the **intermediary** between Nous and the material world. Soul is the **image** of Nous; Soul is what Nous becomes when it "looks outward". Soul includes the **rational souls** (human, divine) and the **irrational souls** (animals, plants)

Each hypostasis is **eternal**; each **precedes** the next in the sense that the next **depends** on it. The relation is not **temporal** ("first" and "then") but **ontological** ("primary" and "derived"). The One is **always** generating Nous; Nous is **always** generating Soul. The generation is **continuous**, not a one-time event.

The material world is **not** a fourth hypostasis; the material world is the **image** of Soul. Matter is the **dark**, the **indefinite**, the **last image** of Soul. Matter is **not** evil (matter is the **product** of Soul), but matter is the **lowest** of the levels of being.

## The Emanation (Proodos)

The process of the generation of the hypostases is **emanation** (Greek *proodos*, "procession"). The emanation is **not** a one-time event; the emanation is **continuous**. The One **always** emanates Nous; Nous **always** emanates Soul; Soul **always** emanates matter.

The emanation is **necessary** — not in the sense of compulsion, but in the sense that **the nature of the One** is to generate. The One is **not** a being that **chooses** to generate; the One is **pure generative power**.

The emanation is **non-diminishing** — the One is **not** diminished by the emanation. The sun is not diminished by the light it generates. The One is **always** full; the One is **always** one.

The emanation is **image-like** — each hypostasis is an **image** of the previous. The image is **lesser** than the source. The image is **similar** to the source. The image is **distinct** from the source. The image is **what the source is** plus a **distance**.

The classic image (Ennead V.1, on the three primary hypostases) is **light**: the sun (the One) generates light (Nous) and the light illuminates the air (Soul), but the sun is not diminished, the light is less than the sun, and the air is less than the light. The image is **imprecise** (Plotinus himself says so), but it is **suggestive**.

## The Return (Epistrophe)

The process of the **return** of the soul to the One is **epistrophe** (Greek "turning back"). The return is the **central concern** of Plotinus' ethical teaching. The return is:

1. **The purification** (*katharsis*) — the **separation** of the soul from the body, the passions, the attachments. The soul is **purged** of the material
2. **The intellectual ascent** — the **intellection** of the Forms. The soul **contemplates** the Forms in Nous. The soul **sees** Nous by **being** Nous (the soul is the image of Nous; the soul can **know** Nous by **becoming** Nous)
3. **The mystical union** — the **direct contact** with the One. The soul **transcends** Nous, the soul **transcends** being, the soul **transcends** thought. The soul **unites** with the One. The union is **rare**. The union is **brief**. The union is **the goal**

Plotinus claims to have achieved the union **six times** in his life (Porphyry, *Life of Plotinus*, sec. 23). Porphyry himself achieved the union **once**, in his 68th year (sec. 23). The achievement is the **goal** of the philosophical life.

## The Practice of the Philosophical Life

Plotinus' ethics is **the practice of the philosophical life**. The philosophical life is the **purification** of the soul. The philosophical life is the **return** to the One.

The practice has **three main elements**:

1. **The moral virtues** (*politikai aretai*) — the civic virtues, the foundation of the philosophical life. The **practical** virtues of justice, courage, temperance, and prudence
2. **The purificatory virtues** (*kathartikai aretai*) — the **purification** of the soul from the body, the passions, the attachments. The soul **separates** from the material
3. **The contemplative virtues** (*theoretikai aretai*) — the **intellection** of the Forms. The soul **contemplates** Nous. The soul **becomes** Nous

The progression is **upward**. The moral virtues are the **beginning**; the contemplative virtues are the **end**. The end is the **mystical union** with the One.

The progression is **not** automatic. The progression requires **discipline**, **practice**, **ascent**, **a guide** (a teacher in the **lineage**), and the **grace** of the god. The discipline is **lifelong**. The progress is **slow**. The end is **rare**.

## The Place of Plotinus in the Tradition

Plotinus is the **founder** of Neoplatonism, the **bridge** between the Platonic tradition and the Late Antique religious revival. Plotinus' Enneads are the **source** of:

1. **Porphyry's** *Sentences* and his biography of Plotinus
2. **Iamblichus'** *De Mysteriis* and his defense of theurgy
3. **Proclus'** *Elements of Theology* and the systematic Neoplatonism
4. **Pseudo-Dionysius'** *Divine Names* and *Mystical Theology* (5th-6th c., the synthesis of Neoplatonism and Christianity)
5. **Boethius'** *Consolation of Philosophy* (6th c., the bridge to the Latin Middle Ages)
6. **Augustine's** *Confessions* and *De Trinitate* (the synthesis of Neoplatonism and Latin Christianity)
7. **The Latin** translation of *Proclus' Elements of Theology* by **William of Moerbeke** (1268, the foundation of the medieval scholastic reception of Neoplatonism)
8. **The Renaissance** Platonic revival (Marsilio Ficino's 1492 translation of the *Enneads*, the founding text of the Florentine Academy)
9. **The 19th-century** German Idealism (Schelling, Hegel, the Romantic tradition)
10. **The 20th-century** depth psychology (Jung, who read the Enneads in the MacKenna translation)

Plotinus is **the most influential** philosopher of the Western tradition after Plato and Aristotle. The modern academy treats Plotinus as the **summit** of the ancient metaphysics. The Western esoteric tradition treats Plotinus as the **highest** expression of the philosophical life.
