# Proclus — The Systematization of Neoplatonism

## The Man

**Proclus Lycaeus** (Greek: Πρόκλος ὁ Διάδοχος, "Proclus the Successor", 412-485 CE) was the second-to-last **scholarch** (head) of the **Academy of Athens** — the Platonic school that had been founded by Plato in 387 BCE and had been **continuously operating** for almost 900 years by the time of Proclus.

Proclus was born in **Constantinople** to a wealthy family, raised in **Xanthus** (Lycia), educated in **Alexandria**, and came to **Athens** as a young man to study at the Academy. He was a student of **Plutarch of Athens** (the Neoplatonist, not the biographer; 350-430 CE) and of **Syrianus** (c. 380-450 CE), and became the **scholarch** in **437 CE** at age 25.

Proclus was the **most systematic** of the Neoplatonists. He **organized** the dispersed doctrines of Plotinus, Iamblichus, and the Chaldean Oracles into a **single coherent system**. He was the **peak** of the Athenian school; after his death in 485, his successor **Marinus of Neapolis** continued the school until **529 CE**, when Emperor **Justinian I** closed the school (along with the other pagan schools) and ended the **9th-century tradition** of the Platonic Academy.

## The Central Doctrine — The Three Levels and the Henads

Proclus' central innovation is the **systematic introduction** of the **Henads** (*henades*, "the ones") as the **first level of being**. Proclus divides reality into **three main levels**:

1. **The Unparticipated** (*amethekton*) — the **first principle**, the **source** that is **not shared** with anything else. The One (*to Hen*) is the unparticipated
2. **The Participated** (*methecta*) — the **universal** that is **shared** with **many** participants. The Forms (the Platonic Ideas) are participated
3. **The Participants** (*methexis*) — the **particulars** that **share** in the participated. The individual things are the participants

Each level has a **specific structure**:

- **The One** — the absolute first, beyond all categories
- **The Henads** — the **divine gods** in their **unity**, the **monads** of each godhead. Each god (Zeus, Athena, Apollo, etc.) has a **Henad** that is the **unity** of the god's nature. The Henads are **above** the Forms
- **The Intelligible-Intellective gods** (*Noera*) — the **divine intellects**, the **paradigmatic gods** (the **Forms as gods**)
- **The Intellectual gods** (*Noetoi*) — the **gods of pure thought** (the **gods of the intellect** as such)
- **The Demiurgic / Hypercosmic gods** — the **gods of creation**, the **maker gods**
- **The Psychic / Encosmic gods** — the **gods within the cosmos** (the **planetary gods**, the **angels**, the **heroes**, the **daemones**)
- **The Material / Encosmic gods** — the **gods of matter**

The structure is **hierarchical**: each level is **derived from** the previous, and each level is **present in** the previous. The structure is **systematic**: every level has the **same** internal structure (the unparticipated, the participated, the participants).

## The Elements of Theology

The **Stoicheiōsis Theologikē** (Latin: *Elementatio Theologica*, "Elements of Theology") is Proclus' **most systematic work** — 211 propositions in 6 sections, each proposition with a proof and a brief demonstration. The work is the **systematic Neoplatonic theology** in its **mature form**.

The 6 sections and the proposition ranges:

1. **Section 1: The One** (propositions 1-39) — the first principle, the unparticipated, the beyond-being
2. **Section 2: The Henads and the Gods** (propositions 40-91) — the divine gods, the participation in the first
3. **Section 3: The Forms and the Intelligible-Intellective** (propositions 92-122) — the Forms, the intelligible intellect
4. **Section 4: The Soul and the Intellect** (propositions 123-180) — the soul, the intellect, the intermediary levels
5. **Section 5: The Body and the Material World** (propositions 181-200) — the body, the matter, the physical
6. **Section 6: The Consequence of the Levels** (propositions 201-211) — the unity of the system, the **circular** return of all things to the first

The work is **axiomatic** — each proposition **follows** from the previous. The work is **deductive** — the conclusions are **derived** from the premises. The work is **complete** — the 211 propositions cover the **whole** of reality, from the first principle to the material world.

The 5 most important propositions (from **Dodds'** commentary):

- **Prop. 1**: All that is **multiplied** has a **prior unity** in which it participates
- **Prop. 5**: All that participates in any of the divine is itself a divine
- **Prop. 7**: The divine is the **measure** of all things, not the **measured**
- **Prop. 18**: Every god is **good**, and the **good** is **prior** to every god
- **Prop. 159**: The gods of the highest level are the **causes** of the gods of the lower level

These 5 propositions are the **core** of the Proclean system. The 211 propositions are the **extension**.

## The Negative Theology

Proclus is the **theologian of the negative theology** (*theologia apophatikos*). The negative theology is the **doctrine that the first principle is known by what it is not, not by what it is**. The first principle is **beyond** all categories, all attributes, all thought. The first principle can be **reached** only by **negation**: "the One is not being, not non-being, not God, not Godhead, not the first, not the last, not the all, not the one".

The negative theology is **not** atheism. The negative theology is the **acknowledgment** that the first principle **exceeds** all human categories. The negative theology is the **protection** of the first principle from the limits of the human intellect.

The negative theology is **practical**: the practitioner of the negative theology **withdraws** from the categories, **withdraws** from the attributes, **withdraws** from the thoughts. The withdrawal is the **mystical ascent** (see [[02-Plotinus-Enneads-One-Mind-Soul]]). The withdrawal is the **practical** method of the Neoplatonic life.

## The Platonic Theology

The **Platonic Theology** (Latin: *Theologia Platonica*) is Proclus' **most ambitious work** — 6 books (the work was projected as 18 books but only 6 were completed; books 1-5 are translated by Saffrey/Westerink; book 6 by Festugière). The work is the **comprehensive defense** of the Platonic theology against the **Aristotelian**, **Epicurean**, and **Stoic** theologies.

The 6 books:

1. **Book 1** — the divine, the divine order, the gods
2. **Book 2** — the divine names, the order of the gods
3. **Book 3** — the proofs of the existence of the gods (the arguments from the cosmos, from the soul, from the Forms, from the participation)
4. **Book 4** — the divine attributes (the good, the beautiful, the true, the just)
5. **Book 5** — the divine providence
6. **Book 6** — the divine will, the divine creation, the divine indestructibility

The work is **apologetic** — Proclus defends the **existence of the gods** and the **Platonic theology** against the alternatives. The defense is **philosophical**, not **rhetorical**.

## The Timaeus Commentary

Proclus wrote **two** commentaries on Plato's *Timaeus*. The first commentary is in 5 books (the more elementary); the second commentary is in 6 books (the deeper). The commentaries are the **most thorough** of the Neoplatonic commentaries on the *Timaeus*, and the **Timaeus** is the **central text** of the Platonic cosmology (the cosmology that **all** the Neoplatonists share).

The Timaeus is the **dialogue** where Plato describes the **creation of the cosmos** by the **demiurge**. Proclus' interpretation is **non-literal**: the *creation* is not a one-time event; the *creation* is the **continuous** activity of the demiurge. The **cosmos** is **eternally** created.

## The Parmenides Commentary

Proclus' **commentary on Plato's Parmenides** is the **most difficult** of the Neoplatonic commentaries. The Parmenides is the dialogue where Plato **examines** the **theory of the Forms** by **pushing** it to extremes. The first half of the dialogue is a **set of deductions** that **destroy** the theory of the Forms; the second half is a **set of deductions** that **recover** the theory.

Proclus' commentary reads the Parmenides as a **theological text** — the deductions are the **dialectical** path to the first principle. The first hypothesis is the **doctrine of the One**; the second hypothesis is the **doctrine of Nous**; the third is the **doctrine of Soul**; the fourth and fifth are the **descents** into the lower levels. The Parmenides is **the central text** of the Neoplatonic theology.

## The Practice of the Neoplatonic Life

Proclus' ethics is the **practice** of the Neoplatonic life. The practice is **theurgy** in the **mature** form — the **ascending** path of the soul through the **levels** of being, the **descending** path of the gods into the **material**.

The practice has **7 elements** (per Proclus' *Timaeus* commentary and the *Platonic Theology*):

1. **The mathematical** (*mathematikē*) — the **discipline** of the **mathematical** arts (arithmetic, geometry, harmonics, astronomy). The mathematical is the **first ascent** of the soul to the intelligible
2. **The ethical** (*ēthikē*) — the **discipline** of the **ethical** virtues (the civic, the purificatory, the contemplative). The ethical is the **purification** of the soul
3. **The physical** (*physikē*) — the **discipline** of the **physical** arts (the natural philosophy, the cosmology). The physical is the **comprehension** of the material world
4. **The theological** (*theologikē*) — the **discipline** of the **theological** speculation (the dialectical, the demonstration, the analysis). The theological is the **comprehension** of the divine
5. **The hieratic** (*hieratikē*) — the **discipline** of the **ritual** (the teletai, the telesmata, the theurgic operations). The hieratic is the **operation** of the divine
6. **The theurgic** (*theourgikē*) — the **discipline** of the **theurgic** art (the animation of the statue, the descent of the god, the ascent of the soul). The theurgic is the **union** with the divine
7. **The mystical** (*mystikē*) — the **discipline** of the **mystical** ascent (the negative theology, the unitive, the unsaid). The mystical is the **end** of the path

The progression is **upward**: the mathematical is the **first**; the mystical is the **last**. The last is the **goal**. The goal is the **mystical union** with the One.

## The Place of Proclus in the Tradition

Proclus is the **systematizer** of the Neoplatonic tradition. Proclus' system is the **mature expression** of the Neoplatonic philosophy; the later Neoplatonists (Marinus, Isidore, Damascius) added **details** but did not **change** the system.

Proclus' system is the **source** of:

1. **Pseudo-Dionysius'** *Divine Names* and *Mystical Theology* (5th-6th c., the Christian Neoplatonic synthesis)
2. **Boethius'** *Consolation of Philosophy* (the Latin bridge)
3. **The medieval Latin** tradition of **William of Moerbeke's** 1268 translation of *Elements of Theology* (the source of the scholastic Neoplatonism)
4. **The Renaissance** Platonic revival (Marsilio Ficino's translations)
5. **The German Idealism** of **Schelling** and **Hegel** (the modern philosophical Neoplatonism)
6. **The 19th-century** theosophical tradition (the modern religious Neoplatonism)

Proclus' **Elements of Theology** is the **most influential** single text of the Neoplatonic tradition. The 211 propositions are the **axioms** of the Neoplatonic system. The propositions are the **foundation** of the Western esoteric tradition.
