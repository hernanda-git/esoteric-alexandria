# Damascius and the Closing of the Academy

## The Last Scholarch

**Damascius of Damascus** (c. 458-538 CE) was the **final head** (*scholarch*) of the **Neoplatonic Academy of Athens** and the last major systematic thinker of the Athenian school before its dissolution. A student of **Proclus'** successors — first **Marinus**, then **Isidore of Alexandria** (whom he preferred) — Damascius represents the **terminal, radicalized** phase of Athenian Neoplatonism: more theurgic, more hostile to Christianity, and more rigorously apophatic than any predecessor.

He succeeded **Marinus** as scholarch around 515 CE and led the school through its final decade. His **magnum opus**, the *Dubitationes et Solutiones* ("Problems and Solutions concerning the First Principles"), is the **most radical** statement of Neoplatonic metaphysics: it pushes the **One beyond being, beyond intellect, and beyond the reach of affirmative predication** to a point where even Proclus' graded henads seem like compromise.

## The Text — Problems and Solutions

The *Problems and Solutions* is structured as a **dialectical inquiry** into the *archai* (first principles):

1. **The One** — Damascius asks whether the One can be said to be *one* at all. To predicate "one" of it is already to impose a limit. His answer is **radical apophasis**: the One is **beyond the one and the many**, ineffable, accessible only by *negation* and by a **cognitive leap** (*exaiphnēs*) that suspends discursive thought.
2. **The Henads** — the indivisible unities that bridge the One and the many; Damascius treats them as the **real individuals** of the divine, each a hypostatic "this."
3. **Intellect and Soul** — the emanation downward into *Nous* (the intelligible-intellective plurality) and *Psyche* (the world-soul).

The work is **undogmatic by design**: it *doubts* (the Latin title preserves the Greek *aporiai*) before it *solves*, modeling the **theurgic ascent** as a purification of the knowing faculty itself. Sara Ahbel-Rappe's 2010 translation makes the text available in English for the first time.

## The Closing — 529 CE

In **529 CE**, the emperor **Justinian I** issued an edict **prohibiting the teaching of philosophy by non-Christians** and **withdrawing the public salaries** of the pagan schools. The traditional date for the **formal dissolution of the Athenian Academy** — founded by **Plato** c. 387 BCE — is 529 CE, though the edict's wording suggests a **targeted suppression** of *hellēnistikē paideia* rather than a single dramatic closure.

Damascius, with **six colleagues** (traditionally named: Simplicius, Priscian, Eulamius, Hermias, Diogenes, and Isidore — though Agathias names only Damascius and **Simplicius** explicitly), **fled Athens** and traveled to the **Sasanian Persian court** of **Khosrow I** at Ctesiphon, seeking refuge under a "philosopher-king."

## The Persian Interlude and Return

The exile was **short-lived**. Khosrow I, though receptive, was bound by the **Eternal Peace** treaty (532 CE) with Justinian, which contained a clause **permitting the philosophers to return** to the Roman Empire **without persecution**. The group returned; Damascius likely **died in the East** (Syria or Alexandria) around 538 CE. Simplicius went on to write his monumental commentaries on Aristotle.

The episode marks the **end of organized pagan Neoplatonic teaching** in the Greek East. What remained was:

- **The manuscript tradition** — the commentaries of Simplicius and the *Problems* of Damascius, preserved largely by later Byzantine and Arabic scribes
- **The Christian capture** — Pseudo-Dionysius had already **baptized** the Athenian hierarchy (see [[05-Pseudo-Dionysius-Celestial-Hierarchy]]); later **Maximus the Confessor** would complete the appropriation
- **The Arabic reception** — the *Theology of Aristotle* (a paraphrased Plotinus) carried the Athenian metaphysics into the Islamic world

## The Significance

Damascius matters because he is the **hinge** between the living pagan Academy and its **afterlife** in three traditions:

1. **The Christian** — the *apophatic* Damascius (the One beyond predication) is the **exact structural twin** of the *via negativa* of Pseudo-Dionysius and the later *Cloud of Unknowing*
2. **The Islamic** — the *fana* (annihilation) of the Sufis and the *tanzih* (transcendence) of the theologians echo the radical apophasis (see [[09-Sufism-Irfan/01-Sufism-Overview]])
3. **The modern** — the **aporetic, doubt-first method** of the *Problems* prefigures the **phenomenological epochē** and the **negative theology** of 20th-century thought

The **closing of the Academy** is therefore not an ending but a **dispersal**: the theurgic philosophy survived its institutional death by **migrating** into the very monotheisms that suppressed it.

## The Practice Today

The Damascean apophasis is recoverable as a **modern contemplative method**:

1. **Affirm** a predicate of the absolute ("the One is good")
2. **Negate** it ("it is beyond good")
3. **Transcend** the opposition (the *exaiphnēs* leap)
4. **Rest** in the silence beyond predication

This is the **same structure** as the *neti neti* ("not this, not this") of the Upanishads and the *via negativa* of the Christian mystics — a **cross-traditional** practice the library documents throughout category 11 and category 17.
