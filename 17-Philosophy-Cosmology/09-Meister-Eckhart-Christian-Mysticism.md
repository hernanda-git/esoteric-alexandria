# Meister Eckhart and the Christian Mystical Tradition — The Godhead Beyond God

## The Most Radical Voice in the Christian Tradition

The Christian mystical tradition — the stream of Christian practice that seeks **direct, unmediated experiential knowledge of God** — is one of the richest and most systematically-elaborated bodies of esoteric literature within any of the world religions. Yet it is almost entirely absent from standard esoteric catalogs, which tend to focus on the Hermetic, Kabbalistic, or Islamic currents.

This document corrects that absence by surveying the **Christian apophatic tradition** — the "negative path" that strips away every name, image, and concept of God until only the ineffable ground remains — and anchoring it in its greatest practitioner: **Meister Eckhart**, the 13th‑century Dominican whose preaching of the "Godhead beyond God" and the "birth of the Word in the soul" is among the most daring metaphysical claims ever made within a theistic framework.

Eckhart is not merely a "Christian mystic" in the conventional sense. He is a **philosophical mystic** whose categories — the **Godhead** (Gottheit) beyond the **God** of the Trinity, the **ground of the soul** (Seelengrund) that is uncreated and identical with the Godhead, the **birth of the Son** in the soul as an eternal present event, the **breakthrough** (Durchbruch) into the desert of the divine — constitute a complete esoteric metaphysics that converges with the Advaita Vedanta of Śaṅkara, the *fanā'* (annihilation) of Ibn Arabi, and the *via negativa* of the Upanishads.

## The Christian Mystical Tradition: The Pre‑Eckhart Background

### The Patristic Foundation: Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite (c. 5th–6th c. CE)

The entire Western Christian mystical tradition is built on the writings of an anonymous late‑5th‑century Syrian monk who pseudonymously wrote as **Dionysius the Areopagite** — the Athenian convert of St. Paul (Acts 17:34). The ruse was totally successful for a millennium; his authority was virtually apostolic.

His four key works — *The Divine Names, The Mystical Theology, The Celestial Hierarchy,* and *The Ecclesiastical Hierarchy* — provide the architecture of Christian apophaticism:

**The double‑movement of the soul**: kataphasis (affirmation) and apophasis (negation). In *The Divine Names*, Dionysius affirms every name of God (Good, Being, Life, Wisdom, Power, Light) drawn from scripture. In *The Mystical Theology*, he then **negates** every single one:

> "It is not soul or intellect, nor has it imagination, opinion, reason, or understanding; it cannot be expressed or conceived; it is not number or order, greatness or smallness, equality or inequality, similarity or dissimilarity; it is not standing, moving, or at rest; it is not power, nor light; it does not live, nor is it life; it is not being, nor eternity, nor time; it is beyond all affirmation and negation." — *The Mystical Theology*, Ch. V

This is not intellectual play. It is a **spiritual exercise**: by systematically stripping away every conceptual handle the mind uses to grasp God, the practitioner is brought to an **intellectual silence** — the "luminous darkness" (*phylōtos gnophos*) in which God is known *as unknown*. The final passage of *The Mystical Theology* describes Moses entering the "darkness of unknowing" on Sinai — the point at which all conceptual mediation falls away and the soul touches God directly in the "ray of darkness."

This "darkness of God" is the governing metaphor of the entire subsequent tradition — and it is exactly the same structure as the **Ungrund** of Jacob Boehme (see [[10-Hermeticism-Gnosticism/08-Jacob-Boehme-Christian-Theosophy]]), the **Ayn Sof** of the Kabbalist tradition, and the **Cloud of Unknowing** that names the most famous English mystical text.

### The Patristic Foundation II: Evagrius, the Desert Fathers, and the Jesus Prayer

In the Eastern (Greek) Church, the mystical tradition developed through the **Desert Fathers** (4th c. onwards) — the hermits of the Egyptian wilderness who distilled the Gospel into a **one‑sentence prayer** that became the central formula of Eastern Christian mysticism: the **Jesus Prayer**:

> "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner."

The prayer is not a petition in the ordinary sense. It is a **mantra** — a continuous, rhythmic repetition synchronized with the breath, intended to "bring the mind into the heart" and eventually to become **unceasing** — a constant background presence that persists through sleep, work, and every activity. The *Philokalia* (the 18th‑c. anthology of Eastern ascetic‑mystical writings) is the canonical collection describing this practice and its psycho‑physiological effects.

### The Medieval Flowering: The Victorines, Bonaventure, and the Rhineland Mystics

The 12th‑13th centuries saw an explosion of mystical writing across Western Europe:

- **Hugh and Richard of St. Victor** (Paris, 12th c.) — developed a three‑stage model of spiritual ascent: *cogitatio* (thinking), *meditatio* (reflection), *contemplatio* (direct intuition). Richard's *Benjamin Major* and *Benjamin Minor* map the soul's journey into the "ark of the covenant" — the innermost sanctuary where God dwells in darkness.

- **Bonaventure** (*The Journey of the Mind into God*, 1259) — the Seraphic Doctor's vision of the six wings of the Seraph as six stages of contemplation leading to the "passover" (*transitus*) in which the soul passes beyond all forms of knowing into ecstatic union.

- **The Rhineland Mystics** — the loose circle of German Dominicans (Eckhart, Tauler, Suso) who pushed the apophatic tradition into its most radical formulations. Their audience was not other theologians but the **beguines** (lay religious women) and the ordinary faithful — Eckhart preached his most daring sermons in the vernacular German to the women of the Dominican convent circuit.

## Meister Eckhart (c.1260–1328): The Man from Whom God Hid Nothing

Eckhart von Hochheim entered the Dominican Order at about age 15, studied at the University of Paris (where he served twice as Master of Theology — a rare honour), held administrative posts as provincial of Saxony and vicar‑general of Bohemia, taught at the studium generale in Cologne, and preached in both Latin and German to audiences of clerics, nuns, and laypeople.

In 1326, the Archbishop of Cologne initiated an inquisition against him. Eckhart appealed to the Pope. In 1329, Pope John XXII's bull *In agro dominico* condemned 28 propositions from Eckhart's writings as either heretical or "evil‑sounding." Eckhart died before the bull was published — or, according to some accounts, he died on the journey to Avignon to defend himself.

The irony: Eckhart's teachings were revived in the 19th‑century Romantic rediscovery of medieval mysticism, and in the 20th century he was championed by Heidegger, Suzuki (who called Eckhart "the most profound thinker of the West"), Pope John Paul II (who cited him in a 1980 address), and the radical theologian John Caputo.

### The Four Eckhartian Doctrines

**1. The Godhead (Gottheit) and the God (Gott)**

This is the cornerstone of Eckhart's entire system, and the proposition that most alarmed his inquisitors:

> "God and the Godhead are as different as heaven and earth."

The **Godhead** (Gottheit) is the **divine essence prior to the Trinity** — the abyss of the divine nature that contains no attributes, no relations, no names, no activity. It is not Father, Son, or Spirit. It is the **ground from which the Trinity eternally proceeds**:

> "The Godhead is the pure, absolute being that stands still in itself, that neither gives nor takes. God acts; the Godhead does not act. God has a direction; the Godhead has none."

This is the **Christian Ungrund** — the direct structural equivalent of Jacob Boehme's Ungrund and the Kabbalistic Ayn Sof before the Tzimtzum. The difference is that for Eckhart the Godhead is not "nothing" in the sense of non‑being; it is **super‑essential being** (esse superessentiale) — being so full that it collapses into the silence of absolute simplicity.

**2. The Ground of the Soul (Seelengrund)**

The soul, for Eckhart, has two layers:

- The **faculties** (intellect, will, memory, senses) — these are the equipment for ordinary knowledge and action, created and finite.
- The **ground** (grunt, Seelengrund) — the innermost part of the soul that is **uncreated and uncreatable**, a "spark" (vünkelin) that is continuous with the Godhead itself.

> "There is something in the soul that is uncreated and uncreatable. If the whole soul were such, it would be God." — the Sermon *Beati pauperes spiritu* (Blessed are the poor in spirit)

This is not pantheism. Eckhart is careful to maintain the distinction: the soul's ground is not the Godhead's ground in the sense of *identity*, but it is **continuous** with it — a single, undifferentiated divine ground in which the soul participates at its deepest point. The Christian technical term is **theosis** (deification) — the Orthodox teaching that the human being can become God by participation without becoming God by essence.

Comparative note: the Seelengrund maps precisely onto:
- The **Ātman** of the Upanishads — the innermost self that is one with Brahman
- The **fana'** (annihilation) of Sufism — the dissolution of the ego‑self into the divine self
- The **Buddha‑nature** (tathāgatagarbha) of Mahayana Buddhism — the innate potential for awakening present in every sentient being

**3. The Birth of the Word in the Soul**

For Eckhart, the Incarnation is not a one‑time historical event in 1st‑century Palestine. It is an **eternal event that happens in the soul of every person who is prepared to receive it**:

> "The Father gives birth to his Son in eternity, equal to Himself. But I say more: He gives birth to him in the soul, in the same way as in eternity, and not otherwise. If the Son is to be born in the soul, the soul must strip itself of everything that might cling to it — time, space, images, concepts — and stand in the naked, empty ground."

The "birth" (*geburt*) is an **immediate union of the soul with God** that bypasses all intermediaries — including the Church, the sacraments, and (in its most startling formulation) even the historical Jesus. The soul that has been "stripped bare" (entbloezet) through detachment (abegescheidenheit) becomes the very locus of the Trinitarian process.

**4. Breakthrough (Durchbruch) and Detachment (Abegescheidenheit)**

The **breakthrough** is the goal — the passage beyond the God of the Trinity into the desert of the Godhead. Eckhart describes it in a famous prayer‑sermon:

> "I pray God to rid me of God. When I was in the ground, in the core of the Godhead, no one asked me where I was going or what I was doing. There was nothing there but myself, flowing freely in the will of the Godhead."

The **detachment** (abegescheidenheit — literally "cut‑off‑ness") is the method — a radical, systematic interior poverty that surrenders not only possessions and relationships but also every "self" — every image, concept, desire, memory, and expectation that the soul has of itself.

> "Detachment is the highest virtue, for it strips the soul of all that is created, all that is temporal, all that is multiple, and leaves it standing in the naked, simple, empty ground where God can enter without obstacle."

The detachment is not asceticism (the mortification of the body). It is **phenomenological reduction before Husserl**: the suspension of every presupposition, every attachment, every identification, until the soul stands in the "naked nothing" that is, simultaneously, the fullness of God.

## Beyond Eckhart: The Spanish Mystics

### Teresa of Ávila (1515–1582) — The Interior Castle

Teresa's *The Interior Castle* (1577) is the most systematic map of the Christian mystical path written from the inside. She describes the soul as a **castle with seven dwelling‑places** (moradas):

| Mansion | Name | Practice | Experience |
|---------|------|----------|------------|
| 1st | The Gate | Prayer of recollection | Recognition of one's own sinfulness; the soul is still in the outer courtyard, surrounded by "venomous creatures" (attachments) |
| 2nd | The Beginners | Active prayer, reading, sermons | The soul tries to enter but turns back; alternating progress and relapse |
| 3rd | The Proficient | Acquired contemplation | The soul has some stability; discursive meditation gives way to simpler, quieter attention |
| 4th | The Transition | Prayer of quiet (first passive prayer) | The will is caught by God; the intellect and memory resist; the first "touches" of supernatural experience |
| 5th | The Union | Simple union (the silkworm) | The soul is "like a silkworm that spins a cocoon and dies, and emerges as a white butterfly" — the old self dies and the new self is born in God |
| 6th | The Betrothal | Ecstatic union, raptures, visions | The soul is repeatedly taken out of itself; betrothal to Christ; the "wound of love"; trials and purifications intensify |
| 7th | The Marriage | Transforming union (spiritual marriage) | Permanent, habitual consciousness of indwelling God; the soul is united with the Trinity in the innermost chamber; Mary Magdalene as archetype |

Teresa's framework is important because it distinguishes **acquired** contemplation (something the soul can develop through practice) from **infused** contemplation (a gift that cannot be produced by effort). The transition from the 3rd to the 4th mansion is the **critical threshold** — the moment when the practice ceases to be a human activity and becomes a divine one.

### John of the Cross (1542–1591) — The Dark Night of the Soul

John, Teresa's younger collaborator in the Discalced Carmelite reform, wrote the most famous poetic account of the apophatic path in the Christian West: the **Dark Night** (La noche oscura). His commentary on the poem describes two purifications:

**The Dark Night of the Senses**: God withdraws all sensible consolation from the soul — the "sweetness" that beginners attach to prayer, the emotional rewards of spiritual practice. The soul is left dry, empty, and without feeling. This is not punishment or abandonment; it is the **first stage of purification** — the weaning of the soul from spiritual pleasure so that it can love God for God's own sake.

**The Dark Night of the Spirit**: A more profound and devastating purification. God touches the soul at its deepest level, and the soul experiences itself as **nothing** — as totally inadequate, sinful, and separate from God. John calls this "the night of faith" — the soul must go forward without any experiential support, trusting in the naked love of God.

The "dark night" is not a metaphor for depression or spiritual crisis. It is a **deliberate therapeutic intervention by God** — the stripping away of every conceptual and affective support until the soul can stand in the "naked faith" that alone can receive divine union.

## Critical Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. **Systematic experimental practice**: Unlike many esoteric traditions that rely on doctrinal belief, the Christian mystical tradition provides a complete **phenomenology of the interior life** — detailed, stage‑by‑stage descriptions of what the practitioner actually experiences at each level of spiritual development (see especially Teresa's *Interior Castle* and John's *Dark Night*).

2. **The most extensive apophatic corpus in the West**: No other Western tradition has produced a comparable body of writing dedicated to the systematic negation of concepts about God — a library of spiritual technology for reaching the "radical ignorance" that Eckhart calls the highest knowledge.

3. **Integration with institutional Christianity**: Unlike Hermeticism or the Kabbalah, which existed as marginal traditions outside the dominant religion, Christian mysticism operated **within** the Church. Its figures were priests, bishops, abbots, and friars. This means its textual and institutional preservation is vastly superior to that of most other esoteric traditions.

**Weaknesses/Controversies:**

1. **The institutional suppression**: Every major figure in this tradition was either investigated for heresy (Eckhart), imprisoned by the Inquisition (John of the Cross), or subjected to intense ecclesiastical scrutiny (Teresa). The marginalisation of mysticism within institutional Christianity has led to the loss of many texts and the systematic de‑emphasis of the experiential dimension.

2. **The gender politics: 80% of the medieval vernacular mystical writers whose names we know were women (Hadewijch, Mechthild, Angela of Foligno, Marguerite Porete — the last burned at the stake for her *Mirror of Simple Souls*). The Church's response ranged from cautious endorsement (Teresa) to execution (Porete). The tradition is profoundly shaped by this tension.

3. **The "gnostic" tension**: Eckhart's teaching of the uncreated spark in the soul was condemned as "the error of the Free Spirit" — a medieval heresy that held that the perfected soul could not sin and was free from moral law. The distinction between Eckhart's position and this heresy is subtle; the inquisitors had a point.

## Reading List

**First‑time reader:**
- Denys Turner, *The Darkness of God: Negativity in Christian Mysticism* (1995) — the best modern introduction to the entire apophatic tradition; starts with Dionysius and ends with John of the Cross
- *The Cloud of Unknowing* (anonymous, 14th c.) — the practical manual; short, direct, and surprisingly funny for a text on divine darkness
- Teresa of Ávila, *The Interior Castle* (1577) — the most vivid first‑person account of the interior journey
- Meister Eckhart: the sermons translated by Maurice O'C. Walshe in *The Complete Mystical Works* (2009) — read "Blessed are the Poor in Spirit," "The Virgin and the Wife," and "The Eternal Birth"

**Advanced:**
- Bernard McGinn, *The Mystical Thought of Meister Eckhart* (2001) — the definitive study
- Vladimir Lossky, *The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church* (1944) — the Orthodox apophatic tradition
- John of the Cross, *The Dark Night of the Soul* and *The Ascent of Mount Carmel* (c.1580) — the systematic apophatic manual
- Evelyn Underhill, *Mysticism* (1911) — the classic comparative treatment

**Cross‑Comparative:**
- Rudolf Otto, *Mysticism East and West* (1932) — the classic comparison of Eckhart and Śaṅkara
- Steven T. Katz (ed.), *Mysticism and Religious Traditions* (1983) — the "constructivist" critique of perennialist mysticism
- William James, *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) — the modern psychological study that begins with Christian mysticism and extends cross‑culturally
