# The Picatrix — *Ghayat al-Hakim*

## The Book

**Ghayat al-Hakim** ("The Aim of the Sage") is the longest and most thorough Arabic treatise on planetary talismanic magic. The name "Picatrix" is a Latin corruption (probably of *buqratis* = "Hippocrates", used as a pseudonym). The work is a 10th-11th century compilation, almost certainly produced in **Toledo** during the period of the Toledo Translation Movement, drawing on:

- Greek Hermetic and Neoplatonic material (Apuleius, Iamblichus, Proclus)
- Babylonian and Sabian-of-Harran material (the Mandaeans, the Sabians)
- Earlier Arabic material (Abu Ma'shar, al-Kindi, Thabit ibn Qurra)

The Latin translation was made in 1256 CE by **Abel of Leuven** (or possibly a Sicilian collaborator) for King Alfonso X of Castile. The Latin version was printed in **Cracow in 1480s** and became a major source for the Renaissance magicians.

## Why It Matters

The Picatrix is the **bridge text** between Islamic and Western magic. It is also the **most explicit** of the talismanic texts — it actually tells you what to do, in what order, with what materials, at what time. The Agrippan tradition took its planetary attributions, the deity names, the timing system, and the ritual structure almost directly from Picatrix.

## The Four Books

| Book | Subject | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **Book I** | The heavens and the planetary intelligences | The celestial hierarchy, the names of the stars, the divine names that rule them |
| **Book II** | The images and the talismans | 232 specific talismans and their planetary attributions (this is the famous part) |
| **Book III** | The 28 lunar mansions | The 28 mansions of the moon, the magick of each, the days and hours |
| **Book IV** | The operations | How to perform the talismanic ritual — the preparation, the timing, the consecration, the activation |

The total length is about 400 pages in the standard Arabic edition.

## The Planetary Talismans (Book II)

The Picatrix gives **232 specific talismans**, organized by planet. Each talisman has:

- The **image** (often a figure of a deity or spirit)
- The **materials** (specific metals, gems, colors)
- The **time** (which day, which hour, which mansion)
- The **incense** (each planet has its own incense)
- The **name** (a divine name and a spirit name to invoke)
- The **effect** (what the talisman is supposed to do)

This is the most systematic talismanic catalogue in any pre-modern tradition. Modern occultists (Agrippa, the Golden Dawn, the modern chaos magicians) have drawn on it extensively.

## The Sabian Material

A large portion of the Picatrix is dedicated to the **Sabians of Harran** — a pre-Islamic pagan community in northern Mesopotamia (modern Turkey) who survived into the 10th century by **masquerading as "people of the book"** (a protected religious category in early Islam). They were star-worshippers, practitioners of theurgy, and custodians of the old Babylonian astrolatry.

The Picatrix's references to the Sabians are the **major source** of what we know about this community. The Harranian tradition is also a key ancestor of the Sabaeans/Mandaeans of southern Iraq today.

The sabian material in Picatrix connects the Islamic talismanic tradition to the **Babylonian astrolatry** going back to the Enuma Anu Enlil (see [[13-Mesopotamian-Egyptian/01-Enuma-Anu-Enlil]]).

## The Latin Reception

The 1256 Latin translation circulated in manuscript for 200 years, then was printed in Cracow (1480s) and in a more famous 1509 Basel edition. Key readers:

- **Marsilio Ficino** (1433-1499) — used it for the *De Vita Coelitus Comparanda* (the most explicit Renaissance recipe book for planetary talismans)
- **Giovanni Pico della Mirandola** (1463-1494) — debated with Ficino about whether Christian Kabbalah was legitimate, drew extensively on Picatrix's planetary attributions
- **Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa** (1486-1535) — systematized Picatrix into the *Three Books of Occult Philosophy* (see [[01-Three-Books-Agrippa]])
- **John Dee** (1527-1608/9) — used Picatrix's angel hierarchy as a basis for his "Enochian" system

## The Modern Critical Edition

The Arabic text is published in a massive ongoing critical edition by **Hellmut Ritter** (1933-ongoing). The English translation by Greer and Warnock (2010) is the most usable for modern students.

## The Talismans in Practice — A Recipe

The Picatrix is unusually detailed. Here is a representative recipe (Book II, chapter 5, "Of the Talismans of the Sun"):

> Make a talisman for the Sun at the time when the Sun is in his exaltation (Aries) and in a fortunate day, in the hour of the Sun, when the Moon is well-placed. The image is a king seated on a throne, with a crown on his head, holding a scepter. Make it in gold, on a Sunday. Engrave on the back the divine name of the Sun [the Arabic name "Al-Shams" or the divine name "Allah"]. Suffumigate with frankincense. This talisman gives favor with kings, success in petitions, freedom from fear, and the increase of dignities.

This is a **protocol** with a clear protocol stack: planetary placement → lunar placement → day → hour → image → material → inscription → suffumigation → outcome. A modern engineer can read this as a **deploy script**.

## A Note on Practice

The Picatrix is a **manual of practice**. The recipes are real and operational, not metaphorical. Modern readers should approach with the same care as a chemistry lab manual — explicit, no-nonsense, dangerous if mishandled. The Western occult revival (Golden Dawn, OTO, chaos magic) has tested many of these recipes and reported varying degrees of efficacy. The library takes no position on efficacy; it documents the recipes and their historical use.
