# Ars Notoria — The Notary Art and Memory Rituals

## The Text

The **Ars Notoria** is the oldest section of the **Lemegeton** compilation, predating the Goetia and Almadel strata by at least a century. Unlike most grimoires, it is not about commanding demons or planetary intelligences — it is a **prayer-and-meditation manual for acquiring memory, eloquence, and mental mastery**. The title means "the notary art": the art of infusing the mind with preternatural learning through designated prayers, ritual postures, and *notae* — complex glyph-mnemonics drawn over geomantic or concentric circles.

The practitioner is meant to **recite specific prayers** over the figure, while observing dietary restrictions and ritual washings, so that the mind becomes a receptive instrument not merely remembering but spiritually receiving the disciplines.

## Structure

The classical Latin tradition divides the work into:

- **The First Origin** — prayers for the seven liberal arts and sciences
- **The Second Origin** — prayers for individual arts; some manuscripts add a third and fourth origin
- **The Figures** — the complex circular notae, composed of angelic names, geometric bindings, and sometimes Hebrew words
- **Ritual prerequisites** — fasting, confession, specific hours, wearing linen, burning incense

The Presbyterian figure-work behaves like a medieval **USB**: the operator "loads" the desired art into the glyph, and repeated ritual exposure installs the cognitive upgrade.

## The Cognitive Claim

The Ars Notoria claims that certain rituals and prayers can produce **immediate learning** (*subita doctrina*). The practitioner who has faithfully performed the operation for 28 days "will know everything that concerns that science."

The modern parallel is **spaced-repetition and deliberate practice**, but framed by the text in supernatural terms: Angels of the Hours and planetary angels act as tutors. The knowledge is *received*, not *inferred*.

This is **the cognitive traditions turned into ritual software**. The medieval mind treated knowledge as emanated from the supercelestial; the practice encodes the same drive as modern accelerated learning: repeated ritual exposure to structured symbols toward internalization.

## Provenance

The *Ars Notoria* appears in:

- Sloane 3851 (British Library, c. 14th c.)
- Cotton Vitellius
- BnF Latin 11326 and 9336
- Printed editions from the 15th-17th c., including the 1626 Latin edition

The text was known to **John Dee**, who owned several related manuscripts and incorporated notary-style figures into his own angelic operations with Edward Kelley. **Robert Fludd** defended the art against critics in the early 17th c.

## Modern Use Cases

1. **Memory athletes** — the notae are highly visual mnemonics; the concentration rituals parallel pre-event focus routines.
2. **Deep-work practitioners** — the isolation/diet component is a deliberate-distress technique that increases suggestibility.
3. **Symbolic designers** — as a predecessor to sigil-craft, the geometric figures offer medieval precedents for binding concepts into form.

## The Critique

Critics from **Pico della Mirandola** onward noted that the Latin phrases are often nonsensical, the attributions legendary, and the promised cognitive feats inconsistent. Some passages read like **pious nonsense ( glossolalia-inspired )**. However, the text is internally coherent as a **psycho-technical manual**: it presupposes a theory of learning-by-divine-impression that has been continuously reinvented in modern religion and self-development.
