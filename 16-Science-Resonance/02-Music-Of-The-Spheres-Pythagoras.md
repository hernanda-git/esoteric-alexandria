# Music of the Spheres — Pythagoras

## The Claim

The **Music of the Spheres** (Greek *harmonia tōn kosmōn*, "harmony of the cosmos") is the **Pythagorean doctrine** that the **planets, the sun, the moon, and the stars** produce a **continuous music** as they move through the heavens. The music is **not** audible to humans (in the ordinary state of consciousness); only the **initiate** can hear it. The music is **mathematical** in nature — the ratios of the planetary distances are the **ratios of the musical scale**.

The doctrine is **one of the most influential** in the history of Western thought. It is the **first** explicit **mathematical cosmology** — the cosmos is a **harmony**, a **mathematical structure**, an **expression of number**. The modern physics of string theory, of cosmic harmonics, of the "music of the universe" is a **direct descendant** of the Pythagorean claim.

## The Discovery

The Pythagorean tradition holds that Pythagoras himself discovered the **mathematical basis of harmony**. The story (told by Nicomachus of Gerasa, 2nd c. CE, in his *Enchiridion Harmonices*):

> Pythagoras was walking past a blacksmith's shop, and heard the hammers striking. He noticed that some pairs of hammers produced a **consonant** sound, others a **dissonant** sound. He went into the shop, weighed the hammers, and found that the **consonant** pairs were in **simple mathematical ratios** (2:1, 3:2, 4:3). He took the discovery back to his school and developed the theory of harmonics.

The story is **almost certainly legend**. The actual discovery is more likely to have been made with a **monochord** (a single-stringed instrument with a movable bridge) — by moving the bridge, Pythagoras (or his students) discovered that the **musical intervals** correspond to **simple ratios** of the string length.

## The Four Ratios

The Pythagorean tradition gives the **four fundamental ratios** of the musical scale. They are the ratios that produce the **consonant** intervals — the **tetractys** of harmony:

| Ratio | Interval | Name | Modern |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1:1 | Unison | The fundamental | The same note |
| 1:2 | Octave | *Diapason* | The 8th |
| 2:3 | Fifth | *Diapente* | The 5th |
| 3:4 | Fourth | *Diatessaron* | The 4th |

The four ratios are the **basis** of the entire musical scale. The 1:2 ratio is the **octave** (the same note, one octave higher). The 2:3 ratio is the **perfect fifth**. The 3:4 ratio is the **perfect fourth**. The 1:1 ratio is the **unison** (the same note).

The discovery of these ratios is the **most important** in the history of Western music. The **Western musical scale** is built from these ratios (with additional ratios for the thirds, sixths, etc., that were added by later theorists).

## The Tetractys

The **tetractys** (τετρακτύς, "the four") is the **Pythagorean symbol** of the four ratios. The tetractys is a triangular arrangement of 10 dots: 1 on top, 2 below, 3 below that, 4 at the bottom. The 10 dots represent the **first four numbers** (1+2+3+4 = 10) and the **first four ratios** (1:1, 1:2, 2:3, 3:4).

The tetractys was the **sacred symbol** of the Pythagorean school. The school oath (preserved in Iamblichus, *De Vita Pythagorica*):

> "I swear by him who transmitted to our souls the tetractys, the source and root of ever-flowing nature."

The tetractys is the **ancestor** of the **sephirot** of the Kabbalah (see [[17-Philosophy-Cosmology/04-Tetraktys-And-Pythagorean-Number]]). The Kabbalistic Tree of Life is, in a deep sense, a **renaming** of the tetractys.

## The Planetary Harmony

The **planetary harmony** is the doctrine that the **seven planets** (in the Ptolemaic / Pythagorean system: Moon, Mercury, Venus, Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn) correspond to the **seven notes** of the musical scale, and the **heavenly spheres** correspond to the **seven intervals** of the octave.

The doctrine has many versions. The most famous (Ptolemy, 2nd c. CE, *Harmonics*):

- The **Moon** corresponds to the **lowest note** (the *hypate* in the Greek mode)
- **Mercury** to the next note
- **Venus** to the next
- **The Sun** to the **middle note** (the *mese*, the keynote)
- **Mars** to the next
- **Jupiter** to the next
- **Saturn** to the **highest note** (the *nete*)

The **distances** between the planets correspond to the **intervals** of the scale. The **Pythagorean ratios** are the basis.

The **Music of the Spheres** is the **celestial** music. The **practitioner's task** is to **hear** it. The **initiate** can hear it; the **ordinary person** cannot. The initiate is the one who has **tuned** their own being to the cosmic harmony.

## The Modern Note

The Music of the Spheres is **alive** in three communities:

1. **The Pythagorean revival** — the modern Pythagorean orders (the **Pythagorean Society**, the **Mathesis** of Basel) study the doctrine
2. **The modern physics community** — the **"music of the universe"** tradition in modern physics (the cosmic microwave background, the string theory, the **Tonality of the universe** hypothesis) is a direct descendant
3. **The popular spirituality** — the "music of the spheres" is a common figure in the modern popular culture, used in music, art, and the new-age tradition

The library's category 16 treats the Music of the Spheres as the **first mathematical cosmology** in the Western tradition, and as the **deep ancestor** of the modern physics of cosmic harmonics. ## The Practice

A modern practice of the Pythagorean tradition requires:

1. **The foundation** — basic music theory, basic acoustics, basic mathematics
2. **The texts** — read the *Republic* (Book VII, on the mathematical education), the *Timaeus* (on the structure of the cosmos), the *Harmonics* of Ptolemy, the *De Institutione Musica* of Boethius
3. **The practice** — study a **monochord** (a single-stringed instrument with a movable bridge), explore the **mathematical ratios** of the intervals
4. **The tradition** — find a teacher in the modern Pythagorean tradition
5. **The community** — the practice is best learned in a **community** of students of harmonics
