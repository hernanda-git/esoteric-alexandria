# Cymatics — The Geometry of Vibration

## The Phenomenon

**Cymatics** (from Greek *kyma*, "wave") is the **study of visible sound and vibration**. The phenomenon: when a surface (a metal plate, a membrane, a liquid) is vibrated at a specific frequency, the **standing wave pattern** is **visible** as a **geometric figure** of nodes and antinodes.

The cymatic pattern depends on:

- The **frequency** of the vibration
- The **medium** (the substance of the surface)
- The **geometry** of the surface (round, square, rectangular)
- The **mode** of excitation (single point, edge, center)

The most famous cymatic images are the **Chladni patterns** — the figures produced when a metal plate is vibrated with a violin bow and **sand** (or another fine powder) is sprinkled on the plate. The sand **migrates to the nodes** (the still points) and **reveals** the standing wave pattern as a geometric figure.

## The Pioneers

### Robert Hooke (1665)

**Robert Hooke** (1635-1703) was the first to **observe** the phenomenon systematically. He used a **glass plate** with sand on it, and a violin bow, to produce patterns. His report to the **Royal Society** is the **first published** account of cymatic figures.

### Ernst Chladni (1787)

**Ernst Chladni** (1756-1827) was the **German physicist** who **systematized** the study. Chladni's book *Entdeckungen über die Theorie des Klanges* (Discoveries in the Theory of Sound, 1787) presented the **first systematic classification** of cymatic patterns. The patterns are still called **Chladni patterns** in his honor.

Chladni **traveled Europe** with his demonstrations. **Napoleon** was so impressed that he offered a prize for a mathematical explanation of the patterns — the prize was eventually won by **Sophie Germain** (1816) and **Chladni's law** became part of the theory of elastic plates.

### Hans Jenny (1967)

**Hans Jenny** (1904-1972) was a **Swiss physician, anthroposophist, and natural scientist** who coined the term **cymatics** and produced the **most extensive** documentation of cymatic phenomena. His multi-volume *Cymatics* (1967) contains thousands of photographs and films of cymatic patterns in **liquids, gases, and solids**.

Jenny's contribution was to **show** the **isomorphism** between cymatic patterns and **natural forms**:

- The **hexagonal** pattern at certain frequencies looks like **snowflakes** and **honeycomb cells**
- The **spiral** pattern at certain frequencies looks like **galaxies** and **shells**
- The **radial** pattern at certain frequencies looks like **flowers** and **radiolarians**
- The **grid** pattern at certain frequencies looks like **crystal lattices** and **cell walls**

Jenny's argument: **form arises from vibration**. The natural forms we see are **frozen cymatic patterns** — the result of standing waves in the medium of the cosmos.

## The Causal Mechanism

A standing wave on a surface has **nodes** (the still points, where the surface does not move) and **antinodes** (the points of maximum displacement). When sand is placed on the surface, the sand is **thrown** from the antinodes to the nodes by the vibration. The result: the sand **maps** the nodes, producing the **geometric pattern**.

The pattern depends on the **mode** of vibration:

- **First mode** (lowest frequency): the simplest pattern — a single line of nodes through the center
- **Second mode**: a cross, with two perpendicular lines of nodes
- **Third mode**: a triangle, or a star, depending on the geometry
- **Higher modes**: increasingly complex patterns, often with the symmetry of the plate

The patterns are **mathematically determined** by the geometry of the plate and the frequency. The **Chladni equation** (a partial differential equation) gives the exact pattern for any frequency and geometry.

## The Modern Note

Cymatics is **alive** in three communities:

1. **The scientific community** — cymatics is a **sub-branch of acoustics**, studied in materials science, the study of vibrating plates, the study of granular materials
2. **The art community** — the **Cymatics** of the contemporary art (Shirley Shor, Evan Grant) use cymatic patterns in sculpture, light, and sound installations
3. **The esoteric / new age community** — cymatics is the **modern proof** of the esoteric claim that vibration creates form. The **Sound-Healing** tradition, the **Nada Brahma** yoga tradition, the **Cymatics Institute** of John Stuart Reid all draw on the cymatic phenomenon

The library's category 16 treats cymatics as the **cleanest case** of the **bridge** between the modern scientific and the ancient esoteric traditions. The cymatic pattern is a **phenomenon** that can be **observed** in the lab; the **interpretation** of the pattern (as the modern proof of the ancient claim) is the **bridge**.

## The Practice

A modern practice of cymatics requires:

1. **The equipment** — a **function generator** (a signal source), an **amplifier**, a **vibration source** (a speaker or a piezo transducer), a **plate** (a metal plate or a membrane), a **substance** (sand, salt, water, lycopodium powder)
2. **The setup** — set the frequency, sprinkle the substance, observe the pattern
3. **The exploration** — vary the frequency and observe the changing patterns
4. **The recording** — photograph or film the patterns for documentation

A **simple setup** can be built for less than $100: a function generator (~$30), a small speaker (~$20), a metal plate (~$10), some sand or salt (~$1). The practice is **accessible**.

## The Modern Applications

The cymatic phenomenon has **modern applications** in:

- **Music education** — the Chladni figures make the abstract concept of harmonics **visible**
- **Acoustics** — the design of concert halls, the placement of speakers, the geometry of instruments
- **Materials science** — the study of granular materials, the flow of powders
- **Medical imaging** — the visualization of vibrations in the body
- **Cymatic therapy** — the (controversial) use of cymatic patterns in alternative medicine
