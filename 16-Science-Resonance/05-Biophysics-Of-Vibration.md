# Biophysics of Vibration — Cells, Bone, and Sound

## The Claim

Living tissue is not inert matter — it is a **vibratory medium** that **senses, conducts, and is shaped by** mechanical oscillation. The claim that form arises from vibration (see [[01-Cymatics-And-Vibration]]) is not only a property of sand on a plate; it is a property of **bone, cells, and the cytoskeleton itself**. The biophysics of vibration is the modern, experimental extension of cymatics into the **living body**.

The claim is **quantitative**: cells have measurable resonant frequencies, bone adapts to specific frequency bands, and coherent oscillations appear in biological systems. This is the **bridge** between cymatics and biology.

## The Mechanobiology — Bone Responds to Vibration

**Wolff's law** (1892) states that bone remodels along lines of mechanical stress. The modern refinement is the work of **Clinton Rubin and Janet McLeod** (1994), who showed that **low-magnitude, high-frequency mechanical signals** (LMHF — strains of ~10–50 microstrain at 15–90 Hz, applied for minutes per day) **increase trabecular bone density** in animals and humans, without exercise-level loads.

The mechanism: osteocytes (the bone's mechanosensory cells) act as **frequency filters**. They respond to the **dynamic acceleration**, not the static load. The implication — confirmed in NASA-sponsored studies of simulated microgravity — is that **bone is tuned to vibration**, and that the absence of vibration (spaceflight, bedrest) is a primary driver of bone loss.

This is the biological echo of the cymatic principle: the **medium** (bone matrix) is organized by the **standing wave** (the mechanical signal). The pattern of mineral deposition follows the stress field, exactly as sand follows the nodal lines.

## The Frohlich Coherence

**Herbert Frohlich** (1968) proposed that biological systems support **long-range coherent oscillations** in the microwave / dielectric range (typically ~10^11 Hz, ~100 GHz), driven by metabolic energy. In a Frohlich system, supplied energy does not thermalize uniformly; instead a **macroscopic condensate** of dipolar oscillations forms — an ordered vibrational state, analogous to a Bose-Einstein-like coherence.

Candidate sites:
- **Microtubules** — the protein filaments of the cytoskeleton, which support longitudinal and transverse elastic waves
- **Membranes** — collective dipole oscillations in the lipid bilayer
- **Collagen** — piezo-electric and acoustic propagation along fibrils

The Frohlich hypothesis remains **contested** (the energy barrier to coherence at body temperature is real), but it frames vibration as a possible **carrier of biological order**, not merely a byproduct of chemistry — directly parallel to the esoteric claim that the subtle body is a **resonant lattice**.

## Cellular and Tissue Resonance

Cells and tissues have measurable **resonant frequencies**:
- **Erythrocytes** and other cells resonate in the low-kHz acoustic range; sonication at the match frequency causes maximal deformation
- **DNA** double helices support transverse phonon modes (~GHz–THz) studied by Brillouin scattering
- **Tensegrity** models (Ingber) show the cytoskeleton as a **prestressed network** that transmits mechanical signals to the nucleus — the physical route by which a vibration at the membrane becomes a change in gene expression

The practical consequence is **mechanotransduction**: a vibration is converted into a **biochemical signal**. Sound literally becomes biology.
