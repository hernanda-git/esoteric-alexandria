# Al-Hallaj and the Ana al-Haqq — The Martyr of Love

## The Man

**Abu al-Mughith al-Husayn ibn Mansur al-Hallaj** (c. 858-922 CE) was a **Persian** Sufi, born near **Shiraz** (in the **Fars** province of **Persia**) to a **carder of cotton** (*hallaj* means "the carder", the source of his surname). He travelled through **Persia**, **Iraq**, **Khorasan**, **India**, and possibly **Turkestan** and **the Caucasus**, performing the **pilgrimage** to **Mecca** multiple times. He was executed in **Baghdad** in **922 CE** after a **long trial** (the proceedings began c. 912) on charges of **heresy** and **sedition**.

Al-Hallaj is the central figure of the **"intoxicated"** (*sukr*) school of Sufism — those whose **mystical** **experience** **overflows** into **public** **utterance**. He stands in deliberate contrast to the **"sober"** (*sahw*) school of his teacher-figure **Junayd al-Baghdadi** (d. 910 CE), who held that the **mystic** must **preserve** the **discipline** of the **law** (*sharia*) and **conceal** the **unions** of the **path**. Al-Hallaj was, by tradition, **expelled** from Junayd's circle precisely for his **disclosure** (*tajalli*) of the **inner** **secret**.

## The Saying — Ana al-Haqq

The **famous** **utterance** of al-Hallaj is ***Ana al-Haqq*** (أنا الحق, "**I am the Truth**", i.e. "**I am God**"). The **saying** is reported from his **execution** and from his **public** **ecstasies** in the **mosque** of **Baghdad**. The **saying** is **not** a claim of **personal** **divinity** in the **Christian** **sense**; the **saying** is the **mystical** **report** that, in the **state** of **annihilation** (*fana'*) and **union**, the **limited** **ego** is **absorbed** and what **speaks** is the **divine** **Truth** **itself**.

The **doctrine** behind the **saying** is the **Sufi** **distinction** between the **self** (*nafs*) and the **spirit** (*ruh*). In the **state** of *fana'*, the **nafs** is **dissolved** and the **ruh** **returns** to its **source**; the **utterance** "**Ana al-Haqq**" is the ** ru**h** speaking through the **empty** **form** of the **saint**. The **saying** is **structurally** **akin** to the **Neoplatonic** **doctrine** of the **return** of the **soul** to the **One** (see [[11-NeoPlatonism-Theurgy/02-Plotinus-Enneads-One-Mind-Soul]]); the **two** are **not** **identical** in **vocabulary** but **isomorphic** in **structure**.

The **saying** is **controversial**. The **"sober"** school (Junayd) holds that the **saying** is **permissible** **only** in **private** and **catastrophic** in **public**, because it **scandalizes** the **law**. The **"intoxicated"** school (Hallaj, and later **Bayazid Bastami**, d. c. 874, with his "**Subhani**", "**Glory** **be** **to** **me**") holds that the **saying** is the **inevitable** **fruit** of **realization** and that **concealment** would be a **lie**. The **debate** is the **central** **axis** of the **Sufi** **ethics** of **disclosure**.

## The Kitab al-Tawasin

The **Kitab al-Tawasin** (كتاب الطواسين, "**The Book of the Tawasin**") is the **major** **prose** **work** of al-Hallaj. The **title** refers to **Surat Taha** (Quran 20), one of the **mysterious** **disconnected** **letters** (*muqatta'at*) of the Quran; al-Hallaj wrote **eleven** **chapters** (the **Tawasin**), each a **meditation** on a **Quranic** **theme** of **divine** **love** and **self-sacrifice**. The **central** **chapter** is the **"Tasin al-Azal"** (the **Chapter of Eternity**), which presents the **pre-eternal** **covenant** between **God** and the **souls**.

The **Tawasin** presents the **doctrine** of the **"crucifixion"** of the **soul** — that the **mystic** must **die** to the **self** as the **body** **hangs** on the **cross** of **existence**. This **imagery** is the **source** of the **later** **Christian**-influenced **readings** of al-Hallaj as a **"Islamic** **Christ**" (a reading advanced by **Massignon**). The **image** is **not** a **claim** of **incarnation**; the **image** is the **Sufi** **metaphor** of *fana'* as **voluntary** **sacrifice**.

## The Trial and the Martyrdom

The **trial** of al-Hallaj is one of the **best-documented** **events** of **early** **Sufism**. He was **arrested** c. 912 CE, **imprisoned** for **nearly** **nine** **years**, **tried** before the **Abbasid** **court** in **Baghdad**, **convicted** of **claiming** **divinity** and of **sedition** (the **charge** of **sedition** was tied to his **political** **following** and his **travels** among **dissident** **groups**), and **executed** in **922 CE**: **crucified** (or **hanged**), **beheaded**, **dismembered**, and **burned**, with his **ashes** **cast** into the **Tigris**.

The **martyrdom** made al-Hallaj the **patron** **saint** of **Sufi** **suffering** and the **proof-text** for the **"intoxicated"** **school**. **Massignon** counted **three** **levels** of **meaning** in the **martyrdom**: the **historical** (a **juridical** **murder**), the **mystical** (the **union** **consummated** in **death**), and the **cosmic** (the **perpetual** **intercession** of the **saint**). The **martyrdom** is **not** a **failure**; the **martyrdom** is the **seal** of the **realization**.

## The Modern Reception

Al-Hallaj is a **living** **figure** in the **modern** **West**. **Louis Massignon** (1889-1962) made the **study** of al-Hallaj the **centre** of his **life** **work** and a **foundation** of **modern** **Islamic** **studies**; his **conversion** **experience** at **Touggourt** (1908) was bound to the **invocation** of al-Hallaj. **Annemarie Schimmel** carried the **Hallaj** **tradition** into **German** **scholarship**. The **poets** **Nietzsche**, **Rilke**, and **Iqbal** (Muhammad Iqbal, the **muslim** **philosopher-poet**) all **drew** on the **figure** of al-Hallaj.

The **modern** **Sufi** should **read**:
1. **The Kitab al-Tawasin** (Arberry's English, 1974) — the **core** **text**
2. **The Diwan** (selections in Ernst, 2018) — the **lyric** **voice**
3. **Massignon's Passion** (Mason's abridged English, 1982) — the **critical** **hagiography**
4. **Schimmel's Mystical Dimensions of Islam** — the **survey**

The **figure** of al-Hallaj is the **test** of the **Sufi** **claim**: whether **union** **justifies** **disclosure**, or whether **disclosure** **betrays** the **law**. The **question** is **unsettled**; the **question** is the **Sufi** **question**.
