# Sufism & 'Irfan — The Inner Path

## The Terms

- **Tasawwuf** (تَصَوُّف) — the practice; the most common word in Arabic
- **Sufism** — the English word; coined in 19th century Europe
- **'Irfan** (عرفان) — Persian/Arabic; "gnosis, knowing"; used in the Shia tradition for the inner knowledge
- **Maqamat** (مقامات) — "stations", the stages of the path
- **Ahwal** (أحوال) — "states", the spiritual conditions the practitioner passes through

## The Two Anchor Texts

### Al-Qushayri's *Risala* (1040 CE)

Abu al-Qasim al-Qushayri (986-1072) wrote the **first systematic manual of Sufism** — a guide for legal scholars who wanted to understand what the Sufis were doing. The *Risala* is structured as:

1. The **stations** (repentance, abstinence, renunciation, poverty, patience, trust, satisfaction) — these are the practices, the work
2. The **states** (proximity, intimacy, witness, certainty, love, longing, intimacy) — these are the conditions, the gifts
3. The **masters** — short biographies of the great Sufis up to al-Qushayri's time

This is the **authoritative exoteric overview** of Sufism for a generation of Sunni scholars. Al-Ghazali's *Ihya* (1095-1100) is the more famous follow-up.

### Ibn Arabi's *Fusus al-Hikam* (1220s)

Muhyi al-Din Ibn Arabi (1165-1240) wrote the **most philosophically ambitious Sufi text** — the "Bezels of Wisdom". Each bezel is a chapter about a prophet (Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, Muhammad) seen as a station of the inner path. The unifying doctrine is **Wahdat al-Wujud** ("Unity of Being") — that all of existence is theophany, the self-manifestation of God, and there is no being but God's being.

The Fusus is the **esoteric core** of Sufism, and also its most controversial work. Al-Qushayri and al-Ghazali wrote within the Shari'a; Ibn Arabi wrote **above** it. The Fusus is the modern Sufi's daily reading; it is also the most-banned Sufi text.

## The Station-State Structure

Sufism has a **station** structure (the path is a series of earned positions) and a **state** structure (the path is also a series of received conditions).

| Station (Maqam) | Practice | Earned by |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Repentance (tawba) | Turning from sin | Conscious choice |
| 2. Abstinence (zuhd) | Voluntary simplicity | Continued practice |
| 3. Renunciation (taqarrub) | Cutting attachments | Sustained discipline |
| 4. Poverty (faqr) | Not owning, depending on God | Deepened trust |
| 5. Patience (sabr) | Enduring without complaint | Test of faith |
| 6. Trust (tawakkul) | Acting as if outcome is God's | Letting go of control |
| 7. Satisfaction (rida) | Being content with whatever happens | Total submission |

The **states** (ahwal) are gifts that come to the practitioner in the stations, not by effort but by God's grace. The famous states include *qabz* (contraction), *bast* (expansion), *wajd* (ecstasy), *sahw* (sobriety), *walah* (bewilderment), and *fana'* (annihilation of the ego).

The **most important** state is *fana'* — the moment when the practitioner's sense of separate self dissolves. It is the *nigredo* of Sufism, the "dark night of the soul" of Christian mysticism, the *satori* of Zen, the *moksha* of Vedanta. Different names, same territory.

## The Tariqa Structure

A **tariqa** (طريقة, "path") is a Sufi order — a specific lineage of transmission. The major tariqas:

| Tariqa | Founder | Year | Region | Practice |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Qadiriyya | Abdul Qadir Gilani | 12th c. | Baghdad / N. Africa | Loud dhikr, hadra |
| Naqshbandiyya | Baha al-Din Naqshband | 14th c. | Central Asia | Silent dhikr, khalwa |
| Chishtiyya | Moinuddin Chishti | 12th c. | India | Sama (music), qawwali |
| Mevleviyya | Rumi | 13th c. | Turkey | Whirling, sama |
| Shadhiliyya | Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili | 13th c. | N. Africa | Silent dhikr, wirds |
| Tijaniyya | Ahmad al-Tijani | 18th c. | W. Africa | Daily wird, lazim |

Each tariqa has a **silsila** (chain) going back to the Prophet. The chain is the authority. The chain is the *isnad* (see [[01-Origins-Traditions/03-Oral-Transmission-Chains]]).

## The Wahdat al-Wujud Controversy

Ibn Arabi's doctrine of **Wahdat al-Wujud** (Unity of Being) is the most philosophically powerful and most attacked doctrine in Islamic thought. It states that:

- There is no being (*wujud*) but God's being
- All created things are **theophany** — the places where God shows Himself to Himself
- The world is a **mirror** of God
- The Sufi who realizes this has realized the truth of existence

The doctrine was attacked by later Sunni ulema (Ibn Taymiyya, al-Dhahabi) as **monism** — reducing God to the world or the world to God. The defense (by Ibn Arabi's followers, including Sadr al-Din al-Qunawi and Mulla Sadra) is that Wujud is a **method of knowing**, not a description of reality. The doctrine is preserved in the **Mevlevi**, **Naqshbandi**, **Chishti** and most other tariqas.

The modern philosophical parallel is **Spinoza's monism**, **Advaita Vedanta** (Shankara's *Brahman = Atman*), and process philosophy (Whitehead). The convergence is genuine — these traditions independently arrived at a non-dual ontology.

## The Modern Note

Sufism today has a global following estimated at 200+ million. It is the **fastest-growing** form of Islamic spirituality in the West. Modern teachers include:

- **Idries Shah** (1924-1996) — popularized Sufism in the West; the Shah name is associated with the **Suhrawardiyya** lineage
- **Cemalnur Sargut** (Turkey) — modern Mevlevi sheikha
- **Kabir Helminski** (USA) — the Threshold Society, Mevlevi lineage
- **Bawa Muhaiyaddeen** (Sri Lanka, d. 1986) — Qadiri-Muhammadiyya

The modern Sufi revival is most active in:
- Turkey (the post-Islamist Mevlevi restoration)
- Pakistan (the Barelvi movement, sufism-infused)
- The UK and France (the European Muslim diaspora)
- Indonesia and Malaysia (large traditional communities)

## The Bridge to Modern Categories

| Sufi concept | Modern equivalent |
|---|---|
| Tariqa (path) | A learning track (a school, a bootcamp, a graduate program) |
| Silsila (chain) | A verifiable lineage of teachers (the academic advisor chain) |
| Dhikr (remembrance) | A daily practice (meditation, journaling, exercise) |
| Maqam (station) | A skill level (junior, senior, principal) |
| Hal (state) | A peak experience (the "flow state" of Csikszentmihalyi) |
| Fana' (annihilation) | Ego death in trauma, in psychedelic experience, in deep meditation |
| Wahdat al-Wujud (unity of being) | Monism in philosophy, non-duality in mysticism, integrated information in consciousness theory |
