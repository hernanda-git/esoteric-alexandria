# Ibn Arabi and the Fusus al-Hikam

## The Man

**Muhyi al-Din ibn 'Arabi** (c. 1165-1240 CE), known as **al-Sheikh al-Akbar** ("The Greatest Master"), was born in **Murcia, Spain** (then a Muslim city in the **Muwahhidun** empire), into a family descended from **Hatim al-Ta'i** (the pre-Islamic Arab poet famed for generosity). The epithet **al-Akbar** is attributed to **'Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani** (the founder of the Qadiriyya) in a vision; the epithet is **the most debated** honorific in the Sufi tradition.

Ibn Arabi lived in **Seville** (the **capital** of the **Iberian** Sufi tradition), in **Tunis** (where he wrote the **Mashahid al-Asrar** and met **Ibn Hani al-Andalusi**, the court poet of the **Almohad** caliph), in **Fez** (where he met **Ibn Sab'in** and the **Muwahhidun** court), in **Baghdad** (where he was **controversial** with the **Hanbali** establishment), in **Mecca** (where he **had** the **visions** of the **Futuhat**), in **Konya** (where he **met** the **Mawlawi** tradition of **Rumi**), in **Damascus** (where he **died** in 1240 CE).

Ibn Arabi was **not** a **formal** shaykh of a **tariqa** in the **modern** sense. Ibn Arabi was **not** the **founder** of a **tariqa** (the **Akbariyya** as a **tariqa** is a **modern** construction, not a **historical** fact). Ibn Arabi was **a** **Sufi** **scholar**, a **Sufi** **poet**, a **Sufi** **philosopher**, a **Sufi** **sage** — but **not** the **founder** of a **specific** **tariqa**.

## The Central Doctrine — Wahdat al-Wujud

The **central** doctrine of Ibn Arabi is the **wahdat al-wujud** (وحدة الوجود, "the oneness of being", "the unity of existence"). The wahdat al-wujud is the **doctrine** that **being** (*wujud*) is **one**, that the **multiplicity** of **things** is **not** a **multiplicity** of **beings** but a **multiplicity** of **appearances** of the **one** being.

The wahdat al-wujud is **not** the **same** as **pantheism** (the **doctrine** that **everything** is **God**). The wahdat al-wujud is the **doctrine** that **being** is **one** but the **things** are **many**. The wahdat al-wujud is the **doctrine** that **God** is **the** **only** **reality**, that the **things** are **the** **appearances** of **God**, that the **things** are **not** **God** but are **God's** **appearances**.

The wahdat al-wujud is **not** the **same** as the **Christian** doctrine of **panentheism** (the **doctrine** that **God** is **in** **everything** and **everything** is **in** **God**). The wahdat al-wujud is **closer** to the **Neoplatonic** doctrine of **emanation** (the **One** **emanates** the **Many**, the **Many** is **the** **expression** of the **One**) but the wahdat al-wujud is **not** the **same** as the **Neoplatonic** doctrine (the **Neoplatonic** doctrine is **more** **cosmological**, the **Ibn Arabi** doctrine is **more** **mystical**).

The wahdat al-wujud is **controversial**. The **doctrine** is **rejected** by the **Hanbali** establishment, the **Ash'ari** school, the **Maturidi** school, the **Salafi** movement, the **Wahhabi** movement, the **modernist** Islam. The **doctrine** is **accepted** by the **Sufi** tradition, the **Mawlawi** tradition, the **Qadiri** tradition, the **Shadhili** tradition, the **Chishti** tradition, the **Naqshbandi** tradition, the **'Alawi** tradition, the **Darqawi** tradition, the **modernist** Sufi scholarship.

The wahdat al-wujud is **not** the **only** doctrine in the **Ibn Arabi** corpus. The **Ibn Arabi** corpus **also** includes the **wahdat al-shuhud** (the "oneness of witnessing"), the **doctrine** that **God** is **witnessed** **in** **all** **things** but the **things** are **not** **God**. The **distinction** between the **wahdat al-wujud** and the **wahdat al-shuhud** is **debated**; the **distinction** is **attributed** to **Ahmad Sirhindi** (1564-1624 CE, the **Mujaddid**, the "Renewer" of the second millennium), who **argued** that the **wahdat al-shuhud** is the **more** **orthodox** doctrine.

## The Fusus al-Hikam

The **Fusus al-Hikam** (فصوص الحكم, "The Bezels of Wisdom") is the **most quoted**, the **most studied**, the **most commented** of Ibn Arabi's works. The work is **27 chapters**, each chapter **named** after a **prophet** in the **Quran**. The 27 prophets (the **27** is the **list** in Ibn Arabi; the **Quran** names **25** **prophets** explicitly; the **additional** two are **Khidr** and **al-Khadir** as the **same** figure, and **Muhammad** as the **seal**):

1. **Adam** — the **first** **man**, the **primordial** **form** of the human being
2. **Seth** — the **inheritor** of Adam
3. **Noah** — the **preservation** of the form
4. **Idris** (Enoch) — the **first** **initiated** into the divine knowledge
5. **Abraham** — the **friend** of God
6. **Ishmael** — the **sacrifice**
7. **Isaac** — the **patience** of the form
8. **Jacob** — the **suffering** of the form
9. **Joseph** — the **beauty** of the form
10. **Job** — the **endurance** of the form
11. **Jonah** — the **swallowing** of the form
12. **Hud** — the **separation** of the form
13. **Salih** — the **vindication** of the form
14. **Lot** — the **transformation** of the form
15. **Shu'ayb** — the **subtlety** of the form
16. **Moses** — the **divine** **speech** in the form
17. **Aaron** — the **intercessor**
18. **David** — the **psalmist**
19. **Solomon** — the **king** of the form
20. **Elijah** — the **ascetic**
21. **Elisha** — the **disciple** of Elijah
22. **Ezra** — the **resurrector**
23. **Zechariah** — the **father** of John
24. **John the Baptist** — the **baptizer**
25. **Jesus** — the **word** of the form
26. **Khidr** — the **green** **one**, the **guide**
27. **Muhammad** — the **seal** of the prophets

Each chapter is a **meditation** on the **prophet** as a **form** (*qalb*, the "bezel") of the **divine** **wisdom** (*hikma*). The **wisdom** is **the** **same** **wisdom**; the **form** is **different** in each **chapter**. The **wisdom** is the **divine** **nature** of the human being; the **form** is the **prophetic** **expression** of the **wisdom** in the **specific** **prophet**.

The **central** doctrine of the **Fusus** is the **doctrine** of the **divine** **forms** (*al-suwari al-ilahiyya*). The doctrine is the **doctrine** that **each** **prophet** **is** a **divine** **form**, that the **divine** **nature** of the **human** being is **revealed** in **each** **prophet** in a **specific** **form**, that the **prophets** are the **forms** of the **divine** **wisdom** in the **world**.

The **doctrine** of the **divine** **forms** is **not** the **same** as the **Christian** doctrine of the **incarnation** (the **doctrine** that **God** **became** **flesh** in **Jesus**). The **doctrine** of the **divine** **forms** is **not** the **same** as the **Hindu** doctrine of the **avatar** (the **doctrine** that **Vishnu** **descended** in **specific** **forms**). The **doctrine** of the **divine** **forms** is the **Sufi** doctrine that **each** **prophet** is a **form** of the **divine** **wisdom** in the **world**.

## The Futuhat al-Makkiyya

The **al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya** (الفتوحات المكية, "The Meccan Illuminations", also "The Meccan Conquests") is the **magnum opus** of Ibn Arabi. The work is **560 chapters** across **~12,000 pages** (the **Mathan edition**, the standard Cairo 1911-1927 print, has 9 volumes; the **Osman Yahya edition**, the more recent critical edition, has 15+ volumes). The work was **written** over a **period** of **38 years** (1202-1240 CE), the **work** of **a lifetime**.

The **Futuhat** is the **comprehensive** **Sufi** **encyclopedia**. The **work** covers **all** **aspects** of the **Sufi** **tradition**: the **Quranic** **commentary** (the **work** contains a **complete** **commentary** on the **Quran**, **chapter** by **chapter**), the **Hadith** **commentary** (the **work** contains a **complete** **commentary** on the **Hadith** of the **prophet**), the **doctrine** of the **divine** **names** (the **work** contains a **complete** **treatment** of the **99** **names** of **God**), the **doctrine** of the **perfect** **man** (the **work** contains a **complete** **treatment** of the **perfect** **man** as the **form** of the **divine** **names** in the **world**), the **doctrine** of the **imaginal** **world** (the **work** contains a **complete** **treatment** of the **'alam al-mithal**, the **world** of the **intermediary** **images**), the **practice** of the **dhikr** (the **work** contains a **complete** **treatment** of the **dhikr** of the **divine** **names**), the **metaphysics** of the **being** (the **work** contains a **complete** **treatment** of the **doctrine** of the **oneness** of **being**).

The **Futuhat** is **not** an **easy** **work**. The **Futuhat** is **the** **most** **difficult** **Sufi** **text** in the **Sufi** **tradition**. The **Futuhat** is **written** in a **style** that is **deliberately** **obscure**; the **Futuhat** is **a** **text** that is **read** **only** by **the** **most** **advanced** **Sufi** **scholars**. The **Futuhat** is **the** **text** of the **Sufi** **tradition**, but the **Futuhat** is **not** a **text** for **beginners**.

## The Imaginal World (Alam al-Mithal)

One of **Ibn Arabi's** **most** **important** **contributions** to the **Sufi** **tradition** is the **doctrine** of the **'alam al-mithal** (عالم المثال, "the imaginal world", also "the world of similitudes"). The 'alam al-mithal is the **intermediary** **world** **between** the **world** of the **pure** **intellect** (*'aql*) and the **world** of the **senses** (*hiss*). The 'alam al-mithal is the **world** of the **images** that **have** **being** but **are** **not** **physical** (the **dream** **world**, the **vision** **world**, the **imagination** **world**).

The **'alam al-mithal** is **not** the **same** as the **physical** **world** (the **'alam al-mithal** is **not** **the** **world** of **the** **senses**). The **'alam al-mithal** is **not** the **same** as the **intellectual** **world** (the **'alam al-mithal** is **not** **the** **world** of the **pure** **intellect**). The **'alam al-mithal** is **the** **world** of the **images** that **have** **being** but **are** **not** **physical** — the **world** of the **dream**, the **vision**, the **imaginal** **experience**.

The **'alam al-mithal** is **the** **world** of the **prophetic** **visions**, the **Sufi** **dreams**, the **miraculous** **images**. The **prophets** **receive** **revelation** in the **'alam al-mithal** (the **Moses** **revelation** on the **Mount**, the **Muhammad** **revelation** in the **cave** **of** **Hira**). The **Sufis** **receive** **guidance** in the **'alam al-mithal** (the **dreams** of the **Sufis**, the **visions** of the **Sufis**, the **miraculous** **images** of the **Sufis**).

The **'alam al-mithal** is **the** **bridge** **between** the **Sufi** **metaphysics** and the **Sufi** **practice**. The **Sufi** **practice** is **based** on the **'alam al-mithal** — the **dhikr** is **the** **practice** of the **'alam al-mithal** (the **dhikr** **produces** **images** in the **'alam al-mithal**), the **vision** is **the** **practice** of the **'alam al-mithal** (the **vision** is **received** in the **'alam al-mithal**), the **dream** is **the** **practice** of the **'alam al-mithal** (the **dream** **occurs** in the **'alam al-mithal**).

## The Perfect Man (al-Insan al-Kamil)

The **doctrine** of the **al-insan al-kamil** (الإنسان الكامل, "the perfect man") is **the** **central** **doctrine** of the **Ibn Arabi** **tradition**. The **al-insan al-kamil** is **the** **human** **being** who **is** **the** **form** of **all** **the** **divine** **names**, who **is** **the** **microcosm** of **the** **macrocosm**, who **is** **the** **bridge** **between** **the** **divine** **and** **the** **world**.

The **al-insan al-kamil** is **not** **a** **specific** **historical** **person** (the **al-insan al-kamil** is **not** **the** **prophet** **Muhammad**, **although** **Muhammad** is **the** **most** **perfect** **instance** **of** the **al-insan al-kamil** in the **Ibn Arabi** **tradition**). The **al-insan al-kamil** is **the** **archetypal** **form** of **the** **human** **being**, **the** **form** **that** **each** **prophet** **embodies** **in** **the** **specific** **form** **of** **the** **prophet's** **revelation**.

The **al-insan al-kamil** is **the** **form** **of** **the** **divine** **names** **in** **the** **world**. The **al-insan al-kamil** **is** **the** **mirror** **of** **the** **divine** **attributes** **in** **the** **world**. The **al-insan al-kamil** **is** **the** **expression** **of** **the** **divine** **wisdom** **in** **the** **world**. The **al-insan al-kamil** **is** **the** **goal** **of** **the** **Sufi** **path**, the **goal** **of** **the** **Sufi** **practice**, the **goal** **of** **the** **Sufi** **life**.

The **doctrine** of the **al-insan al-kamil** is **closely** **related** to the **Jungian** **doctrine** of the **Self** (see [[18-Psychology-Jungian/03-Archetypes-And-Collective-Unconscious]]). The **two** **doctrines** **are** **not** **the** **same** (the **Jungian** **Self** is **the** **archetypal** **form** **of** **the** **psyche**; the **al-insan al-kamil** is **the** **archetypal** **form** **of** **the** **human** **being**), but the **two** **doctrines** **are** **structurally** **isomorphic** (the **two** **are** **the** **same** **form** **in** **two** **different** **vocabularies**).

## The Modern Reception

The **Ibn Arabi** **tradition** is **active** **today**. The **Ibn Arabi** **tradition** is **preserved** in **three** **modern** **forms**:

1. **The Akbarian Sufi orders** — **several** **tariqas** **trace** **their** **spiritual** **lineage** **to** **Ibn Arabi** (the **'Alawi** **tariqa** of **Algeria** and **Tunisia**, the **Darqawi** **tariqa** of **Morocco**, the **Shadhili** **tariqa** of **Egypt**, the **Naqshbandi** **tariqa** of **the** **Maghreb** and **the** **Caucasus**, the **Rifa'i** **tariqa** of **Iraq** and **Egypt**). The **Akbarian** **tariqas** **transmit** **the** **practice** **of** **the** **dhikr** **of** **the** **divine** **names** **in** **the** **specific** **form** **of** **the** **Ibn Arabi** **tradition**.
2. **The academic study of Ibn Arabi** — **the** **modern** **academic** **study** **of** **Ibn Arabi** **is** **led** **by** **William Chittick** (the **standard** **modern** **English** **scholar**), **Michel Chodkiewicz** (the **standard** **French** **scholar**), **Seyyed Hossein Nasr** (the **Iranian-American** **scholar**), **Titus Burckhardt** (the **Swiss-German** **scholar**), **Henry Corbin** (the **French** **scholar** **of** **Sufism** **and** **Shi'ism**). The **academic** **study** **is** **based** **on** **the** **Osman Yahya** **critical** **edition** **of** **the** **Futuhat** (1968-1992).
3. **The Western esoteric adoption** — **Ibn Arabi** **is** **a** **central** **figure** **in** **the** **Western** **esoteric** **tradition**. **Henry Corbin** **was** **the** **first** **to** **recognize** **the** **Ibn Arabi** **tradition** **as** **the** **central** **of** **the** **Sufi** **tradition** **in** **the** **West**. **Titus Burckhardt** **was** **the** **first** **to** **bring** **the** **Ibn Arabi** **metaphysics** **into** **the** **Western** **esoteric** **tradition** **through** **the** **Beshara** **community** (the **Beshara** **community** **was** **founded** **in** **Scotland** **in** **1972** **as** **a** **Western** **Akbarian** **community**).

The **modern** **Sufi** **scholar** **of** **Ibn Arabi** **should** **read**:

1. **The Fusus al-Hikam** (the **27** **chapters** **on** **the** **prophets**) — the **R.W.J. Austin** **English** **translation** (1980) is the **standard** **modern** **English** **translation**
2. **The Mashahid al-Asrar** (the **Sealing** **of** **the** **Spiritual** **Witnesses**) — the **partial** **English** **translation** **by** **Chittick** **in** "The Sufi Path of Knowledge" (1989)
3. **The Tarjuman al-Ashwaq** (the **Diwan** **of** **Poetry**) — the **partial** **English** **translation** **by** **Chittick** **in** "The Sufi Path of Knowledge" (1989)
4. **The al-Durra al-Fakhira** (the **Precious** **Pearl**) — the **English** **translation** **by** **Chittick** **and** **Wilson** (1982)

The **modern** **Sufi** **practitioner** **of** **the** **Ibn Arabi** **tradition** **should** **find** **a** **tariqa** **that** **transmits** **the** **Ibn Arabi** **practice** **of** **the** **dhikr** **of** **the** **divine** **names**. The **best** **modern** **options** **are** **the** **'Alawi** **tariqa** **of** **Algeria** **and** **Tunisia**, **the** **Darqawi** **tariqa** **of** **Morocco**, **the** **Beshara** **community** **of** **the** **West**, **and** **the** **'Alawi** **tariqa** **of** **the** **West** (the **'Alawi** **tariqa** **of** **the** **West** **is** **led** **by** **the** **Swiss** **shaykh** **al-** **Tijani** **of** **Geneva**).

## The Reception by the Critics

The **Ibn Arabi** **tradition** **is** **controversial**. The **critics** **of** **Ibn Arabi** **are** **many** — **the** **Hanbali** **establishment** (the **Hanbali** **reject** **Ibn Arabi** **as** **a** **heretic**, **the** **'aqida** **of** **Ibn Taymiyya** **explicitly** **rejects** **Ibn Arabi** **as** **a** **heretic**), **the** **Ash'ari** **establishment** (the **Ash'ari** **reject** **Ibn Arabi** **as** **a** **heretic** **in** **the** **form** **of** **the** **wahdat al-wujud**, **but** **accept** **the** **Ibn Arabi** **doctrine** **of** **the** **divine** **names** **in** **the** **form** **of** **the** **Ibn Arabi** **doctrine** **of** **the** **divine** **names** **as** **a** **valid** **Sufi** **doctrine**), **the** **Salafi** **movement** (the **Salafi** **reject** **Ibn Arabi** **as** **a** **heretic** **and** **a** **pagan**), **the** **Wahhabi** **movement** (the **Wahhabi** **reject** **Ibn Arabi** **as** **a** **heretic** **and** **a** **pagan**), **the** **modernist** **Islam** (the **modernist** **Islam** **rejects** **Ibn Arabi** **as** **a** **medieval** **figure** **irrelevant** **to** **the** **modern** **world**).

The **defenders** **of** **Ibn Arabi** **are** **many** — **the** **Sufi** **tradition** **as** **a** **whole** **accepts** **Ibn Arabi** **as** **the** **most** **profound** **of** **the** **Sufi** **masters**, **the** **most** **important** **of** **the** **Sufi** **metaphysicians**, **the** **most** **influential** **of** **the** **Sufi** **thinkers**. The **Sufi** **tradition** **treats** **Ibn Arabi** **as** **the** **Sufi** **counterpart** **of** **Plotinus** — **the** **Plotinus** **of** **the** **Sufi** **tradition**, **the** **Plotinus** **of** **the** **Islamic** **world**.

The **debate** **is** **not** **settled**. The **debate** **continues**. The **debate** **is** **the** **modern** **Sufi** **debate**.

## The Practice Today

The **modern** **practitioner** **of** **the** **Ibn Arabi** **tradition** **should**:

1. **Read** **the** **Fusus** **in** **the** **Austin** **translation** (the **standard** **English**)
2. **Read** **the** **Futuhat** **in** **the** **Chittick** **partial** **translations** (the **standard** **English**)
3. **Read** **the** **Chittick** **The Sufi Path of Knowledge** (the **standard** **modern** **treatment**)
4. **Find** **a** **tariqa** **that** **transmits** **the** **Ibn Arabi** **practice** **of** **the** **dhikr** **of** **the** **divine** **names** — the **'Alawi** **tariqa** **of** **Algeria** **and** **Tunisia**, **the** **Darqawi** **tariqa** **of** **Morocco**, **the** **Beshara** **community** **of** **the** **West**
5. **Practice** **the** **dhikr** **of** **the** **divine** **names** **at** **the** **specific** **form** **of** **the** **specific** **tariqa**
6. **Maintain** **the** **rabita** **with** **the** **shaykh** **at** **the** **specific** **form** **of** **the** **specific** **tariqa**
7. **Conclude** **with** **the** **specific** **form** **of** **the** **specific** **tariqa**

The **Ibn Arabi** **tradition** **is** **not** **a** **self-help** **tradition**. The **Ibn Arabi** **tradition** **is** **a** **chain** **tradition**. The **chain** **is** **the** **transmission**. The **transmission** **is** **the** **practice**. The **practice** **is** **the** **lifelong** **work**.
