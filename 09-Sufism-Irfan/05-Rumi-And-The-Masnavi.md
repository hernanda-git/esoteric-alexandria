# Rumi and the Masnavi — The Spiritual Couplets

## The Man

**Jalal al-Din Rumi** (1207-1273 CE) was born in **Balkh**, in the family of **Baha' al-Din Walad**, a respected Hanafi preacher and jurist. The family **fled** the **Mongol** advance around 1219-1220, travelling through **Nishapur** (where the young Jalal met the poet **Attar**, who reportedly recognized his destiny), **Baghdad**, **Mecca**, and finally **Konya**, the capital of the **Sultanate of Rum**, where Baha' al-Din died in 1231. Rumi succeeded his father as a religious teacher and became a formidable **jurist** and **preacher** — before his life was **overturned** by a meeting.

In **1244** Rumi met **Shams-i Tabrizi**, a wandering dervish of fierce intensity and dubious orthodoxy. The encounter is the **turning point** of the tradition: Rumi abandoned his legal career for **poetry** and **mystical** union. When Shams **disappeared** (probably murdered by jealous disciples) in 1247, Rumi's **grief** became the **source** of the lyric **Divan-e Shams** and, later, the didactic **Masnavi**. Rumi died in **Konya** in 1273; his **death-anniversary** (17 December) is kept by the Mevlevis as the **Seb-i Arus** ("the wedding night" — his union with the Beloved).

## The Masnavi

The **Masnavi-ye Ma'navi** (مثنوی معنوی, "The Spiritual Couplets") is the **magnum opus** of Rumi and, with the **Quran** and the **Shahnameh**, one of the three books every Persianate household was said to own. It is **six books** (*dafters*), **more than 25,000 couplets**, dictated in **rhyming** **mathnawi** meter to his scribe **Husam al-Din Chelebi** beginning around **1258**. Rumi called it "the **roots** of the **roots** of the **roots** of the **faith**" — the esoteric **commentary** on the **outer** law.

The Masnavi is **not** a **systematic** treatise. It is a **patchwork** of **stories**, **fables**, **Quranic** exegesis, **hadith**, **jokes**, and **doctrine**, held together by the **logic** of the **heart** rather than the **line** of the **argument**. Each **story** is a **shell**; the **meaning** is the **kernel**. The famous **opening** — the **lament** of the **reed flute** (*ney*) cut from its **reed-bed** — is the **archetypal** image of the **soul** separated from its **source**, longing to **return**.

## The Central Doctrine — 'Ishq (Love)

The **central** force of Rumi's teaching is **'ishq** (عشق, "love" — intense, annihilating love). **'Ishq** is the **cause** of the **world**: God **loved** to be **known**, and so **created** the **creation** as a **mirror**. The **path** is not **ascetic** withdrawal alone but **total** **surrender** to the **Beloved**; the **self** (*nafs*) must be **slain** that the **real** **self** may **live**. This is the **fana** (annihilation) of the Sufi stations, here rendered as **love's** **death** and **resurrection**.

Rumi's **love** is **not** sentimentality. It is the **cosmic** **eros** that **drives** the **planets** and the **atoms**. "**Love** is the **astrolabe** of the **mysteries** of God" — the **heart** is the **instrument** by which the **hidden** is **read**, just as the **astrolabe** reads the **heavens**. The **intellect** ('aql) is a **candle**; **love** is the **moth** that **burns** in it and **sees** what the **candle** cannot.

## The Sama' and the Mevlevi Order

The **sama'** (سماع, "audition" — the **listening** to **music** and **poetry** that induces the **mystical** **state**) is the **praxis** Rumi made **famous**. The **whirling** (*devr*), performed by the **Mevlevi** dervishes (founded by Rumi's son **Sultan Walad** and systematized by **Celebi** Husam al-Din and **Pir** Adil), is the **embodiment** of the **cosmic** **rotation**: the **left** hand receives from **heaven**, the **right** gives to **earth**; the **turning** around the **heart** mirrors the **turning** of the **spheres**.

The **Mevlevi** order became the **most** **visible** Sufi **tariqa** in the **Ottoman** world and, later, the **global** **emblem** of **Sufism**. The **sama'** is **not** mere **entertainment**: it is a **rigorous** **discipline** requiring **ritual** **purity**, a **master** (*sheikh*), and a **defined** **sequence** of the **four** **salaams** (greetings). The **dance** is the **prayer** made **visible**.

## The Modern Reception

Rumi is the **best-selling** **poet** in the **United States** today, largely through the **free** **renderings** of **Coleman Barks** (who works from **Nicholson's** **English**, not the **Persian**). The **Barks** **versions** are **beautiful** but **detheologized** — they **strip** the **Islamic** **frame** and present Rumi as a **universal** **mystic**. The **scholarly** **tradition** (Nicholson, Mojaddedi, Lewis, Schimmel, Chittick) restores the **Quranic** and **Sufi** **context**: Rumi is **unthinkable** outside **Islam** and the **Sufi** **science** of the **stations**.

The **Mevlevi** order was **officially** **abolished** in **Turkey** in **1925** (along with all **tariqas**); it **survives** today as a **cultural** **foundation** and a **UNESCO** **heritage** **performance**, while **living** **Mevlevi** **practice** continues **underground** and in the **diaspora**. The **gap** between the **popular** **Rumi** and the **traditional** **Mevlevi** is the **central** **problem** of the **modern** **reception**.
