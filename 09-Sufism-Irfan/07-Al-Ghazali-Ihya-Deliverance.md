# Al-Ghazali and the Revival — From Doubt to the Ihya

## The Man

**Abu Hamid Muhammad al-Ghazali** (1058-1111 CE), known as **Hujjat al-Islam** ("the Proof of Islam"), was a **Persian** theologian, jurist, and Sufi. Born in **Tus** (Khorasan), he studied in **Nishapur** under the Ash'arite master **al-Juwayni**. In **1091** the Vizier **Nizam al-Mulk** appointed him head of the **Nizamiyya madrasa** in **Baghdad** — the most prestigious post in the Sunni world. At the **height** of his fame, Ghazali **collapsed**.

## The Crisis and the Munqidh

In **1095** Ghazali suffered a **spiritual crisis**: a **doubt** so total that he **lost** the power of speech and could **no longer** teach. The crisis is described in his **autobiography**, **al-Munqidh min al-Dalal** ("Deliverance from Error"). He examined the **four** sources of knowledge — the **taqlid** (blind imitation) of the schools, the **ta'lim** (authoritative instruction) of the Ismailis, the **philosophical** demonstration, and the **Sufi** **direct experience** (*dhawq*, "taste"). He found the **first** three **inadequate**: imitation is **not** knowledge; authority is **not** certainty; philosophy **concludes** only to the **possible**, never the **necessary**.

Only the **Sufi** method — the **discipline** of the **heart**, the **purification** of the **soul**, the **direct** tasting of the **real** — yields **certainty**. Ghazali **resigned** his post, traveled to **Damascus**, **Jerusalem**, **Mecca**, and **Medina**, and spent **eleven** years in **retreat** and **Sufi** practice before returning to teach in **Tus**. The Munqidh is the **classic** account of a **rationalist's** conversion to **experiential** mysticism.

## The Ihya' — The Revival of the Religious Sciences

Ghazali's **magnum opus** is the **Ihya' 'Ulum al-Din** ("Revival of the Religious Sciences"), a **forty-book** work organized in **four quarters**:

1. **'Ibadat** (worship) — the outer and inner meaning of the ritual pillars
2. **'Adat** (customary matters) — the ethics of the everyday, eating, marriage, earning
3. **Muhlikat** (the destructive) — the vices that **kill** the heart (anger, envy, worldliness)
4. **Munjiyat** (the salvific) — the virtues that **save** the heart (patience, gratitude, hope, love)

The **Ihya'** is the **synthesis** of the **Sharia** (exoteric law) and the **Haqiqa** (esoteric reality). Ghazali **demonstrates** that the **outer** act is **dead** without the **inner** intention, and that the **Sufi** **interiority** is the **completion**, not the **abandonment**, of the **law**. The work is the **most widely read** book in the Islamic world after the **Quran**. The work is the **bridge** that made **Sufism** **acceptable** to **orthodox** Sunni Islam.

## The Tahafut — The Incoherence of the Philosophers

Before his crisis Ghazali wrote the **Tahafut al-Falasifa** ("Incoherence of the Philosophers"), a **critique** of **Avicenna** (Ibn Sina) and **al-Farabi**. Ghazali argued the **philosophers** erred on **three** points that touch **unbelief**: the **eternity** of the world (vs. creation *ex nihilo*), God's **knowledge** of **particulars**, and the **bodily** resurrection. On **twenty** points he charged them with **error**; on **three** with **unbelief**.

The Tahafut **triggered** **Averroes'** (Ibn Rushd) **Tahafut al-Tahafut** ("Incoherence of the Incoherence"), the great **defense** of philosophy. The exchange is the **central** debate of **medieval** Islamic thought. The debate is the **boundary** between **reason** and **revelation** in the tradition.

## The Historical Reach

Ghazali's **status** in Sunni Islam is **near-canonical** — "if there had been a prophet after Muhammad, it would have been Ghazali," runs the saying. His **Ihya'** shaped **later** Sufism, fed **Ibn Arabi's** synthesis, and reached the **Latin** West through the **Maqasid al-Falasifa** ("Intentions of the Philosophers"), a summary that **Thomas Aquinas** knew. His **Deliverance** anticipates the **methodic doubt** of **Descartes** by **six centuries** — a **modern** philosopher who noted the parallel was **Descartes'** editor.

## The Modern Mapping

Ghazali **maps** onto modern categories:

1. **Epistemology of doubt** — the **Munqidh** is a **case study** in the **limits** of **authority** and **reason**, and a **model** for the **intellectual** **humility** required before **certainty** can be **earned**.
2. **Integration of reason and spirituality** — Ghazali refuses the **split** between **science** and **meaning**; his **critique** of "scientism" (the claim that **demonstration** alone yields the **whole** truth) is **live** in **modern** debates.
3. **Reparative ethics** — the **Ihya'**'s turn from the **outer** to the **inner** is a **framework** for **authenticity** and **moral** **formation** in **secular** as well as **religious** life.

The Ghazalian **synthesis** is the **reason** Sufism **survived** within **orthodox** Islam. The synthesis is the **template** for the **interior** reading of **exterior** practice.
