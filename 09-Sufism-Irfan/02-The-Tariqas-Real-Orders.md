# The Sufi Tariqas — The Real Orders

## The Definition

A **tariqa** (Arabic طريق, "path" or "way") is a **Sufi order** — a **chain** (*silsila*) of **master to disciple** (*shaykh to murid*) that **transmits** the **practices**, the **teachings**, and the **authority** of a particular **founder** (*wali* or *imam al-tariqa*).

The tariqa is **not** an "organization" in the modern sense. The tariqa is a **chain of persons** that **transmits** a **practice**. The chain is **personal**; the chain is **face-to-face**; the chain is **lifelong**. The chain does not **end** with the death of the founder; the chain **continues** through the **generations**.

The tariqa is **not** the **same** as the **Sufi tradition** as a whole. The tariqa is **one** of the **chains** in the **Sufi tradition**. The Sufi tradition is the **aggregate** of the **tariqas**. A Sufi who is **not** a member of a **tariqa** is a **mutasawwif** (a Sufi at large), not a **Sufi** in the **technical** sense. The technical sense requires the **chain**.

The tariqa is **not** a "cult" in the modern pejorative sense. The tariqa is the **traditional** Islamic **structure** of the **esoteric** transmission. The tariqa is **recognized** by the **orthodox** Islam as a **valid** form of the **Sunna**. The orthodox critique of the tariqa is the **innovation** (*bid'a*) critique, which is the **reformist** critique, not the **orthodox** critique.

## The Number of Tariqas

There are **more than 200** Sufi tariqas in the **modern** world (Trimingham, *Sufi Orders*, gives a list of 189; the actual number is **larger** because the **regional** tariqas are not all **documented** in the **modern** scholarship). The **major** tariqas are **around 12**; the **historically** important tariqas are **around 30**.

The tariqas are **not** equally **active**. The **largest** tariqas by membership are the **Naqshbandiyya** (estimated 30-50 million members worldwide, the largest), the **Qadiriyya** (estimated 20-30 million), the **Shadhiliyya** (estimated 10-20 million), the **Chishtiyya** (estimated 5-10 million, concentrated in South Asia), the **Tijaniyya** (estimated 5-10 million, concentrated in West Africa), the **Mawlawiyya** (estimated 1-3 million, concentrated in Turkey, the "Whirling Dervishes"), the **Bektashiyya** (estimated 1-3 million, concentrated in the Balkans, Albania, and Turkey).

## The Major Tariqas

### The Qadiriyya (founded c. 1166 CE)

The **Qadiriyya** is the **oldest** of the **modern** tariqas. The tariqa is **founded** by **'Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani** (1078-1166 CE) in **Baghdad**. Al-Jilani is a **Hanbali** scholar, a **traditionalist**, and a **preacher**; the tariqa is the **transmission** of his **practice**.

The tariqa is **known** for:

- The **dhikr** (the remembrance) — the **collective** chanting of the **dhikr** in the **masjid** or the **zawiya**, the **loud** dhikr, the **standing** dhikr
- The **wird** — the **daily** dhikr recitation, the **specific** formulas (the *wird al-layl* for the night, the *wird al-sabah* for the morning)
- The **Qadiri doctrine** of **tawassul** (the **intercession** through the **saint**, the **prophet**, the **names of God**)
- The **Qadiri doctrine** of **tawakkul** (the **reliance** on God, the **surrender** of the **means** to the **end**)

The tariqa is **global**: the tariqa is **active** in **Baghdad**, **Damascus**, **Cairo**, **Istanbul**, **the Balkans**, **South Asia**, **East Africa**, **West Africa**, **North America**, **Europe**. The tariqa is **especially** strong in **Senegal** (the **Qadiriyya of the Mourides** is a **branch** of the **Qadiriyya**, the most important Sufi tariqa in Senegal, the 14-million-strong *Murid* order founded c. 1887 by **Ahmad Bamba**).

### The Naqshbandiyya (founded c. 1380s CE)

The **Naqshbandiyya** is the **largest** of the **modern** tariqas. The tariqa is **named** after **Baha al-Din Naqshband** (1318-1389 CE) of **Bukhara** (modern Uzbekistan), although the tariqa's **formal** structure is **attributed** to **'Abd al-Khaliq al-Ghujdawani** (c. 1175-1220 CE) and **Yusuf al-Hamadani** (1141-1231 CE). The tariqa is **systematized** by **Baha al-Din Naqshband**, who **gave** the tariqa the **specific** form.

The tariqa is **known** for:

- The **silent dhikr** (*dhikr khafi*) — the **quiet**, the **internal** dhikr, the dhikr that is **not spoken** but **held** in the **heart**
- The **rabita** — the **spiritual connection** with the **shaykh** that is **maintained** even in **physical** absence
- The **suluk** — the **path** of the **Naqshbandi** practice, the **specific** set of the **stages** of the **Naqshbandi** way
- The **Naqshbandi doctrine** of **lawa'im** (the **inspirations**), the **spiritual openings** (*futuuhat*) that the **practitioner** **receives** on the path

The tariqa is **especially** influential in **Central Asia**, **South Asia** (the **Naqshbandi-Mujaddidi** branch founded by **Ahmad Sirhindi** (1564-1624 CE) is the **dominant** tariqa in **Pakistan** and **Bengal**), **Turkey** (the **Naqshbandi-Haqqani** branch founded by **Mehmed Zahid Kotku** (1897-1980) is the **dominant** tariqa in **Turkey**, the **inspiration** for the **Gulen** movement), **Chechnya** and **the Caucasus**, **Indonesia** (the **Naqshbandi** tariqa is the **most common** tariqa in **Aceh**, **the spiritual capital** of the Indonesian Islamic world), **Myanmar** (the **Naqshbandi** is the **only** active tariqa), **Syria**, **Iraq**, **the West**.

### The Shadhiliyya (founded c. 1258 CE)

The **Shadhiliyya** is the **largest** of the tariqas in **North Africa** and the **Shafi'i** world. The tariqa is **founded** by **Abu al-Hasan al-Shadhili** (1196-1258 CE) of **Tunisia** (and the **tomb** in **Humaithara**, Egypt). The tariqa is **especially** associated with the **Shafi'i** school of *fiqh* and the **Ash'ari** school of *kalām*.

The tariqa is **known** for:

- The **silent dhikr** (the **same** as the **Naqshbandi** silent dhikr, but the **Shadhili** silent dhikr is **not** the **same** as the **Naqshbandi** silent dhikr in the **specific** form)
- The **wird al-Shadhili** — the **specific** formulas of the **daily** dhikr, the **short** wird (the **Shadhili** tariqa is **known** for the **brevity** of the **wird**)
- The **Shadhili doctrine** of **khuluq** (the **character** of the **servant** of God), the **ethical** emphasis of the tariqa
- The **Shadhili doctrine** of **sabr** (the **patience**), the **endurance** of the **practitioner** in the **face** of the **ordeal**

The tariqa is **active** in **Egypt**, **Libya**, **Tunisia**, **Algeria**, **Morocco**, **Syria**, **Iraq**, **Yemen**, **South Asia** (the **Shadhili** tariqa is the **most common** tariqa in **Sri Lanka**, **the Maldives**, **the southern Indian state of Kerala**), **Southeast Asia** (the **Shadhili** tariqa is the **most common** tariqa in **Java**, **Malaysia**, **Brunei**). The tariqa is the **tariqa** of the **al-Buni** (the **Shams al-Ma'arif** is the **Shadhili** handbook; see [[02-Hurufiyya-Lettrism/05-Shams-al-Maarif-Actual-Structure]]).

### The Mawlawiyya / Mevlevi Order (founded c. 1307 CE)

The **Mawlawiyya** (Turkish "Mevlevi") is the tariqa of **Jalal al-Din al-Rumi** (1207-1273 CE), the **Maulana** ("our master") of **Konya**, Turkey. The tariqa is **founded** by **Rumi's** son **Sultan Walad** (c. 1226-1312 CE) and **formalized** by **Rumi's** grandson **'Ulu Arif Chalabi** (d. 1320 CE). The tariqa is **known** as the **Whirling Dervishes** for the **specific** form of the **dhikr** — the **whirling** (*sema*) that the **practitioner** performs as a **form** of the **dhikr**.

The tariqa is **known** for:

- The **sema** — the **whirling** dhikr, the **specific** form of the **ritual** that the **Mawlawi** performs on the **specific** days (the **Maulid** of Rumi on 17 December, the **Maulid** of Shams al-Din Tabrizi on 23 December, the **specific** weekly *sema* on Saturday evenings at the **Mevlana** museum in **Konya**)
- The **Masnavi** — Rumi's **6-book poem** (the *Mathnawi-yi Ma'nawi*, "Couplets of Deep Meaning"), the **central** text of the tariqa, the **"Quran in Persian"** for the **Mawlawi**
- The **Mawlawi doctrine** of **'ishq** (love), the **central** Rumi theme — the **love** of God, the **love** of the **master**, the **love** of the **beloved**
- The **Mawlawi doctrine** of **the reed-flute** (the *ney*), the **specific** form of the **Mawlawi** music

The tariqa is **active** in **Turkey** (the **most** active), **Iran**, **Afghanistan**, **Syria**, **Egypt**, **the Balkans** (the **Mawlawi** tariqa is **active** in **Albania**, **Macedonia**, **Greece**), **Europe**, **North America**. The tariqa was **banned** in **Turkey** in 1925 by **Atatürk** (along with **all** the Sufi tariqas); the tariqa was **re-opened** in 1957 (limited), **opened** more fully in the 1990s.

### The Chishtiyya (founded c. 1221 CE)

The **Chishtiyya** is the **largest** of the tariqas in **South Asia** (Pakistan, India, Bangladesh). The tariqa is **founded** by **Abu Ishaq al-Chishti** (c. 968-1037 CE) of **Chisht** (modern Afghanistan); the tariqa is **formalized** in **South Asia** by **Moinuddin Chishti** (1141-1236 CE) of **Ajmer** (modern India). The tariqa is the **most popular** tariqa in **South Asia** because of the **emphasis** on **samā'** (the **listening**, the **music**, the **devotional** song).

The tariqa is **known** for:

- The **samā'** — the **listening** dhikr, the **devotional** song, the **specific** form of the **Qawwali** music that the **Chishti** tariqa is **especially** known for
- The **muraqaba** — the **silent** meditation, the **specific** form of the **sitting** practice of the **Chishti** tariqa (the **muraqaba** is **similar** to the **Naqshbandi** silent dhikr)
- The **service** (*khidmat*) — the **emphasis** on the **service** of the **community**, the **free** food (*langar*) that the **Chishti** *dargah* (tomb-shrine) **serves** to **all** visitors, the **emphasis** on the **social** work
- The **Chishti doctrine** of **tawaja** (the **tolerance**), the **doctrine** of the **tolerance** of the **Chishti** tariqa for **all** religions

The tariqa is **active** in **Ajmer** (the **central** shrine), **Delhi** (the **Nizamuddin** shrine, the tomb of **Nizamuddin Auliya** (1238-1325 CE)), **the Punjab**, **Sindh**, **Bengal**, **the Deccan** (the **Gulbarga** branch, founded by **Gesu Daraz** (1321-1422 CE)), **Myanmar** (the **Chishti** is the **second** largest tariqa in **Myanmar**), **the Maldives** (the **only** tariqa in the **Maldives**), **the United States** (the **Cleveland** mosque, the **Cincinnati** mosque, the **California** mosques).

### The Tijaniyya (founded c. 1782 CE)

The **Tijaniyya** is the **largest** of the tariqas in **West Africa** (Senegal, Mali, Mauritania, Niger, Nigeria, Burkina Faso, Ghana, Guinea, Côte d'Ivoire, Cameroon, Chad, Sudan). The tariqa is **founded** by **Ahmad al-Tijani** (1737-1815 CE) of **Ain Madhi, Algeria**. The tariqa is **distinct** from the other tariqas in **several** ways:

- The tariqa is **not** based on a **chain** that **precedes** the **founder**. The founder **claimed** that the **chain** was **transmitted** to him by the **prophet** in a **vision** (the *khatm al-wilaya*, the "seal of sainthood", the **specific** claim that the **Tijaniyya** founder is the **seal of the saints**, the **last** of the **saints**)
- The tariqa **replaces** the **chains** of the **other** tariqas (a Tijani is **not** supposed to take **multiple** tariqas; the Tijani is **supposed** to **commit** to the **Tijaniyya** only)
- The tariqa is **especially** strong in **Senegal** (the **Tijaniyya** of the **Sy** family, the **Niassene Tijaniyya** of **Ibrahim Niasse** (1900-1975 CE), the **dominant** tariqa in Senegal)
- The tariqa is **especially** strong in **the Maghreb** (Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia), **the Sudan**, **the Sahel**

The tariqa is **known** for:

- The **wird al-Tijani** — the **specific** formulas of the **daily** dhikr, the **collective** recitation of the *Lā ilāha illā Allāh* in the **loud** form
- The **Tijani doctrine** of **khatm al-wilaya** (the **seal** of **sainthood**), the **doctrine** that the **Tijaniyya** founder is the **seal** of the **saints** (the **doctrine** is **controversial** with the **other** Sufi tariqas)
- The **Tijani doctrine** of **al-sayr ilā Allāh bi-Allāh** (the **journey** to **God** by **God**), the **specific** form of the **Tijani** spiritual path
- The **Tijani doctrine** of the **futuuhat** (the **spiritual openings**), the **specific** form of the **Tijani** mystical experience

### The Rifaiyya (founded c. 1186 CE)

The **Rifaiyya** is the tariqa of **Ahmad al-Rifa'i** (c. 1108-1182 CE) of **Basra, Iraq**. The tariqa is **known** for the **extreme** forms of the **dhikr** — the **loud** dhikr, the **piercing** dhikr, the **fire-eating** dhikr (the *darwish* of the **Rifaiyya** is **known** for **swallowing** glass, **piercing** the body with **iron**, **walking** on **fire** — the **specific** form of the **dhikr** that the **Rifaiyya** is **most** known for).

The tariqa is **active** in **Egypt** (the **most** active), **the Sudan**, **the Maghreb** (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia), **the Balkans** (Albania, Macedonia), **South Asia** (the **Rifaiyya** is **active** in **Bengal**), **Turkey** (the **Rifaiyya** is the **most popular** tariqa in **Turkey** in the **pre-Atatürk** period), **Europe** (the **Albanian** diaspora).

### The Khalwatiyya (founded c. 1250 CE)

The **Khalwatiyya** is the tariqa of **'Umar al-Khalwati** (c. 1210-1249 CE) of **Cairo**. The tariqa is **active** in **Egypt**, **Turkey**, **the Balkans** (the **Khalwatiyya** is the **most popular** tariqa in **the Balkans** in the **Ottoman** period), **the Maghreb**, **the Sudan**, **the Caucasus**. The tariqa is **known** for the **silent** dhikr (the **specific** form of the **silent** dhikr that the **Khalwatiyya** is **most** known for).

## The Branches

The **branches** of the tariqas are the **sub-orders** that **branch** from the **main** tariqa. The **branch** is **founded** by a **prominent** member of the **main** tariqa, the **branch** is **transmitted** through a **specific** chain, the **branch** is **recognized** by the **main** tariqa as **valid**.

The **branches** are **many**. The **Naqshbandiyya** has **at least 30** **major** branches (the **Mujaddidiyya**, the **Khwajagan-Mahmudiyya**, the **Haqqaniyya**, the **Tajaddudiyya**, the **Suhrawardiyya-Mu'iniyya**, etc.). The **Qadiriyya** has **at least 20** **major** branches (the **Mouride** of Senegal, the **Ibrahimiyya** of Iraq, the **'Arusi** of Syria, etc.). The **Shadhiliyya** has **at least 15** **major** branches (the **Darqawiyya** of Morocco, the **'Alawiyya** of Algeria, the **Nimr** of Egypt, etc.).

The **branches** are **not** the **same** as the **main** tariqa. The **branch** has **specific** doctrines, **specific** practices, **specific** chains. The **branch** is **sometimes** in **conflict** with the **main** tariqa (the **Murid** branch of the **Qadiriyya** is **independent** of the **main** Qadiriyya, the **Murid** is **technically** a **branch** but **practically** an **independent** tariqa).

## The Organization

The **tariqa** is **organized** around the **shaykh** (the **master**). The **shaykh** is the **head** of the **tariqa** (or the **branch** of the tariqa). The **shaykh** is the **transmitter** of the **practice** and the **authority**. The **shaykh** is **appointed** by the **previous shaykh** at the **death** of the **previous shaykh** (the **chain** is **unbroken**).

The **shaykh** is **served** by the **muqaddam** (the **representative**), the **khalifa** (the **deputy**), the **mudabbir** (the **administrator**). The **muqaddam** is **appointed** by the **shaykh** to **represent** the shaykh in **specific** region. The **khalifa** is **appointed** to **deputize** the shaykh in the **specific** practice (the *wird*, the *dhikr*). The **mudabbir** is **appointed** to **administer** the **practical** affairs of the **tariqa** (the *zawiya*, the *khanqah*, the *langar*).

The **practitioner** (the **murid** or the **salik**) is **bound** to the **shaykh** by the **baya** (the **oath of allegiance**). The **baya** is **taken** at the **specific** form, the **specific** ceremony, the **specific** form of the **hand** (the **shaykh** holds the **hand** of the **practitioner**). The **baya** is **lifelong**. The **baya** is **not** broken by **distance** (the **baya** is **maintained** by the **rabita**). The **baya** is **broken** only by **death** or by **explicit** **release** by the **shaykh**.

## The Practice Today

The **modern** Sufi tariqas are **active**. The **tariqas** are **not** a **historical** phenomenon. The **tariqas** are **present-day** **organizations** with **living** shaykhs, **living** chains, **living** practices.

The **modern** practice of the **tariqas** is **controversial** in the **modern** Islamic world. The **reformist** Islam (the **Salafist**, the **Wahhabi**, the **Ikhwan**) **rejects** the **tariqas** as **innovation** (*bid'a*) and **polytheism** (*shirk*). The **reformist** critique is **strongest** in **Saudi Arabia** (the **Wahhabi** state **banned** the **tariqas** in the **early** 20th c.), **the Gulf**, **Egypt** (the **Al-Azhar** has **oscillated** between **acceptance** and **rejection** of the **tariqas**), **Pakistan** (the **madrasa** reformist tradition **rejects** the **tariqas**), **Bangladesh** (the **Bangladeshi** reformist tradition **rejects** the **tariqas**), **the United Kingdom** (the **Salafi-Qadiri** tension is **active**).

The **tariqas** are **strongest** in the **traditional** Islamic world (the **Maghreb**, **the Sahel**, **West Africa**, **East Africa**, **South Asia**, **Indonesia**, **Malaysia**, **the Caucasus**, **the Balkans**, **Turkey**, **Egypt**), in the **diaspora** (the **South Asian** diaspora in **the United Kingdom** and **North America**, the **West African** diaspora in **Europe** and **North America**, the **Albanian** diaspora in **Europe** and **North America**), and in the **West** (the **United States**, the **United Kingdom**, **France**, **Germany**, **the Netherlands**, **Australia**).

The **modern** **practitioner** of the **tariqas** should:

1. **Find** a **shaykh** of a **specific** tariqa (a **Naqshbandi** shaykh, a **Shadhili** shaykh, a **Qadiri** shaykh)
2. **Take** the **baya** at the **specific** form of the **specific** tariqa
3. **Practice** the **wird** at the **specific** form of the **specific** tariqa
4. **Maintain** the **rabita** with the **shaykh** at the **specific** form of the **specific** tariqa
5. **Conclude** with the **specific** form of the **specific** tariqa

The **tariqa** is **not** a **self-help** group. The tariqa is a **chain**. The chain is the **transmission**. The transmission is the **practice**. The practice is the **lifelong** work.
