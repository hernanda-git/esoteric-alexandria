# Tantra — Left-Hand and Right-Hand Paths

## The Term

**Tantra** (तन्त्र, "loom", "weave", "continuum") is the umbrella term for a set of religious-philosophical traditions in India, Nepal, Tibet, and (through diffusion) Southeast Asia, China, Korea, and Japan, that flourished from the **5th to the 12th century CE** and continue to the present.

The Tantric traditions are **Buddhist** (Vajrayana, Mantrayana), **Hindu** (Shaiva, Shakta, Vaishnava), and **Jain**. The **Tantric period** is the third great phase of Indian religion, after the **Vedic** period (1500-600 BCE) and the **Upanishadic / Epic** period (600 BCE-400 CE).

The Western term "Tantra" has been narrowed to refer to the **Shakta** (goddess-worshipping) and **Vajrayana** Buddhist traditions, and especially to the **sexual** aspects of the practice. This is a **misreading**. The sexual elements are a small (and very advanced) part of the Tantric repertoire; the majority of Tantric practice is **non-sexual**.

## The Two Paths

The Tantric traditions are organized into **two main paths** (sometimes three):

### 1. Dakshinamarga (दक्षिणमार्ग, "Right-Hand Path")

- **Conservative**, **conformist**, **orthodox**
- Adheres to the **caste system**, the **Vedic** ritual, the **social norms** of the tradition
- The deities are **public** (Shiva, Vishnu, the goddess)
- The practice is **exoteric** in the sense that it does not require the breaking of social taboos
- The goal is the same as the non-Tantric tradition: *moksha* (liberation)
- The classical Shaiva Siddhanta of South India is the most prominent right-hand tradition

### 2. Vamamarga (वाममार्ग, "Left-Hand Path")

- **Transgressive**, **non-conformist**, **anti-orthodox**
- Deliberately **violates** social taboos: alcohol (*madya*), meat (*mamsa*), fish (*matsya*), and sexual union (*maithuna*) — the **pancha makara** (the "five M's")
- The deities are often **"left-hand" deities** — the fierce, the terrifying, the goddess in her terrible aspects (Kali, Bhairava, Chamunda, the Dakinis)
- The practice is **esoteric** in the sense that it requires initiation into the *sadhaka* (practitioner) lineage
- The goal is **transformative** — the *jivanmukti* (liberation while alive) in this body

The Western popular "Tantric sex" tradition draws almost exclusively on the **Left-Hand Path**, but misinterprets the sexual practice as **pleasure** rather than as **transformation**. The classical Left-Hand practice is **ritual**, **sacred**, and **requires a teacher**.

## The Central Practices

The Tantric traditions have a **core repertoire** of practices. The major ones:

### 1. Mantra (मन्त्र)

A **mantra** is a **sacred syllable, word, or phrase** that is **charged with spiritual power**. The practitioner repeats the mantra (or holds it in the mind) for the duration of the practice. The mantra is the **instrument** of transformation.

The mantras are of various types:
- **Bija mantras** — seed syllables (e.g., **Om**, **Hrim**, **Shrim**, **Klim**, **AIM**)
- **Nama mantras** — the name of the deity (e.g., **Om Namah Shivaya**)
- **Dhyani mantras** — the meditation mantras
- **Kundalini mantras** — the activation mantras

The **guru mantra** is the mantra given to the student at initiation. It is **secret**, **personal**, and **never** revealed to others.

### 2. Yantra (यन्त्र)

A **yantra** is a **geometric diagram** that represents the deity. The yantra is a **mandala** of the deity's cosmic form. The practitioner **looks at**, **meditates on**, or **worships** the yantra.

The most famous yantra is the **Shri Yantra** (the yantra of the goddess Tripura Sundari), the **geometric structure** of 9 interlocking triangles (4 pointing down, 5 pointing up) surrounded by concentric circles and a square.

The yantra is the **visual** counterpart of the mantra. The yantra is to the eye what the mantra is to the ear.

### 3. Mandala (मण्डल)

A **mandala** is a **circular diagram** that represents the **cosmos** and the **deity's palace**. The practitioner is placed at the center; the practitioner is the deity. The mandala is the **architectural** counterpart of the yantra.

The Vajrayana Buddhist mandala tradition is the most developed. The practitioner is **initiated** into a specific mandala and is placed in the **appropriate position**.

### 4. Puja (पूजा)

**Puja** is the **ritual worship** of the deity. The classical Tantric puja has 16 "limbs" (the *shodasha upachara*): invitation, seat, washing the feet, offering water, offering clothes, offering scents, offering flowers, offering incense, offering lamp, offering food, offering betel, offering water for the mouth, offering camphor, offering the circumambulation, offering the mantra, and dismissal.

The 16-limbs puja is the **outer ritual** of the Tantric tradition. The **inner puja** is the visualization of the 16 limbs as the 16 stages of the practitioner's spiritual journey.

### 5. Yantra-Puja (the Outer Puja of the Subtle Body)

The **yantra-puja** is the **internalized** puja — the worship is performed on the **subtle body**, not on a physical image. The practitioner visualizes the deity in the **heart** and offers the 16 limbs **mentally**.

The yantra-puja is the **meditation** form of the puja. It is the **higher** of the two.

## The Subtle Body

The Tantric traditions have an **elaborate model of the subtle body** (see [[01-Seven-Chakras]]). The subtle body is the **vehicle** of the practice. The practitioner **works on** the subtle body through the mantras, yantras, and meditations.

The **Kundalini** (the dormant power at the base of the spine) is **raised** through the 7 chakras to the crown. The raising of the Kundalini is the **central practice** of the left-hand traditions.

## The Modern Note

The Tantric tradition is **alive** in modern India, Nepal, Tibet, and the West:

1. **The Hindu Tantric tradition** — the **Shakta** (goddess) tradition of Bengal and Assam, the **Kashmir Shaivism** of northern India, the **Kaula** and **Kapalika** traditions, the **Bengal Vaishnavism** of Chaitanya Mahaprabhu, the **Tantric Christianity** of Brazil
2. **The Vajrayana Buddhist tradition** — the Tibetan **Nyingma**, **Kagyu**, **Sakya**, **Gelug** schools, the **Newar** tradition of Nepal, the **Shingon** tradition of Japan
3. **The Western "Tantra" tradition** — the modern "Neo-Tantra" of Margot Anand, the "Tantric sex" tradition of Osho, the "Tantric Christianity" of the School of the Holy Spirit
4. **The modern academic study** — the **Tantric Studies** field (Alexis Sanderson, David Gordon White, Hugh Urban, Mark Dyczkowski, Srilata Raman) has produced a sophisticated study of the tradition

The library's category 14 treats the Tantric tradition as the **most sophisticated** of the Indian esoteric traditions, and as the **parallel** of the Western ceremonial magic tradition. The two traditions are **structurally similar** (the visualization, the mantra, the mandala, the consecration).

## The Practice

A modern practice of the Tantric tradition requires:

1. **The foundation** — basic meditation, basic mantra repetition, basic visualization
2. **The initiation** — find a **teacher** in a recognized lineage
3. **The personal mantra** — receive the **guru mantra** at initiation
4. **The daily practice** — sit with the mantra, the yantra, the visualization
5. **The retreat** — periodic **intensives** (10 days, 30 days, 100 days) to deepen the practice
6. **The community** — the practice is best learned in a **community** of practitioners

The library's category 14 is the **textual foundation**. The practice requires a teacher and a tradition.
