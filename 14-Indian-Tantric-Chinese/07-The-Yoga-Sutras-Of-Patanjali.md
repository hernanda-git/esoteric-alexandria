# The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali — The Eight Limbs and the Stilling of Mind

## The Text

The **Yoga Sutras** (Sanskrit *Yoga-sutra*) attributed to **Patanjali** is the founding text of **Raja Yoga** (the "royal" or classical yoga) and the single most influential systematic exposition of yogic practice in the Hindu tradition. It is a collection of **196 aphorisms** (*sutras*, "threads") organized into four chapters (*padas*).

The opening sutra states the entire method in one line: *"Yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ"* — **yoga is the stilling of the fluctuations of the mind**. Liberation (*kaivalya*, "isolation" or "aloneness" of pure consciousness) is attained not by belief but by the systematic quieting of mental activity so that the seer rests in its own nature.

## The Four Padas

| Pada | Name | Subject |
|---|---|---|
| **I — Samadhi** | Contemplation | The nature of mind, the goal of absorption, the means to stillness |
| **II — Sadhana** | Practice | The practical disciplines (the eight limbs) and the causes of suffering |
| **III — Vibhuti** | Powers | The extraordinary attainments (*siddhis*) that arise from deep practice |
| **IV — Kaivalya** | Liberation | The final isolation of pure consciousness, freedom from karma |

## The Eight Limbs (Ashtanga)

Chapter II presents the **ashtanga** ("eight-limbed") path — a graduated discipline from external restraint to interior absorption:

1. **Yama** — ethical restraints (non-violence, truth, non-stealing, continence, non-possessiveness)
2. **Niyama** — observances (purity, contentment, austerity, self-study, surrender to the divine)
3. **Asana** — posture (the limb later emphasized in modern postural yoga)
4. **Pranayama** — breath regulation (see [[05-The-Five-Koshas-Real-Source]] for prana)
5. **Pratyahara** — withdrawal of the senses (turning inward)
6. **Dharana** — concentration (fixing the mind on one point)
7. **Dhyana** — meditation (sustained flow of attention)
8. **Samadhi** — absorption (the object and subject merge; the culmination)

The first five are **outer** practice; the last three (*samyama* — the combined Dharana-Dhyana-Samadhi) are **inner**. The limbs are sequential yet mutually reinforcing: concentration is impossible without the steadiness that asana and pranayama build.

## The Kleshas — The Roots of Suffering

Patanjali's psychology identifies five **kleshas** (afflictions) that bind consciousness to the cycle of suffering:

- **Avidya** — spiritual ignorance (the root; the misidentification of the self with the not-self)
- **Asmita** — egoism (the "I-am-ness" that claims the fluctuations as one's own)
- **Raga** — attachment to pleasure
- **Dvesha** — aversion to pain
- **Abhinivesha** — clinging to bodily life

The entire practice exists to burn away the kleshas. As they thin, the gunas (the three qualities of nature) fall back into equilibrium and the light of *purusha* (pure consciousness) stands revealed. This is structurally the same move as the Western alchemical **dissolution of the false self** (see [[04-Alchemy-Transmutation/04-Emerald-Tablet-Of-Hermes-Real-Source]]) and the Hermetic *"as above, so below"* inversion of attention inward.

## The Modern Note

The Yoga Sutras underwrite most modern yoga, yet the modern West largely reduced *asana* to physical fitness and forgot the metaphysics. The text remains the anchor for:

- **Transpersonal psychology** — the stages of absorption parallel the levels of consciousness mapped by Ken Wilber and Stanislav Grof
- **Mindfulness science** — Patanjali's *citta-vrtti-nirodha* is the classical antecedent of the attention-training studied by modern contemplative neuroscience (see [[18-Psychology-Jungian/05-Synchronicity-Acausal-Connecting-Principle]] for the wider frame)
- **Contemplative cross-tradition** — the eight limbs parallel the graded paths of the Sufi *mujahada* and the Christian *purgatio-illuminatio-unio*

The library's category 14 treats the Sutras as the **disciplinarian core** beneath the subtle-body maps of the chakras and the liberating spontaneity of Tantra.
